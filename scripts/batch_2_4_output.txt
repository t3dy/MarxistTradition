BODY: Chartist Associations were local and regional organizations formed across Britain in the late 1830s and 1840s to advance the demands of the People's Charter, which called for universal male suffrage, secret ballots, annual parliaments, equal electoral districts, abolition of property qualifications for MPs, and payment of MPs. The organizational forms pioneered by Chartist Associations influenced subsequent debates about the relationship between democratic demands and socialist revolution.

===ENTRY: terms/communism===
BODY: Communism (Kommunismus), as theorized by Marx and Engels, designates not a utopian blueprint but the real movement that abolishes the present state of things. Marx distinguished communism from earlier socialisms by grounding it in the actual contradictions of capitalist production rather than moral critique. Key debates concern the relationship between communism and the state, the role of planning versus spontaneous coordination, and whether communism requires abundance or a fundamental transformation of needs and social relations.

VALUE-FORM: Communism entails not merely the redistribution of wealth or the socialization of property titles but the abolition of the value-form itself â€” of abstract labor, commodity production, and money as the dominant forms of social mediation â€” thereby liberating human activity from its subsumption under the logic of capital.

===ENTRY: terms/crisis-theory-general===
BODY: Crisis theory in Marxism analyzes the systemic tendency of capitalist economies toward periodic breakdown, encompassing several distinct but interrelated explanations. Marx identified crisis as intrinsic to capitalist production, rooted in the contradictions of the commodity form, the tendency of the rate of profit to fall, and the anarchic character of capitalist accumulation. Major theoretical traditions include: underconsumptionism, which locates crisis in insufficient effective demand resulting from the exploitation of workers; the profit squeeze approach, which emphasizes rising wages or raw material costs compressing profitability; disproportionality theories, which stress imbalances between departments of production; and overaccumulation theories, which focus on the tendency to produce more capital than can be profitably invested. The law of the tendency of the rate of profit to fall, derived from the rising organic composition of capital, has been the most debated mechanism. Contemporary crisis theory addresses financialization, long waves, and the relationship between ecological limits and accumulation.

===ENTRY: terms/dependency-theory===
BODY: Dependency theory is a body of Marxist and neo-Marxist analysis arguing that the underdevelopment of peripheral countries is not a pre-capitalist condition but a structural product of their integration into the capitalist world system on subordinate terms. Developed primarily by Latin American theorists including Andre Gunder Frank, Ruy Mauro Marini, Theotonio dos Santos, and Samir Amin, dependency theory challenged modernization theory's assumption that all countries follow a linear path from tradition to modernity. Frank's thesis of the "development of underdevelopment" argued that metropolitan accumulation and peripheral impoverishment are two sides of the same process. Marini theorized superexploitation of labor in the periphery, where workers are paid below the value of their labor power. Critics, including Robert Brenner and Bill Warren, argued that dependency theory underestimated the development of productive forces in the periphery and substituted circulationist analysis for class-based analysis of production relations. Despite these critiques, dependency theory remains influential in analyses of imperialism, unequal exchange, and global inequality.
CLASSICAL: Dependency theory demonstrates that the accumulation of capital in the imperialist center is structurally linked to the extraction of surplus from the periphery through unequal exchange, superexploitation, and the subordination of peripheral economies to metropolitan interests, making genuine development impossible without breaking from the world capitalist system.
VALUE-FORM: Dependency theory can be deepened through value-form analysis by showing how the world market enforces abstract labor as a global measure while systematically transferring value from the periphery to the center through the equalization of prices on the world market, where less productive labor in peripheral economies exchanges against more productive metropolitan labor to the disadvantage of the former.

===ENTRY: terms/division-of-labour===
BODY: The division of labour (Teilung der Arbeit) is a foundational concept in Marx's analysis of capitalism, examined extensively in *Capital* Volume 1, Chapters 14â€“15. Marx distinguishes between the social division of labor (the distribution of productive activities across society) and the manufacturing or detail division of labor (the fragmentation of a single production process into specialized operations). While Adam Smith celebrated the division of labor as the source of productivity and wealth, Marx demonstrated that under capitalism it serves as a mechanism for increasing relative surplus value, deskilling workers, and subordinating labor to capital. The transition from manufacture to modern industry represents the real subsumption of labor, as the worker becomes an appendage of the machine. Marx also analyzed the gendered division of labor and its role in cheapening labor power. The concept connects to broader themes of alienation, as the division of labor separates workers from the product, the process, their fellow workers, and their species-being.
CLASSICAL: The capitalist division of labor is a means of increasing the productivity of labor and extracting greater surplus value by fragmenting the production process, deskilling workers, and subordinating living labor to the requirements of machinery and the valorization of capital.
VALUE-FORM: The division of labor under capitalism must be understood not merely as a technical organization of production but as a social form through which abstract labor is constituted â€” the fragmentation and interchangeability of concrete labors being the practical basis upon which qualitatively different activities are rendered commensurable as quantities of value-producing labor.
===END===

===ENTRY: terms/eco-marxism===
BODY: Eco-Marxism designates a body of theory that integrates ecological analysis with Marxist critique of capitalism, arguing that environmental destruction is not an accidental byproduct but a structural feature of capitalist accumulation. Key contributions include Marx's own concept of metabolic rift â€” the disruption of the material interchange between humanity and nature caused by capitalist agriculture â€” as recovered and developed by John Bellamy Foster. James O'Connor theorized the "second contradiction of capitalism" between the forces of production and the conditions of production, including natural conditions. Jason W. Moore's "world-ecology" framework rejects the dualism of nature and society, analyzing capitalism as a way of organizing nature. Andreas Malm has historicized fossil capitalism as a specific energy regime tied to the imperatives of capital accumulation. Debates within eco-Marxism concern whether Marx's own framework is adequate to ecological analysis, the relationship between value theory and ecological limits, and the political implications for ecosocialist strategy and degrowth.
CLASSICAL: Eco-Marxism demonstrates that capitalism's drive to expand surplus value extraction systematically undermines the natural conditions of production, creating an ecological crisis that, like the economic crisis, is rooted in the fundamental contradiction between the social character of production and the private appropriation of nature for profit.
VALUE-FORM: The ecological crisis reveals a constitutive blind spot of the value-form: because value measures only abstract labor time and is indifferent to the material qualities of use-values and natural conditions, the logic of valorization systematically externalizes and destroys the ecological basis of social reproduction, exposing the irrationality of a social form that cannot register nature except as a free gift or a cost to be minimized.

===ENTRY: terms/ecological-crisis===
BODY: The ecological crisis, from a Marxist perspective, encompasses the interrelated phenomena of climate change, biodiversity collapse, soil degradation, resource depletion, and pollution that result from capitalist production and accumulation. Marx identified the tendential destruction of natural conditions in his analysis of capitalist agriculture and the metabolic rift. Contemporary Marxist ecology argues that the ecological crisis is not a problem of individual consumption or insufficient regulation but is structurally generated by capital's imperative to grow without limit in a finite biosphere. Andreas Malm's concept of fossil capital demonstrates how the adoption of fossil fuels was driven not by thermodynamic efficiency but by the requirements of labor discipline and surplus value extraction. The ecological crisis intensifies existing inequalities, as its effects fall disproportionately on the Global South and on racialized and working-class communities. Key debates concern the relationship between ecological limits and value theory, the viability of green capitalism, and the necessity of ecosocialist planning.
CLASSICAL: The ecological crisis is a manifestation of capitalism's fundamental contradiction between unlimited accumulation and the finite natural conditions of production, requiring the overthrow of capitalist property relations and the establishment of rational, planned social control over the metabolism between humanity and nature.
VALUE-FORM: The ecological crisis exposes the destructive irrationality of the value-form as a mode of social mediation: because value is constituted solely by abstract labor time and is categorically indifferent to material conditions and natural limits, capitalist production necessarily treats the biosphere as an externality, driving ecological destruction as a systemic consequence of the form of social wealth itself.
===END===

===ENTRY: terms/economic-congress===
BODY: Economic Congresses were assemblies convened by bourgeois economists and political figures in nineteenth-century Europe to discuss questions of trade policy, industrial regulation, currency, taxation, and economic reform. Marx engaged critically with these gatherings as sites where the ideological presuppositions of bourgeois political economy were publicly articulated and contested. These congresses often promoted laissez-faire economics, free trade, and the harmonization of commercial law, presenting the interests of the capitalist class as universal economic rationality. Marx's critical analysis of such events, particularly in his journalism for the *New York Tribune* and the *Neue Rheinische Zeitung*, demonstrated his method of revealing the class content of ostensibly neutral economic discourse. The congresses are significant for understanding the institutional self-organization of the bourgeoisie as a class and the development of economic policy as a terrain of ideological struggle between competing class interests and fractions of capital.
CLASSICAL: The Economic Congresses functioned as organs of bourgeois class self-organization, where the general interests of capital were formulated and propagated as universal economic science, and where Marx's critique exposed the particular class interests concealed behind the rhetoric of free markets and economic harmony.
VALUE-FORM: The Economic Congresses articulated the categories of bourgeois political economy â€” price, profit, interest, rent â€” as natural and eternal, performing the ideological work of naturalizing historically specific value-form categories and obscuring the social relations of exploitation that constitute them.
===END===

===ENTRY: terms/economic-determinism-debate===
BODY: The economic determinism debate concerns whether Marxism reduces all social, political, cultural, and ideological phenomena to direct expressions of the economic base. Critics, both internal and external, have charged that the base-superstructure metaphor in Marx's 1859 Preface to *A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy* implies a mechanistic, one-directional causality from economy to all other social spheres. Engels himself, in late letters, acknowledged the criticism and insisted that the economic element is determinant only "in the last instance," leaving room for the relative autonomy and reciprocal influence of politics, law, ideology, and culture. Subsequent Marxists developed diverse responses: Gramsci's theory of hegemony, Althusser's concept of overdetermination and structural causality, E.P. Thompson's emphasis on experience and agency, and the Analytical Marxists' attempts at methodological rigor. The debate is foundational because it shapes how Marxists understand the relationship between structural constraint and political possibility, between objective conditions and subjective intervention.
CLASSICAL: While the economic base â€” the relations and forces of production â€” ultimately determines the character of social life, the superstructure of politics, law, and ideology possesses relative autonomy and can react back upon the base, making the relationship between economy and society dialectical rather than mechanically deterministic.
VALUE-FORM: The value-form approach dissolves the base-superstructure dichotomy by showing that the economic is not a separate sphere determining others from outside but that the value-form constitutes a total mode of social mediation in which the categories of political economy â€” commodity, money, capital â€” are simultaneously economic, juridical, political, and ideological forms of a single social reality.
