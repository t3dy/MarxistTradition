The International Working Men's Association, known as the First International, was founded in London in 1864 with Marx as its leading intellectual figure. It brought together British trade unionists, French Proudhonists, Italian Mazzinians, and German socialists in the first sustained attempt at international working-class organisation.

Marx drafted the Inaugural Address and General Rules, establishing the principle of working-class self-emancipation. The International supported strikes, campaigned for the eight-hour day, and debated questions of strategy and programme. Internal conflicts between Marx and Bakunin over the role of the state and organisational authority led to a split at the 1872 Hague Congress. The International was formally dissolved in 1876, but its legacy of proletarian internationalism shaped all subsequent socialist and communist movements.
