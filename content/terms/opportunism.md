Opportunism in Marxist theory designates political tendencies within the workers' movement that sacrifice revolutionary principles for immediate advantages, electoral gains, or class compromise. Lenin developed the concept extensively, linking right opportunism to the material basis of the labour aristocracy — a stratum of workers benefiting from imperialist super-profits and therefore inclined toward reformism.

Right opportunism adapts to bourgeois politics through parliamentary cretinism, class collaboration, and abandonment of revolutionary goals. Left opportunism (or ultra-leftism) rejects necessary tactical flexibility, united fronts, and transitional demands in favour of abstract revolutionary posturing. Lenin's *What Is To Be Done?* and *Left-Wing Communism* addressed both deviations. The concept has been criticised for functioning as a polemical weapon to silence dissent within parties, particularly under Stalinism where accusations of opportunism served bureaucratic factional interests rather than genuine political clarification.
