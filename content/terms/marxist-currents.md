Marxist currents encompass the diverse theoretical and political tendencies that have developed within the Marxist tradition since Marx's death. The revisionism debate, initiated by Bernstein's argument that capitalism could be reformed gradually, produced the foundational split between revolutionary and reformist socialism that Luxemburg and others contested.

Council communism (Pannekoek, Gorter) and left communism (Bordiga) rejected both parliamentary reformism and Leninist party organisation in favour of workers' councils as the form of proletarian power. The socialism-in-one-country debate between Stalin and Trotsky shaped twentieth-century communist politics. Market socialism debated whether planning could incorporate price mechanisms. Trotsky's theory of permanent revolution and uneven and combined development challenged stagist conceptions of historical progress. Each current represents a distinct answer to the fundamental questions of revolutionary strategy: the role of the party, the state, reform versus revolution, and the transition to socialism.
