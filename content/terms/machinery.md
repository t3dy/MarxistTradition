Machinery occupies a central place in Marx's analysis of capitalism in *Capital* Volume 1, Chapter 15. Marx distinguished the tool, which extends the worker's hand, from the machine, which replaces the worker as the active agent of production. Under capitalism, machinery serves not to lighten labour but to increase relative surplus value by cheapening the commodities that constitute the value of labour-power.

Marx analysed how machinery transforms the labour process: it enables the employment of women and children, extends the working day through continuous operation, and intensifies labour by increasing the pace of production. The 'Fragment on Machines' in the *Grundrisse* anticipates how the development of fixed capital in automated production might undermine the labour theory of value itself, as science and social knowledge rather than direct labour become the main productive force — a passage central to contemporary debates about cognitive capitalism and post-work politics.
