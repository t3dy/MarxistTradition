The Prague Spring of 1968 was a period of political liberalisation in Czechoslovakia under Alexander Dubček's leadership, seeking 'socialism with a human face.' The reform programme included freedom of the press, political pluralism, decentralisation of economic planning, and rehabilitation of victims of Stalinist repression.

The Soviet Union, fearing the reforms would undermine the Eastern Bloc's political cohesion, invaded Czechoslovakia on 20-21 August 1968 with Warsaw Pact forces, crushing the reform movement. The invasion was justified by the Brezhnev Doctrine, asserting the Soviet right to intervene in any socialist state threatened by counter-revolution. The suppression radicalised sections of the Western left against Stalinism while disillusiioning many Communist Party members. The Prague Spring raised enduring questions about whether bureaucratic socialism could reform itself, the relationship between economic decentralisation and political democracy, and the possibility of democratic socialism distinct from both Stalinist command economies and Western capitalism.
