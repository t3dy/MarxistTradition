Marxist analysis of trade and protectionism examines tariff policy and commercial regulation as expressions of class interests and inter-capitalist competition rather than neutral instruments of economic efficiency. Marx addressed free trade in his 1848 speech to the Brussels Democratic Association, arguing that while free trade accelerates capitalist development and sharpens class antagonisms, this does not make it progressive in any absolute sense. Protectionism serves nascent bourgeoisies seeking to develop domestic industry against more advanced competitors, as analyzed by Friedrich List, whom Marx criticized for conflating national industrial development with universal progress. Engels similarly analyzed tariffs as weapons in inter-imperialist rivalry. Contemporary Marxist approaches examine trade agreements as mechanisms for enforcing the global division of labor, examining how trade liberalization benefits metropolitan capital at the expense of peripheral economies, and how trade policy intersects with imperialism and unequal exchange.
