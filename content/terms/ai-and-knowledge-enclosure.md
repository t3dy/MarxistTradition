AI and knowledge enclosure describes the process by which collectively produced human knowledge — scientific publications, cultural works, internet content, natural language itself — is appropriated as training data for proprietary AI systems, effectively privatising the cognitive commons. Large language models trained on the entirety of the publicly accessible internet represent perhaps the largest single act of enclosure in history, transforming the accumulated linguistic production of humanity into corporate assets owned by a handful of technology firms. The parallel with the historical enclosure of common lands is precise: in both cases, collectively produced resources are appropriated as private capital, and those who produced them must subsequently purchase access to what they formerly held in common.

The struggle between open-source and proprietary AI models expresses this contradiction in practical terms. Open models — released with permissive licenses and accessible weights — represent an attempt to maintain AI as a knowledge commons, while proprietary models enclose the same capabilities behind subscription fees and API access. The related concepts of 'AI and intellectual property regimes,' 'the privatisation of cognition,' and 'knowledge monopolies' describe different aspects of the same process: the systematic transformation of collective human cognitive capacity into privately owned capital. The politics of technological design — who builds AI systems, for what purposes, and under what governance — determines whether AI extends the commons of human knowledge or completes its enclosure as corporate property.
