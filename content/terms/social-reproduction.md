Social reproduction refers to the ensemble of activities and relationships through which the conditions of capitalist production are renewed on a daily and generational basis — the bearing and raising of children, the physical and emotional maintenance of workers, the transmission of skills and social norms, and the reproduction of class relations. Feminist Marxists have argued that classical Marxist theory systematically neglected these activities, which are disproportionately performed by women, unpaid, and rendered invisible by an analytical framework focused exclusively on commodity production and waged labor. Social reproduction theory, developed from the domestic labor debates of the 1970s through contemporary theorists such as Lise Vogel, Tithi Bhattacharya, and Cinzia Arruzza, argues that capitalism depends on but does not pay for the reproductive labor that produces and maintains the commodity labor-power. This structural dependence on unwaged reproductive work, combined with capital's tendency to cut costs by offloading reproduction onto households and communities, produces a permanent crisis of social reproduction — an intensifying contradiction between capital's need for cheap, compliant workers and its systematic undermining of the conditions required to produce and sustain them.
