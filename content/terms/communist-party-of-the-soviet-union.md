The Communist Party of the Soviet Union (CPSU) was the ruling party of the USSR from the 1917 revolution until the dissolution of the Soviet state in 1991. Originating as the Bolshevik faction of the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party, it became the organizational model for communist parties worldwide through the Communist International. The CPSU's history concentrates the central debates of twentieth-century Marxism: the vanguard party and its relationship to the working class, the possibility of socialism in one country, the dynamics of bureaucratic degeneration, the nature of Soviet society (workers' state, degenerated workers' state, or state capitalism), and the relationship between planning and democracy. Under Stalin, the party apparatus became an instrument of bureaucratic rule, suppressing internal democracy and subordinating international communist movements to Soviet state interests. The CPSU's collapse prompted fundamental reassessments of the Leninist party form and the viability of centrally planned economies.
