The reserve army of labor (*industrielle Reservearmee*) designates the mass of unemployed and semi-employed workers that capitalist accumulation necessarily produces and maintains as a condition of its own reproduction. Marx argues in Chapter 25 of *Capital, Volume I* that as accumulation proceeds and the organic composition of capital rises, fewer workers are required relative to the total capital employed, creating a relative surplus population that exists in three forms: the floating reserve (workers periodically expelled from and reabsorbed into employment), the latent reserve (agricultural and other workers available for absorption into industrial production), and the stagnant reserve (workers trapped in irregular, precarious, and poorly paid employment). The existence of this reserve army performs a crucial disciplinary function: the threat of unemployment compels employed workers to accept longer hours, lower wages, and worse conditions, while the availability of surplus labor ensures that capital can rapidly expand production when profitable opportunities arise. The concept anticipates contemporary analyses of precarious labor, the gig economy, and structural unemployment.
