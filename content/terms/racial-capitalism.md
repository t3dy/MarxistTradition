Racial capitalism, a concept developed most influentially by Cedric Robinson, holds that capitalism has always been racially structured, relying on racial hierarchy and differentiation as integral mechanisms of accumulation rather than incidental features. Robinson argued that European feudalism was already organized through racial and ethnic hierarchies, and that capitalism extended these logics globally through slavery, colonialism, and racialized labor markets. The concept challenges orthodox Marxist frameworks that treat race as secondary to class, insisting that racial oppression is constitutive of capitalist exploitation. Contemporary applications address migration regimes that create tiered labor forces, the growth of precarious and informal economies disproportionately affecting racialized populations, and the structural production of surplus populations along racial lines. Debates concern whether racial capitalism names a specific historical formation or an intrinsic feature of all capitalism.
