Socialist planning refers to the conscious, collective coordination of economic activity as an alternative to the anarchic regulation of production by market competition. Marx and Engels argued that the socialization of production under capitalism already creates the material preconditions for planned economy, since large-scale industry requires coordination that the market accomplishes only through wasteful crises. The Soviet model of central planning, formalized through Gosplan and five-year plans, became the dominant historical realization but attracted criticism for bureaucratic rigidity, information problems, and the absence of democratic participation. Alternative models include participatory planning as proposed by Pat Devine and Michael Albert, decentralized planning using input-output models, and market socialism. The socialist calculation debate between Mises, Hayek, Lange, and others remains a reference point. Contemporary discussions address whether digital technology could resolve information problems that plagued earlier planning attempts.
