Surveillance capitalism, a concept developed by Shoshana Zuboff, describes a new logic of accumulation in which human experience is claimed as free raw material for the extraction of behavioral data, which is then processed into prediction products and sold on behavioral futures markets. While Zuboff's analysis focuses on the novelty of this extractive logic, Marxist theorists have situated it within longer histories of capitalist expropriation. Nick Couldry and Ulises Mejias's concept of 'data colonialism' argues that the appropriation of human life through data extraction reproduces the structure of historical colonialism, turning human relations into raw material for a new phase of capital accumulation. The related concept of 'data enclosure' draws an explicit parallel with the enclosure of common lands, framing the privatisation of behavioral data as a digital equivalent of primitive accumulation.

The infrastructure of surveillance capitalism — algorithmic management systems, smart city technologies, biometric tracking, predictive analytics — extends the monitoring and control of labour that Marx described in the factory system to encompass the entirety of social life. Data extraction operates not only in the workplace but through every digital interaction, turning consumption, communication, movement, and even emotion into inputs for the valorisation process. The result is a form of capital accumulation that does not merely exploit labor in the point of production but colonises the full range of human experience as a resource for extraction, blurring the boundary between production and reproduction that earlier phases of capitalism maintained.
