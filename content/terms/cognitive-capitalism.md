Cognitive capitalism is a concept developed primarily by Italian and French autonomist theorists — including Carlo Vercellone, Yann Moulier Boutang, and Andrea Fumagalli — to describe a historical phase of capitalism in which the primary source of value creation shifts from physical labor to cognitive, creative, and communicative activity. The concept draws on Marx's fragment on machines in the Grundrisse, where he anticipated that the development of the 'general intellect' — the accumulated knowledge of society embedded in technology and social organisation — would make direct labor time an increasingly inadequate measure and source of wealth. Cognitive capitalism theorists argue that this transition is now underway, but that capital responds to it not by liberating cognitive activity but by enclosing it through intellectual property regimes, platform monopolies, and the privatisation of knowledge.

The related concept of 'informational capitalism' describes the broader transformation in which information becomes both a key productive input and a primary commodity. The 'knowledge commons' — the collectively produced stock of scientific knowledge, cultural works, open-source software, and shared information — represents what autonomists identify as the material basis for a post-capitalist mode of production, already existing within and against capitalist enclosure. The tension between the inherently social and shareable character of knowledge and capital's need to impose artificial scarcity through intellectual property constitutes, in this analysis, one of the fundamental contradictions of contemporary capitalism.
