The Vietnamese Revolution encompasses the decades-long struggle from the anti-colonial resistance against France through the defeat of American intervention in 1975, constituting one of the most significant revolutionary processes of the twentieth century. Beginning with the Viet Minh's anti-Japanese and anti-French resistance, the revolution achieved the 1954 partition of Vietnam and culminated in the reunification of the country under communist leadership. The revolution's significance for Marxist theory lies in its demonstration that revolutionary movements in colonized countries could defeat major imperialist powers through protracted people's war, combining military strategy with mass political mobilization. The Tet Offensive of 1968 proved decisive in undermining American public support for the war. The Vietnamese Revolution influenced anti-colonial movements worldwide and stimulated debates about the relationship between national liberation and socialism, the role of the peasantry in revolutionary struggle, and the limits of military power against politically mobilized populations.
