Fictitious capital refers to financial assets such as stocks, bonds, government debt, and derivatives whose value derives from claims on future surplus-value rather than from any direct involvement in the production process. Marx distinguished fictitious capital from the real capital invested in means of production and labor-power, showing that financial claims can circulate, appreciate, and accumulate independently of the actual valorization of productive capital. The price of fictitious capital is determined by capitalizing expected future income streams at the prevailing rate of interest, creating paper values that bear no necessary relationship to the underlying productive assets. The expansion of fictitious capital intensifies the speculative and crisis-prone character of capitalism, as the growing gap between paper claims and actual value production periodically erupts in financial collapses — moments when the fictitious values are violently brought back into conformity with the real capacity of capital to extract surplus-value from living labor. The concept has gained enormous relevance in the era of financialization, as the volume of financial claims on future value has expanded far beyond any historical precedent.
