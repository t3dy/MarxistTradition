AI and historical materialism addresses the question of how artificial intelligence relates to the fundamental categories of Marxist social theory — forces and relations of production, base and superstructure, determination and contradiction. The most basic question is whether AI represents a development of the productive forces that has outgrown the capitalist relations of production, creating a revolutionary situation in which new social relations must emerge to accommodate the new technology, or whether capital has demonstrated sufficient adaptability to incorporate AI within existing relations of exploitation. The debate over technological determinism — whether technology drives social change or social relations shape technology — takes on renewed urgency when the technology in question can potentially transform every dimension of human productive activity.

The dialectical analysis of technology that Marx employed — recognising that machinery is simultaneously a productive force and a weapon of class domination — is essential for understanding AI. AI is not neutral; it embodies the social relations under which it was produced, encoding the priorities, biases, and power structures of the society that created it. Yet it also develops capacities that exceed the uses to which capital puts them, generating possibilities for collective coordination, knowledge sharing, and the reduction of necessary labor time that point beyond capitalist social relations. The question of the future of class structure under AI-driven capitalism — whether AI produces a new class division between those who own and control AI systems and those subject to them, or whether it creates the material conditions for the abolition of class distinctions altogether — remains the defining political question of the technological transformation now underway.
