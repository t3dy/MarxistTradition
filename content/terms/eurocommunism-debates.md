Eurocommunism emerged in the 1970s as a strategic orientation adopted by the Italian, French, and Spanish Communist Parties, emphasizing parliamentary democracy, pluralism, and independence from Soviet direction. Drawing on Gramsci's concept of hegemony and Togliatti's polycentrism, Eurocommunists argued that socialist transformation in advanced capitalist societies required winning consent through democratic institutions rather than insurrectionary seizure of power.

Critics from the orthodox left charged that Eurocommunism represented capitulation to bourgeois democracy and abandoned the dictatorship of the proletariat. Poulantzas engaged sympathetically but critically, warning that a purely statist strategy risked absorption. The debate raised fundamental questions about socialism and democracy, the nature of the capitalist state, and whether the working class could achieve power through existing political forms without revolutionary rupture.
