Exchange-value is the quantitative proportion in which use-values of one kind are exchanged for use-values of another kind, appearing initially as a contingent, fluctuating relation between commodities. Marx argues that the regularity of exchange ratios presupposes a common substance — abstract human labor — that makes qualitatively different products commensurable. Exchange-value is thus the necessary form of appearance of value: the socially necessary abstract labor objectified in the commodity cannot manifest itself directly but must express itself through the relation of one commodity to another, ultimately through the money-form. Exchange-value is not an intrinsic natural property of any object but a social relation that expresses itself through the act of exchange, belonging to commodities only insofar as they are products of labor destined for exchange in a society based on the social division of labor. The distinction between exchange-value and value is crucial for the value-form tradition, which emphasizes that exchange-value is a form-determination — an answer to the question of *how* value appears — rather than a simple quantitative measure.
