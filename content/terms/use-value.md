Use-value (*Gebrauchswert*) designates the qualitative, material utility of a thing — its capacity to satisfy some human want or need, whether physical, cultural, or imaginative. A commodity's use-value is determined by its physical, chemical, or functional properties: a coat is useful for warmth, wheat for nourishment, a book for reading. Use-value is the material content of wealth regardless of its social form, and a necessary condition for any object to function as a commodity — an object without use-value for anyone cannot be sold. However, Marx insists that use-value as such is not the subject matter of political economy, which concerns itself with social form rather than material content. The critical tension arises from the subordination of use-value to exchange-value under capitalism: commodities are produced not primarily to satisfy human needs but to be sold for profit, meaning use-value serves as the mere bearer or material vehicle of exchange-value. This contradiction between the material content and social form of the commodity — between the concrete, qualitative world of human needs and the abstract, quantitative logic of value — is, for Marx, the elementary contradiction from which all further contradictions of the capitalist mode of production develop.
