Open Marxism is a theoretical current that emerged in the 1990s, primarily associated with John Holloway, Werner Bonefeld, and Richard Gunn. It opposes what it sees as the closed, deterministic character of both structuralist Marxism and orthodox historical materialism, insisting that social forms are constituted through living human practice and are therefore always open to transformation through struggle.

Drawing on the Frankfurt School, Italian autonomism, and the state derivation debate, Open Marxism analyses the capitalist state, money, and law as fetishised forms of class relations rather than autonomous structures. Holloway's *Change the World Without Taking Power* (2002) argued for revolution through the refusal of alienated social relations rather than the seizure of state power. Critics from both Leninist and structuralist positions have challenged Open Marxism's rejection of party organisation and its allegedly idealist understanding of social transformation.
