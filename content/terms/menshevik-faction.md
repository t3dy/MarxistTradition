The Menshevik faction emerged from the 1903 split in the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party, opposing Lenin's conception of a disciplined vanguard party of professional revolutionaries. Led by Martov, Plekhanov, and later Tsereteli and Dan, the Mensheviks advocated a broad, inclusive party modelled on Western European social democracy.

The Mensheviks held that Russia must first complete its bourgeois-democratic revolution before conditions would ripen for socialist transformation — a stagist theory that led them to support the Provisional Government in 1917 and oppose the October Revolution as premature. They participated in the soviets and the Constituent Assembly but were progressively marginalised after October. The Menshevik experience crystallised fundamental debates about the relationship between democratic and socialist revolution, the role of the bourgeoisie in anti-feudal struggle, and whether backward countries could bypass capitalist development.
