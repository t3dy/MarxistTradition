The lumpenproletariat designates the stratum of declassed, marginal social elements — beggars, vagabonds, petty criminals, discharged soldiers, and the chronically unemployed — whom Marx described as incapable of developing independent class consciousness and therefore available for hire as the shock troops of reaction. In *The Eighteenth Brumaire*, Marx shows how Bonaparte recruited his political base from this social residuum, the Society of the Tenth of December, composed of men with no stable relationship to the means of production or the wage form. Marx's dismissal of the lumpenproletariat has been challenged by later theorists, particularly Fanon, who argued in *The Wretched of the Earth* that in the colonial context, the lumpenproletariat — the urban dispossessed excluded from the formal colonial economy — constitutes the most revolutionary class, precisely because it has no stake in the existing order. The Black Panther Party similarly revalued the lumpenproletariat as a potentially revolutionary force in conditions of racialized exclusion from formal employment.
