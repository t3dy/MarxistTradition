The German Revolution of 1918-1919 overthrew the Kaiser and established the Weimar Republic amid naval mutinies, workers' and soldiers' councils, and mass strikes. The revolution posed the question of workers' power through the council system, but the Social Democratic leadership under Ebert and Noske allied with the military and Freikorps to suppress the radical left.

The Spartacist uprising in January 1919, led by Rosa Luxemburg and Karl Liebknecht, was crushed and both leaders murdered. The revolution's defeat foreclosed socialist transformation and contributed to conditions enabling fascism's rise. The experience profoundly shaped Marxist thinking on party, councils, and revolutionary strategy.
