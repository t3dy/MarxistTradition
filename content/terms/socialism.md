Socialism, in Marxist theory, designates the transitional stage between capitalism and communism, characterized by collective ownership of the means of production, production for social need rather than profit, and the principle of distribution according to labor performed. Marx outlined these features most explicitly in the *Critique of the Gotha Programme*, distinguishing a lower phase of communist society (later called socialism) retaining certain bourgeois features, including differential remuneration, from a higher phase in which distribution proceeds according to need. The concept has been contested by the divergence between its theoretical formulation and its historical realizations in the Soviet Union, China, and elsewhere. Debates concern the relationship between socialism and democracy, the role of markets and planning, whether socialism requires workers' self-management or centralized direction, and whether actually existing socialist states achieved the transition Marx envisioned.
