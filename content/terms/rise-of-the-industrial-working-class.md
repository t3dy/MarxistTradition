The rise of the industrial working class refers to the historical process by which capitalist industrialization created a new social class of propertyless wage laborers concentrated in factories and urban centers from the late eighteenth century onward. Marx and Engels analyzed this process as the outcome of primitive accumulation, which dispossessed peasants and artisans, and the development of machine production, which gathered workers into large-scale enterprises. The concentration of workers in factories created the material conditions for collective organization and class consciousness. Engels's *Condition of the Working Class in England* documented the brutal conditions accompanying this formation. The emergence of Chartism, early trade unions, and workers' insurrections marked the political awakening of this class. Debates concern the unevenness of proletarianization across regions, the role of gender and race in working-class formation, and how artisanal traditions shaped early labor movements.
