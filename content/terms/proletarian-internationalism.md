Proletarian internationalism is the principle that working-class interests transcend national boundaries, demanding solidarity and coordination among workers of all countries against the global capitalist system. Rooted in Marx and Engels's declaration that workers have no country, the concept became organizationally concrete through the First, Second, and Third Internationals. It opposes nationalism as a bourgeois ideology that divides the working class, while recognizing the right of oppressed nations to self-determination. Key debates concern the tension between internationalism and national liberation movements, the failure of Second International parties to oppose World War I, and whether socialist states genuinely practiced internationalism or subordinated it to national interests. The concept remains central to anti-imperialist politics and debates over globalization and cross-border labor organizing.
