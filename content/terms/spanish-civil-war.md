The Spanish Civil War (1936-1939) was a pivotal conflict in which a coalition of republican, socialist, communist, and anarchist forces fought against Francisco Franco's fascist-backed military uprising. For Marxists, the war posed fundamental strategic questions about popular fronts, workers' revolution, and anti-fascist struggle. Anarchists and the POUM pursued social revolution alongside the military conflict, collectivizing factories and land. The Spanish Communist Party, following Comintern directives, prioritized military discipline and alliance with bourgeois republicans, suppressing revolutionary experiments. Trotsky and his followers criticized the popular front strategy as subordinating working-class independence to bourgeois allies, arguing this ultimately undermined the republic's capacity to resist fascism. The defeat of the republic, facilitated by non-intervention from Western democracies and Soviet political manipulation, became a formative trauma for the international left and a lasting reference point in debates over revolutionary versus gradualist strategies.
