Labor-power (*Arbeitskraft*) is the capacity or potential to labor that the worker sells to the capitalist as a commodity on the market. It is distinct from labor itself, which is the actual exercise of that capacity in the production process — a distinction Marx regarded as his most important scientific contribution and the key to solving the riddle of surplus-value. The value of labor-power is determined, like that of any other commodity, by the socially necessary labor time required to produce the means of subsistence needed to reproduce the worker and their family — including food, housing, clothing, education, and the raising of the next generation of workers. The crucial asymmetry is that the use-value of labor-power to the capitalist — its capacity to produce value through living labor — exceeds its exchange-value as determined by reproduction costs. This difference between the value labor-power creates when consumed in production and the value of labor-power itself is the source of surplus-value, making the distinction between labor and labor-power the analytical foundation of Marx's theory of exploitation.
