The *Rheinische Zeitung* was a liberal democratic newspaper published in Cologne from 1842 to 1843, which Marx edited from October 1842 until its suppression by Prussian censorship in March 1843. The paper was founded by liberal Rhenish industrialists to oppose Prussian absolutism and advocate constitutional reform. Under Marx's editorship, it became increasingly radical, publishing articles on press freedom, the theft of wood from common lands, and the condition of Moselle wine-growers that brought Marx into direct confrontation with material economic questions. This experience proved intellectually decisive: Marx later described it as prompting his turn from pure philosophy to the study of political economy. The *Rheinische Zeitung* thus marks a transitional moment between Marx's Young Hegelian philosophical beginnings and his subsequent development of historical materialism, demonstrating how practical journalistic engagement with social questions catalyzed his theoretical transformation.
