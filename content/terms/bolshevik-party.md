The Bolshevik Party, formed as a faction of the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party in 1903 and constituted as an independent party by 1912, was the organizational vehicle of the October Revolution of 1917. Under Lenin's leadership, the Bolsheviks developed the theory and practice of the vanguard party: a disciplined organization of professional revolutionaries operating under democratic centralism. The party's seizure of power and subsequent construction of a workers' state became the central reference point for twentieth-century revolutionary politics. Debates over the Bolshevik model divide Marxists sharply: Leninists defend the vanguard party as essential to overcoming trade-union consciousness, while council communists and libertarian Marxists argue that the Bolshevik form prefigured Stalinist bureaucratization. The relationship between party, class, and soviets in the revolutionary period remains one of the most contested questions in Marxist political theory.
