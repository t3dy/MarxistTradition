The relationship between technology and capitalism is a central concern of Marxist political economy, rooted in Marx's analysis of machinery as a means of increasing relative surplus value by reducing the socially necessary labor time embodied in commodities. Marx showed that technological innovation under capitalism is driven not by the pursuit of human welfare but by competitive pressures to cheapen production and discipline labor. The tendency for mechanization to raise the organic composition of capital figures in the theory of the falling rate of profit. Contemporary debates address digital capitalism and platform economies, where technology enables new forms of rent extraction, surveillance, and labor control while potentially reducing the role of living labor in production. Theorists like Nick Dyer-Witheford and Shoshana Zuboff have examined how information technology transforms accumulation strategies, while autonomists have explored whether digital commons and networked cooperation might prefigure post-capitalist social relations.
