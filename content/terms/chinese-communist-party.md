The Chinese Communist Party (CCP), founded in 1921, became the ruling party of the People's Republic of China following the 1949 revolution. Its trajectory has been central to Marxist debates on peasant revolution, the role of the party, socialist construction in agrarian societies, and the nature of post-revolutionary states. Under Mao Zedong, the CCP adapted Marxism-Leninism to Chinese conditions, theorizing the role of the peasantry as a revolutionary subject and developing concepts such as New Democracy, the mass line, and protracted people's war. The party's post-1978 turn toward market reforms under Deng Xiaoping generated intense debate over whether China represents a form of state capitalism, market socialism, or a sui generis formation. The CCP's history raises fundamental questions about bureaucratic degeneration, the relationship between party and class, and the possibilities and limits of socialist transformation in the periphery of the world system.
