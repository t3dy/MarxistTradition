The revolutionary party is the organizational form through which the most politically advanced sections of the working class provide leadership and theoretical coherence to the broader class struggle. Lenin's conception, developed in *What Is to Be Done?* and elaborated through Bolshevik practice, emphasized a disciplined vanguard party of professional revolutionaries capable of bringing socialist consciousness to the working class from without. This model was institutionalized through the Communist International's organizational principles, including democratic centralism. Debates over the party form have been among the most contentious in Marxist history, centering on the relationship between party and class, the dangers of bureaucratic substitutionism, the applicability of the Leninist model outside Russian conditions, and whether spontaneous mass movements can develop revolutionary consciousness without organized leadership. Council communists and autonomists have rejected the party form entirely.
