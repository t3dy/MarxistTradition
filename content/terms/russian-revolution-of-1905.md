The Russian Revolution of 1905 was a mass upheaval triggered by the Bloody Sunday massacre, encompassing workers' strikes, peasant rebellions, military mutinies, and the spontaneous creation of soviets as organs of working-class self-governance. The Petersburg Soviet, in which Trotsky played a leading role, demonstrated the capacity of workers to develop their own political institutions independent of existing parties and the tsarist state. Though ultimately defeated, the revolution provided decisive lessons for all tendencies in Russian and international socialism. Lenin revised his organizational theories in light of mass spontaneity, while Trotsky developed his theory of permanent revolution from the experience. The revolution exposed the inability of the Russian bourgeoisie to lead democratic transformation, suggesting that the working class would have to accomplish bourgeois-democratic tasks while simultaneously advancing toward socialism. Rosa Luxemburg drew conclusions about the mass strike as a revolutionary weapon.
