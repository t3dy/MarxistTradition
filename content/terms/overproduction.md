term: Overproduction

tags: [political economy]
traditions:
  classical: "Overproduction crises demonstrate capitalism's fundamental irrationality: the system periodically produces more commodities than can be sold at a profit, destroying wealth and immiserating workers while productive capacity stands idle."
  value-form: "Overproduction reveals the contradiction between use-value and value at the systemic level: commodities are overproduced not relative to human need but relative to the requirements of valorisation, exposing the absurdity of production governed by abstract value rather than concrete use."
---

Overproduction is the phenomenon in which more commodities are produced than can be sold at prices sufficient to realise the surplus value contained in them. Marx argued against Say's Law — which held that supply creates its own demand — demonstrating that the separation of purchase and sale in commodity exchange creates the formal possibility of crisis, while the dynamics of capitalist accumulation make crises inevitable.

Overproduction is not absolute but relative: goods go unsold not because human needs are satisfied but because effective demand, limited by the wage relation, cannot absorb the output generated by the drive to accumulate. Marx analysed overproduction in relation to the tendency of the rate of profit to fall, the disproportionality between departments of production, and the underconsumption of the working class. Debates continue over whether overproduction is the proximate cause of crisis or merely a symptom of deeper contradictions in the valorisation process.
