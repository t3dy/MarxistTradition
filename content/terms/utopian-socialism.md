Utopian socialism refers to the early nineteenth-century socialist movements associated with Charles Fourier, Henri de Saint-Simon, and Robert Owen, who developed elaborate visions of ideal societies without grounding them in the analysis of existing class struggles and economic contradictions. Engels's *Socialism: Utopian and Scientific* provided the canonical Marxist assessment, crediting the utopians with brilliant critiques of capitalist society and imaginative proposals for cooperative social organization while criticizing their reliance on moral persuasion, philanthropic patronage, and model communities rather than working-class political action. Marx and Engels argued that utopian socialism arose before the proletariat had developed as an independent political force and therefore could not identify the real agent of social transformation. Despite the critique, utopian elements persist in Marxist thought through debates over prefigurative politics and the relationship between imagining alternatives and organizing for systemic change.
