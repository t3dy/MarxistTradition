Chartist Associations were local and regional organizations formed across Britain in the late 1830s and 1840s to advance the demands of the People's Charter, which called for universal male suffrage, secret ballots, annual parliaments, equal electoral districts, abolition of property qualifications for MPs, and payment of MPs. These associations represented one of the first mass working-class political movements in history and served as a critical reference point for Marx and Engels in theorizing proletarian self-organization. The associations varied in political character, ranging from moderate moral-force persuasion to militant physical-force advocacy. Marx regarded Chartism as the first truly political movement of the working class, distinguishing it from earlier forms of machine-breaking or trade unionism. The organizational forms pioneered by Chartist Associations influenced subsequent debates about the relationship between democratic demands and socialist revolution.
