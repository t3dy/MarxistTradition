Crisis theory in Marxism analyzes the systemic tendency of capitalist economies toward periodic breakdown, encompassing several distinct but interrelated explanations. Marx identified crisis as intrinsic to capitalist production, rooted in the contradictions of the commodity form, the tendency of the rate of profit to fall, and the anarchic character of capitalist accumulation. Major theoretical traditions include: underconsumptionism, which locates crisis in insufficient effective demand resulting from the exploitation of workers; the profit squeeze approach, which emphasizes rising wages or raw material costs compressing profitability; disproportionality theories, which stress imbalances between departments of production; and overaccumulation theories, which focus on the tendency to produce more capital than can be profitably invested. The law of the tendency of the rate of profit to fall, derived from the rising organic composition of capital, has been the most debated mechanism. Contemporary crisis theory addresses financialization, long waves, and the relationship between ecological limits and accumulation.
