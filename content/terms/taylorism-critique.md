The Marxist critique of Taylorism, or scientific management, analyzes Frederick Taylor's methods of labor discipline as a systematic strategy for extracting maximum surplus value by separating the conception and execution of work. Harry Braverman's *Labor and Monopoly Capital* provided the most influential Marxist account, arguing that Taylorism degrades labor by monopolizing knowledge in management, reducing workers to executors of pre-planned tasks, and destroying craft autonomy. This deskilling thesis connects to Marx's analysis of the real subsumption of labor under capital, in which the labor process is progressively reorganized to serve the imperatives of valorization. Debates concern whether Taylorism was actually implemented as comprehensively as Braverman suggested, whether workers exercised more resistance and retained more skill than the deskilling thesis allows, and how post-Fordist and lean production methods continue or modify Taylorist principles.
