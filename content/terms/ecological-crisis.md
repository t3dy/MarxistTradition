The ecological crisis, from a Marxist perspective, encompasses the interrelated phenomena of climate change, biodiversity collapse, soil degradation, resource depletion, and pollution that result from capitalist production and accumulation. Marx identified the tendential destruction of natural conditions in his analysis of capitalist agriculture and the metabolic rift. Contemporary Marxist ecology argues that the ecological crisis is not a problem of individual consumption or insufficient regulation but is structurally generated by capital's imperative to grow without limit in a finite biosphere. Andreas Malm's concept of fossil capital demonstrates how the adoption of fossil fuels was driven not by thermodynamic efficiency but by the requirements of labor discipline and surplus value extraction. The ecological crisis intensifies existing inequalities, as its effects fall disproportionately on the Global South and on racialized and working-class communities. Key debates concern the relationship between ecological limits and value theory, the viability of green capitalism, and the necessity of ecosocialist planning.
