The 1848 Revolutions were a wave of upheavals across Europe that fundamentally shaped Marx and Engels's political theory. Beginning in France and spreading to the German states, Austria, Hungary, and Italy, these revolutions saw bourgeois liberals and nascent working-class movements briefly unite against absolutist regimes before fracturing along class lines. Marx's analysis of these events, particularly in *The Eighteenth Brumaire* and *The Class Struggles in France*, established key concepts including Bonapartism, the relative autonomy of the state, and the distinction between bourgeois and proletarian revolution. The failure of 1848 demonstrated to Marx that the bourgeoisie would ally with reactionary forces rather than permit genuine democratic transformation, confirming the necessity of independent working-class political organization. These revolutions remain a key reference point for debates on permanent revolution, class alliances, and revolutionary strategy.
