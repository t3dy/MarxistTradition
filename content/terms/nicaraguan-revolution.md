The Nicaraguan Revolution of 1979 overthrew the US-backed Somoza dictatorship through an insurrection led by the Sandinista National Liberation Front (FSLN). The Sandinistas drew on Marxist-Leninist theory, liberation theology, and anti-imperialist nationalism to build a broad coalition including workers, peasants, students, and sectors of the bourgeoisie opposed to the Somoza regime.

The revolutionary government implemented land reform, literacy campaigns, and health programmes while pursuing a mixed economy rather than full socialisation. The US-funded Contra war devastated the economy and cost tens of thousands of lives. The Sandinistas lost the 1990 election under conditions of war exhaustion and economic embargo. The revolution raised important questions about the relationship between national liberation and socialist transformation, the viability of mixed economies, and the capacity of small nations to resist imperialist intervention.
