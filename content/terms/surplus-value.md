Surplus-value is the value produced by the worker in excess of the value of their labor-power, appropriated without equivalent by the capitalist. It arises because the use-value of labor-power — its capacity to create value through living labor — exceeds its own exchange-value as determined by the socially necessary labor time required for the worker's reproduction. If the worker reproduces the value of their wages in six hours but labors for ten, the additional four hours constitute surplus labor, whose product — surplus-value — is the source of the capitalist's profit. Surplus-value is the specific form that exploitation takes under capitalism, distinguishing it from the forms of surplus extraction characteristic of slave and feudal societies. Marx identifies two methods of increasing surplus-value: absolute surplus-value, produced by extending the working day, and relative surplus-value, produced by reducing necessary labor time through increases in productivity. The theory of surplus-value is the cornerstone of Marx's critique of political economy, demonstrating that profit does not arise from exchange, entrepreneurial ingenuity, or the productivity of capital, but from the systematic appropriation of unpaid labor.
