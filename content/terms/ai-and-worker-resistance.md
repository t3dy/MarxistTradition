AI and worker resistance describes the diverse forms of collective action through which workers contest the deployment of AI systems in the labor process and beyond. The 2023 Hollywood writers' and actors' strikes, which centrally addressed the use of AI to replace creative labor, demonstrated that automation remains what it has been since the Luddites: a terrain of class struggle in which the question is not whether technology should be used but who controls it, who benefits from it, and who bears its costs. 'Digital unionism' describes the emerging organisational forms through which gig workers, content moderators, and other digital laborers attempt to build collective power against platform employers, from the Deliveroo riders' wildcat strikes to Amazon warehouse unionisation campaigns.

'Platform cooperativism,' as theorised by Trebor Scholz, proposes an alternative to both capitalist platforms and traditional unionism: worker- and user-owned platforms that apply the cooperative model to digital infrastructure. The concept of 'post-work theory,' developed by Nick Srnicek, Alex Williams, and others, pushes further by arguing that the left should demand not better conditions within existing employment structures but the reduction of work itself, through full automation, universal basic income, and the radical shortening of the working week. This demand — that the reduction of necessary labor time through AI should translate into free time for workers rather than unemployment and intensified exploitation — represents the political horizon of the struggle over automation, connecting the immediate resistance of platform workers to the broader question of whether technological development can be directed toward human liberation rather than the reproduction of capital.
