Marxist state theory analyzes the state as an instrument and expression of class domination rather than a neutral arbiter of social conflicts. Marx and Engels characterized the state as the executive committee of the ruling class, though later formulations introduced greater complexity. Lenin's *State and Revolution* reasserted the class character of the state and the necessity of its revolutionary destruction. Gramsci's concept of the integral state, combining political society and civil society, emphasized the role of hegemony and consent alongside coercion. The Miliband-Poulantzas debate of the 1970s pitted an instrumentalist view of the state as directly controlled by the capitalist class against a structuralist account of the state as relatively autonomous but functionally constrained to reproduce capitalist relations. Subsequent debates have addressed state capitalism, the capitalist state's role in managing economic crises, and the relationship between national states and global capital.
