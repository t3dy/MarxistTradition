Immiseration (*Verelendung*) is the thesis that capitalist accumulation tends to worsen the conditions of the working class relative to the wealth it produces. Marx's general law of capitalist accumulation in Chapter 25 of *Capital, Volume I* holds that the growth of capital produces a corresponding growth in the industrial reserve army of labor and the pauperized layers of the population, concentrating wealth at one pole and misery at the other. The concept has been the subject of intense debate: some interpreters defend an absolute immiseration thesis in which real living standards decline, while others argue that Marx's position is better understood as relative immiseration — the growing disparity between the working class's share of social wealth and that of capital, compatible with rising real wages. Historical experience has complicated both positions, as periods of rising working-class living standards in the advanced capitalist countries have coexisted with deepening poverty in the Global South and periodic crises that destroy working-class gains. The concept remains relevant to contemporary debates about wage stagnation, wealth inequality, and the precariatization of labor.
