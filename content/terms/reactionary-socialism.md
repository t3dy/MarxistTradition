Reactionary socialism refers to the various forms of anti-capitalist sentiment that look backward to pre-capitalist social arrangements rather than forward to proletarian emancipation. Marx and Engels catalogued these tendencies in the *Communist Manifesto*, identifying feudal socialism, petty-bourgeois socialism, and clerical socialism as expressions of classes threatened by capitalist development who criticize bourgeois society from a nostalgic rather than revolutionary standpoint. Feudal socialists attacked the bourgeoisie not to advance the proletariat but to restore aristocratic privilege. Petty-bourgeois socialists like Sismondi offered acute critiques of capitalist contradictions but proposed returning to guild production and patriarchal agriculture. These currents are characterized by a romanticized view of earlier social forms that obscures their own exploitative character. The category remains analytically useful for identifying contemporary movements that combine anti-capitalist rhetoric with regressive social programs.
