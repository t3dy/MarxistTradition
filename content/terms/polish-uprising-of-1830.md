The Polish Uprising of November 1830 was a national insurrection against Russian imperial rule, led primarily by the Polish officer corps and the szlachta (nobility). The uprising sought to restore Polish independence, lost through the partitions of the late eighteenth century, and briefly established a revolutionary government before being crushed by Russian military force in September 1831.

Marx and Engels regarded Polish independence as strategically essential for European revolution, since tsarist Russia functioned as the chief bulwark of counter-revolution on the continent. The uprising generated waves of solidarity across Europe and radicalised Polish émigrés who participated in subsequent revolutionary movements. The Polish question recurred throughout the nineteenth century as a test case for the relationship between national liberation and social revolution, anticipating later debates about the national question in Marxist theory.
