Political power in Marxist theory is understood not as a neutral capacity exercised by whoever holds office, but as the organised expression of class domination. The *Communist Manifesto* defines political power as the organised power of one class for oppressing another. Marx's analysis of the state in *The Eighteenth Brumaire* and *The Civil War in France* showed how political forms serve class interests even when they appear autonomous.

The question of political power is central to revolutionary strategy: whether the working class can use existing state institutions or must create new ones (the lesson Marx drew from the Paris Commune); whether political organisation requires a vanguard party (Lenin) or can emerge from workers' councils (council communists); and whether political power must be seized in a revolutionary rupture or can be accumulated gradually through electoral and institutional means. Gramsci's theory of hegemony reframed political power as operating through civil society as well as the state.
