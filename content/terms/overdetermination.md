Overdetermination is a concept borrowed by Althusser from Freudian psychoanalysis and adapted to Marxist theory to describe the way in which every social contradiction is constituted by the multiplicity of contradictions in which it exists. Against the Hegelian model of a simple, expressive totality in which every element of the social whole is the expression of a single inner essence (such as the economy), Althusser argues that the Marxist social whole is a complex, structured totality in which each instance — economic, political, ideological — possesses relative autonomy and specific effectivity. No contradiction is ever simple or pure but is always specified by the other levels and contradictions that coexist with it, making the concrete historical situation irreducible to any single determining cause. The concept allows Althusser to maintain the Marxist thesis of determination in the last instance by the economic while rejecting economistic reductionism: the economic is determinant, but it is never the sole determinant, and it operates precisely by assigning dominance to one or another instance in a given conjuncture. The concept has been influential in political theory, cultural studies, and post-Marxist discourse analysis.
