Global production networks refer to the internationally dispersed but coordinated systems through which commodities are designed, produced, and distributed across multiple countries. Marxist analyses examine these networks as expressions of capital's drive to exploit global wage differentials, circumvent organised labour, and access cheap raw materials.

The geographical fragmentation of production has restructured the international division of labour, creating zones of intensive exploitation in the Global South while deindustrialising former manufacturing centres. Theorists such as Smith, Selwyn, and Starosta have analysed these networks through the lens of imperialism, unequal exchange, and the global law of value.
