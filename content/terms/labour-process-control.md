Labour process control encompasses the techniques through which capital organises, disciplines, and extracts maximum effort from workers in production. Marx analysed the transition from formal subsumption, where capital merely directs existing craft methods, to real subsumption, where machinery and management restructure the labour process itself to serve valorisation.

Braverman's *Labor and Monopoly Capital* (1974) revived Marxist analysis of the labour process by demonstrating how Taylorist scientific management systematically deskilled workers and transferred knowledge to management. Subsequent debates addressed automation, Fordist assembly lines, factory despotism, and lean production. Burawoy analysed how consent is manufactured on the shop floor. Contemporary developments include algorithmic management, digital surveillance, platform-mediated gig work, and new forms of workplace resistance that extend the struggle over control of labour into the digital age.
