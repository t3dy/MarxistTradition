Uneven and combined development describes the fundamental asymmetry of capitalist development across nations, regions, and sectors, and the characteristic amalgamation of advanced and archaic social forms that results from the integration of underdeveloped societies into the capitalist world market. Trotsky formulated the concept to explain how Russia, a predominantly agrarian country with autocratic political institutions, could develop a concentrated, modern industrial proletariat capable of leading a socialist revolution — a combination of the most advanced and the most backward features of social development within a single national formation. The unevenness of development is not an accidental or temporary condition but a structural feature of capitalism's expansion: the world market does not equalize conditions across nations but produces and reproduces systematic disparities, as the development of some regions depends on and intensifies the underdevelopment of others. The concept has been adopted far beyond Trotskyism, influencing dependency theory, world-systems analysis, and Marxist geography, and providing a framework for understanding global inequality, differential patterns of industrialization, and the political volatility of societies undergoing rapid but contradictory transformation.
