The concept of successive industrial revolutions periodises major technological transformations in capitalist production. The first (late 1700s) introduced steam power and mechanised textiles; the second (late 1800s) brought steel, chemicals, electricity, and assembly-line production; the third (mid-1900s) centred on electronics and computing; a fourth is often identified with digital automation, artificial intelligence, and platform capitalism.

Marxist analysis emphasises that each revolution transforms the organic composition of capital, displaces labour, creates new forms of surplus value extraction, and restructures class relations. Mandel's *Late Capitalism* linked long waves of accumulation to technological revolutions. Each transformation generates crises of adjustment as old industries decline and new ones emerge, while the fundamental relation between capital and labour is reproduced at higher levels of productivity and intensified exploitation.
