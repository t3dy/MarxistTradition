Absolutism denotes the centralized monarchical state form that prevailed in early modern Europe, characterized by the concentration of sovereign power, standing armies, bureaucratic administration, and mercantilist economic policy. In Marxist theory, absolutism occupies a contested transitional position between feudalism and capitalism. Marx and Engels treated it in *The Communist Manifesto* as an instrument that the rising bourgeoisie initially supported against feudal fragmentation, only to later overthrow. Perry Anderson's *Lineages of the Absolutist State* argued that absolutism was a reorganized apparatus of feudal domination designed to keep the peasantry in its traditional position, while Robert Brenner emphasized the divergent paths of Eastern and Western absolutism. The debate over whether absolutism served feudal or proto-capitalist interests remains central to Marxist state theory and the transition debate.
