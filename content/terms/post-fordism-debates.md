Post-Fordism debates concern the transformation of capitalist production since the crisis of the Fordist regime of accumulation in the 1970s. Fordism — characterised by mass production, Taylorist labour organisation, collective bargaining, and Keynesian demand management — gave way to what theorists have variously termed flexible specialisation, flexible accumulation, or post-Fordism.

The regulation school (Aglietta, Lipietz, Boyer) analysed these transformations through the concepts of regime of accumulation and mode of regulation. Harvey's *The Condition of Postmodernity* linked flexible accumulation to cultural postmodernism. Italian autonomists (Negri, Virno) theorised post-Fordism through the concepts of immaterial labour, the social factory, and the general intellect. Critics question whether post-Fordism represents a genuine structural transformation or merely the intensification and spatial reorganisation of existing capitalist relations, noting that Fordist production persists in much of the Global South.
