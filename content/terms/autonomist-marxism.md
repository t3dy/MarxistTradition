Autonomist Marxism (operaismo and post-operaismo) originated in 1960s Italy through the work of Mario Tronti, Raniero Panzieri, and Antonio Negri, emphasizing working-class autonomy from both capital and institutional labor organizations. The tradition inverts the orthodox reading of *Capital*, placing workers' struggles rather than capital's logic as the motor of historical development. Key concepts include class composition, the social factory, immaterial labor, and the general intellect. Tronti's strategy of refusal and Negri's theorization of the multitude as a new revolutionary subject extended the analysis beyond the factory into social reproduction and cognitive capitalism. Critics argue that autonomism overstates the agency of the multitude, undertheorizes the state, and in its post-operaist phase dissolves class specificity into diffuse networked resistance. The tradition significantly influenced anti-globalization movements and contemporary platform capitalism critiques.
