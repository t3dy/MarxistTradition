Subsumption (*Subsumtion*) describes the process by which capital subordinates the labor process to the imperatives of valorization. Marx distinguishes two forms. Under formal subsumption, capital takes command of labor processes that preexist it — artisanal, domestic, or peasant production — imposing the wage relation and extracting surplus-value without fundamentally altering the technical character of the work. The laborer now works for the capitalist rather than independently, but the methods of production remain essentially unchanged. Under real subsumption, capital transforms the labor process itself, revolutionizing technology, imposing the detailed division of labor, introducing machinery, and applying science to production in ways that make the worker an appendage of a process designed entirely according to the logic of capital's self-expansion. Real subsumption corresponds to the specifically capitalist mode of production and the production of relative surplus-value. The concept has been extended by autonomist Marxists and others to describe the "real subsumption of society," in which capital's logic penetrates not only factory production but all domains of social life — education, healthcare, culture, and subjectivity itself.
