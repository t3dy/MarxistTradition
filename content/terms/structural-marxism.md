Structural Marxism is the theoretical current associated primarily with Louis Althusser and his collaborators, including Etienne Balibar and Nicos Poulantzas, which reformulated historical materialism using structuralist methods drawn from linguistics and anthropology. Althusser rejected humanist and historicist readings of Marx, proposing instead an anti-humanist, anti-empiricist interpretation centered on the concept of structural causality. Key innovations include the theory of ideological state apparatuses, the concept of overdetermination, the distinction between real and formal subsumption, and the analysis of social formations as articulations of relatively autonomous levels (economic, political, ideological) determined in the last instance by the economy. Structural Marxism was influential in the 1960s and 1970s, shaping debates in political theory, cultural studies, and postcolonial analysis. It was criticized for functionalism, theoretical formalism, and marginalizing human agency and class struggle.
