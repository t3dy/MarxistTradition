Historical materialism is the theoretical framework holding that the material conditions of a society's mode of production fundamentally shape its social, political, and intellectual life. Formulated by Marx and Engels in *The German Ideology* (1846) and summarized in the famous 1859 Preface, it posits that historical change is driven by the development of productive forces and the class struggles arising from contradictions between those forces and the existing relations of production. When the relations of production become fetters on the further development of productive forces, an era of social revolution begins in which the legal, political, and ideological superstructure is transformed in correspondence with the revolutionized economic foundation. Rather than treating ideas, great individuals, or consciousness as the motor of history, historical materialism grounds historical explanation in the real processes of social production and reproduction. The framework has been the subject of extensive debate: critics charge it with economic determinism and teleological thinking, while defenders argue that Marx's formulations are far more nuanced than the base-determines-superstructure caricature suggests, and that the concept of "determination in the last instance" allows for the relative autonomy of political and ideological practices.
