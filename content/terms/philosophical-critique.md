Philosophical critique in the Marxist tradition refers to Marx's transformation of philosophy from speculative system-building into a critical method for analysing and transforming social reality. Marx's early critiques of Hegel and the Young Hegelians established that philosophy must be realised through practical transformation rather than remaining in the realm of ideas.

The critique of the metaphysics of political economy — exposing how bourgeois economists treat historically specific capitalist categories as natural and eternal — became central to *Capital*. Marx showed that categories like value, money, and capital are not transhistorical facts but historically specific social forms. The historical school of law similarly naturalised bourgeois legal categories. This philosophical critique extends through the Frankfurt School's critique of instrumental reason, Lukács's analysis of reification, and contemporary debates about whether Marx's critique is primarily epistemological, ontological, or practical.
