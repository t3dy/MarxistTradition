Orthodox Marxism refers to the defence of what are considered the fundamental principles of Marx's thought against revisionist, reformist, or eclectic modifications. The term was given its most famous formulation by Lukács in *History and Class Consciousness*, where he argued that orthodoxy in Marxism refers exclusively to method — the dialectical method — rather than to specific doctrinal theses.

In practice, different currents have claimed orthodoxy: Kautsky's Second International Marxism, Plekhanov's philosophical materialism, the Bolshevik tradition, and various academic reconstructions of Marx's critique of political economy. The label has been contested from all directions — structuralists, humanists, autonomists, and value-form theorists all claim to recover what Marx actually meant against the distortions of other schools. The debate over orthodoxy ultimately concerns the relationship between fidelity to Marx's texts, historical analysis, and the requirements of contemporary revolutionary practice.
