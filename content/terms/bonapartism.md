Bonapartism designates a political form in which the executive power of the state appears to rise above contending classes and achieve independence from civil society, while in practice securing the conditions for continued capitalist accumulation. Marx developed the concept in *The Eighteenth Brumaire* to analyze Louis-Napoleon Bonaparte's 1851 coup, showing how a situation of class equilibrium — in which no class possesses the strength to impose its direct political rule — creates space for a strongman figure to seize state power with the support of declassed and politically disorganized elements. Trotsky later extended the concept to analyze fascism and military dictatorships in the twentieth century, distinguishing between Bonapartism of decay and Bonapartism in periods of capitalist ascent. The concept remains essential for analyzing authoritarian regimes that claim to stand above class conflict while enforcing the interests of capital.
