The Spanish Communist Party (Partido Comunista de Espana, PCE), founded in 1921, played a significant and controversial role in Spanish politics throughout the twentieth century. Initially a small organization, it grew rapidly during the Spanish Civil War through Soviet support and its leadership of the International Brigades. Following Comintern directives, the PCE pursued a popular front strategy prioritizing military victory over social revolution, putting it in direct conflict with anarchists and the POUM. After Franco's victory, the party operated clandestinely for decades, becoming the most organized opposition force within Spain. Under Santiago Carrillo's leadership in the 1970s, the PCE became a leading proponent of Eurocommunism, advocating democratic transition and abandoning the dictatorship of the proletariat. Debates over the party's role center on its Civil War conduct, its relationship to Moscow, and its eventual embrace of parliamentary politics.
