Social reproduction theory examines the processes by which labor power is daily and generationally reproduced, including domestic labor, care work, education, and the maintenance of communities. Drawing on Marx's observation that the reproduction of labor power is a necessary condition for the reproduction of capital, theorists such as Lise Vogel, Silvia Federici, and Tithi Bhattacharya have argued that unwaged reproductive labor, disproportionately performed by women, is structurally integral to capitalist accumulation rather than external to it. The theory challenges the traditional Marxist focus on the point of production by revealing the household and community as sites of exploitation and struggle. Key debates concern whether reproductive labor produces value, the relationship between patriarchy and capitalism, and how racialized and migrant workers bear disproportionate burdens of social reproduction across global labor hierarchies.
