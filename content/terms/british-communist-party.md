The Communist Party of Great Britain (CPGB), founded in 1920, was the principal organizational expression of revolutionary Marxism in Britain for most of the twentieth century. The party played significant roles in the labour movement, anti-fascist organizing, and the 1926 General Strike, while remaining a relatively small force compared to the Labour Party. Its intellectual contributions included the Historians' Group, which produced major figures such as E.P. Thompson, Eric Hobsbawm, Christopher Hill, and Rodney Hilton, who collectively transformed the practice of social history. Internal crises over the 1956 Soviet invasion of Hungary and the later Eurocommunist turn led to significant splits and defections. The party's journal *Marxism Today* became influential in theorizing Thatcherism but was criticized for abandoning class politics. The CPGB dissolved in 1991, but its intellectual legacy, particularly in historiography, remains substantial.
