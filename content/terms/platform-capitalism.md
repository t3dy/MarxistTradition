Platform capitalism, as theorised by Nick Srnicek, describes the business model in which digital platforms position themselves as indispensable intermediaries — between workers and employers (Uber), consumers and producers (Amazon), users and advertisers (Google, Meta) — extracting fees, data, and rent from every transaction they mediate. The platform model represents a response to the profitability crisis of the post-2008 period: when traditional investment opportunities offer declining returns, capital flows toward platforms that can establish monopoly positions over digital infrastructure and extract rent from their control of the channels through which economic activity flows. The tendency toward monopoly is structural rather than incidental, as network effects, data advantages, and the costs of switching produce winner-take-all dynamics.

The concept of 'techno-feudalism,' advanced by Yanis Varoufakis and others, pushes this analysis further by arguing that platform monopolies have transcended capitalism itself, replacing profit with 'cloud rent' as the dominant mode of surplus extraction. Whether or not one accepts this provocative framing, it identifies a genuine transformation: the major platforms increasingly function not as firms competing in markets but as infrastructure owners extracting tolls from economic activity that must pass through their systems. The related concepts of 'digital rent,' 'cloud capital,' and 'infrastructure capitalism' all attempt to theorise how the ownership of computational infrastructure — data centres, cloud services, fibre-optic networks — constitutes a new form of monopoly power that differs in important ways from the industrial monopolies Marx and Lenin analysed.
