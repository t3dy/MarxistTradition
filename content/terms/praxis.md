Praxis designates the unity of theory and practice, of thinking and acting, that Marx counterposed to the merely contemplative materialism of Feuerbach and the merely speculative idealism of Hegel. In the *Theses on Feuerbach* (1845), Marx argues that the question of whether human thinking can attain objective truth is not a question of theory but a practical question: the truth of thought must be demonstrated in practice, and the philosophers have only interpreted the world when the point is to change it. Praxis is not blind activity but critically reflective, transformative action — revolutionary practice (*revolutionare Praxis*) — that simultaneously changes objective conditions and the consciousness of the agents who carry it out. The concept became central to Western Marxism, particularly in Lukacs's *History and Class Consciousness*, Gramsci's "philosophy of praxis," and the Yugoslav Praxis group, all of whom emphasized the creative, self-determining dimension of human activity against mechanical, deterministic interpretations of historical materialism. Whether the mature Marx retained this emphasis on praxis or replaced it with a structural analysis of impersonal social forms remains a fundamental question dividing humanist and anti-humanist readings of his work.
