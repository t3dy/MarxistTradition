Combined and uneven development, formulated by Trotsky to explain the peculiarities of Russian historical development, describes the process by which societies that enter the capitalist world system belatedly do not simply repeat the stages traversed by the advanced countries but instead assimilate the most modern techniques, institutions, and ideas alongside — and in combination with — archaic social forms that persist from earlier modes of production. The result is a contradictory amalgam in which the latest technologies of industrial production coexist with semi-feudal agrarian relations, producing explosive social tensions that the gradualist schemas of classical social democracy cannot accommodate. Trotsky argued that this combination, far from representing backwardness, creates revolutionary possibilities absent in the advanced countries, because the condensation of historical contradictions intensifies class conflict and compresses the timeline of political development. The concept has been widely adopted in world-systems analysis, postcolonial theory, and Marxist geography as a framework for understanding global inequality and the differentiated trajectories of capitalist development.
