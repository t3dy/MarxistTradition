Dependency theory is a body of Marxist and neo-Marxist analysis arguing that the underdevelopment of peripheral countries is not a pre-capitalist condition but a structural product of their integration into the capitalist world system on subordinate terms. Developed primarily by Latin American theorists including Andre Gunder Frank, Ruy Mauro Marini, Theotonio dos Santos, and Samir Amin, dependency theory challenged modernization theory's assumption that all countries follow a linear path from tradition to modernity. Frank's thesis of the "development of underdevelopment" argued that metropolitan accumulation and peripheral impoverishment are two sides of the same process. Marini theorized superexploitation of labor in the periphery, where workers are paid below the value of their labor power. Critics, including Robert Brenner and Bill Warren, argued that dependency theory underestimated the development of productive forces in the periphery and substituted circulationist analysis for class-based analysis of production relations. Despite these critiques, dependency theory remains influential in analyses of imperialism, unequal exchange, and global inequality.
