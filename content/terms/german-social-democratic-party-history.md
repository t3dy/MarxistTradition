The German Social Democratic Party (SPD), founded in 1875 at Gotha, became the world's largest Marxist party before 1914. Under the theoretical guidance of Kautsky and organisational leadership of Bebel, the SPD built a comprehensive workers' movement encompassing trade unions, cooperatives, and newspapers.

The Revisionism Debate, initiated by Bernstein in the 1890s, challenged orthodox Marxism. The SPD's vote for war credits in August 1914 shattered the Second International and provoked Lenin's break with social democracy. The party's suppression of the 1918 revolution further defined the division between reform and revolution. The SPD's history remains central to debates on party organisation and reformism.
