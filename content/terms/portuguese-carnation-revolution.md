The Portuguese Carnation Revolution of 25 April 1974 overthrew the Estado Novo dictatorship through a military coup by left-wing officers of the Armed Forces Movement (MFA), catalysed by exhaustion from colonial wars in Africa. The coup quickly became a social revolution as workers occupied factories, peasants seized latifúndia estates, and revolutionary organisations competed for influence.

The Processo Revolucionário em Curso (Ongoing Revolutionary Process) of 1974-75 saw extensive nationalisations, land reform, and workers' control, making Portugal the most radical revolutionary situation in Western Europe since 1936. The revolution was stabilised through the intervention of the Socialist Party under Mário Soares, supported by Western European social democracy, which channelled the movement toward parliamentary democracy. The Portuguese experience generated important debates about permanent revolution in European conditions, the role of the military in revolutionary processes, and the limits of social transformation without a break from capitalist world-market integration.
