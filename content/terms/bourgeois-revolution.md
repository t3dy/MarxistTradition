Bourgeois revolution designates the political transformations through which capitalist social relations displaced feudal ones, with the English Civil War, the French Revolution, and the American Revolution serving as paradigmatic cases. Marx understood these revolutions as episodes in which the bourgeoisie overthrew the political superstructure of feudalism to bring the state into correspondence with already-developing capitalist relations of production. The concept has generated extensive debate. Political Marxists like Robert Brenner argue that capitalist social-property relations preceded and caused bourgeois political revolution, while Benno Teschke questions whether 1789 was a bourgeois revolution at all. Neil Davidson's comprehensive reassessment defends a modified version of the concept by distinguishing bourgeois revolutions from revolutions made by the bourgeoisie. Trotsky's theory of permanent revolution further complicated the concept by arguing that in late-developing countries, the bourgeoisie could not complete its own revolutionary tasks.
