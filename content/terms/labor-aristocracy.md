The labor aristocracy refers to a privileged upper stratum of the working class in the advanced capitalist countries whose relatively higher wages, better working conditions, and social security are, in the classical formulation, financed by the superprofits that imperialist capital extracts from the exploitation of colonial and semi-colonial labor. Engels first identified the phenomenon in the mid-nineteenth century British working class, and Lenin developed it into a theory of reformism, arguing that the material basis of opportunism in the labor movement — the tendency of trade union leaders and social-democratic politicians to seek accommodation with capital rather than its overthrow — lies in the imperialist bribery of this privileged layer. The concept has been contested within Marxism: critics argue that it exaggerates the share of colonial superprofits flowing to metropolitan workers and risks dividing the international working class by implying that workers in the Global North are complicit beneficiaries of imperialism rather than exploited subjects of capital. The debate remains politically charged in discussions of labor internationalism, trade union strategy, and the structural divisions within the global working class.
