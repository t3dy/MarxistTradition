Crisis theory encompasses the various Marxist explanations for capitalism's inherent tendency toward periodic breakdowns — recessions, depressions, financial panics, and systemic dislocations that punctuate the process of accumulation. Marx did not leave a unified theory of crisis, and subsequent Marxists have developed competing accounts emphasizing different causal mechanisms: the tendency of the rate of profit to fall due to rising organic composition of capital; the disproportionality between departments of production; the underconsumption of the working class whose wages are held below the value of output; and the overproduction of commodities that cannot be profitably sold. These accounts are not necessarily mutually exclusive, and many Marxist economists treat them as complementary dimensions of a single contradictory dynamic. What unites all Marxist crisis theories is the insistence that crises are not external shocks or policy failures but expressions of capitalism's internal contradictions — the systematic tendency to produce more than can be absorbed under existing relations of production, necessitating the periodic destruction of capital values as the precondition for renewed accumulation.
