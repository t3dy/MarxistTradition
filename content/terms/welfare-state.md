The welfare state refers to the set of state institutions and policies that provide social insurance, public services, and income support to mitigate the most destructive effects of capitalist market relations on the working class. Marxist analyses of the welfare state are divided between those who view it as a genuine achievement of working-class struggle, wresting concessions from capital, and those who see it as a mechanism for stabilizing capitalism by managing social reproduction and integrating the working class into the existing order. The welfare state expanded dramatically in advanced capitalist countries after World War II, partly in response to the threat of socialist revolution and Cold War competition. Claus Offe analyzed the structural contradictions of welfare capitalism, while James O'Connor theorized the fiscal crisis of the state arising from the tension between accumulation and legitimation functions. Neoliberal attacks on the welfare state since the 1970s have intensified debates over its political significance.
