Maoism debates concern the theoretical and political legacy of Mao Zedong's contributions to Marxism, including the mass line, protracted people's war, new democracy, the theory of contradictions, and the Cultural Revolution. Mao adapted Marxism to Chinese conditions by centring the peasantry as the revolutionary class and developing strategies for revolution in semi-colonial, semi-feudal societies.

The Sino-Soviet split of the 1960s produced competing models of socialist construction. The Cultural Revolution generated intense debate: supporters saw it as a genuine attempt to prevent capitalist restoration through continuous revolution, while critics identified it as factional manipulation and mass persecution. After Mao's death, China's market reforms raised questions about whether Maoism contained the seeds of capitalist restoration. Contemporary Maoist movements in India, the Philippines, and Nepal continue to debate the applicability of protracted people's war and new democratic revolution.
