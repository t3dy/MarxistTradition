The Marxist critique of reformism targets the belief that capitalism can be fundamentally transformed through gradual legislative reform within existing state institutions. Rosa Luxemburg's *Reform or Revolution* provided the foundational argument that reforms within capitalism, while worth fighting for, cannot cumulatively produce socialism because the bourgeois state and property relations set structural limits on progressive legislation. The critique holds that reformism misunderstands the class character of the state, treating it as a neutral instrument rather than an organ of bourgeois class rule. Key debates include whether the welfare state represents genuine gains or the stabilization of capitalism, whether parliamentary roads to socialism are viable, and whether the distinction between reform and revolution has been superseded by changed conditions. The critique extends to philanthropic socialism and all currents that substitute moral appeals for class struggle.
