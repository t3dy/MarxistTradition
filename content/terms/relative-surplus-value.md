Relative surplus-value is the form of surplus-value produced by reducing the necessary labor time — the portion of the working day during which the worker reproduces the value of their own labor-power — through increases in the productivity of labor. Unlike absolute surplus-value, which extends the working day, relative surplus-value leaves its total length unchanged but alters the ratio between necessary and surplus labor time. When technological innovation cheapens the commodities that enter into the worker's consumption, the value of labor-power falls, and a greater portion of the working day is available for surplus labor. Marx argues that the systematic production of relative surplus-value is the specifically capitalist mode of exploitation, driven by competition among capitals to reduce costs through mechanization, the detailed division of labor, and the application of science to production. This dynamic produces the tendency toward constant technological revolution that distinguishes capitalism from all previous modes of production.
