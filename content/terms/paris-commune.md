The Paris Commune of 1871 was the first working-class government in history, lasting 72 days from 18 March to 28 May. Established after France's defeat in the Franco-Prussian War, the Commune arose when Parisian workers and National Guard units refused to surrender their arms to the reactionary Thiers government and seized control of the city.

The Commune implemented radical democratic measures: elected and recallable officials paid workers' wages, separation of church and state, workers' management of abandoned factories, and the abolition of the standing army in favour of the armed people. Marx analysed the Commune in *The Civil War in France* as the political form finally discovered under which the economic emancipation of labour could be accomplished. The Commune was drowned in blood during the Semaine Sanglante, with approximately 20,000 Communards massacred by Versailles troops. Its legacy shaped Lenin's *The State and Revolution* and remains central to debates about workers' democracy, the state, and revolutionary power.
