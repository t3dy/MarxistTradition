The Italian Communist Party (Partito Comunista Italiano, PCI) was founded in 1921 at the Livorno Congress as a split from the Italian Socialist Party, with Bordiga as its first leader. Gramsci became leader in 1924 before his imprisonment by the Fascist regime in 1926, during which he wrote the *Prison Notebooks* developing concepts of hegemony, the organic intellectual, and the war of position.

After World War II, the PCI became the largest communist party in Western Europe under Togliatti's leadership, pursuing a democratic road to socialism. Under Berlinguer in the 1970s, the PCI pioneered Eurocommunism and proposed the Historic Compromise with Christian Democracy. The party dissolved in 1991, with most members forming the post-communist Democratic Party of the Left. The PCI's history encapsulates central debates about revolution, reform, and the relationship between party and class.
