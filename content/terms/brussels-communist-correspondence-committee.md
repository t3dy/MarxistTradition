The Brussels Communist Correspondence Committee was established by Marx and Engels in 1846 to link communist groups and intellectuals across Europe through systematic political correspondence. Operating from Brussels where Marx had settled after expulsion from Paris, the committee represented a crucial organizational step between the scattered communist circles of the 1840s and the formal founding of the Communist League in 1847. The committee facilitated the ideological clarification that Marx and Engels considered essential to revolutionary practice, including the decisive break with the utopian socialism of Weitling and the philosophical communism of the True Socialists. Through its correspondence network spanning London, Paris, and various German cities, the committee functioned as a prototype of international communist organization and helped consolidate the materialist conception of history as the theoretical foundation for proletarian politics.
