The economic determinism debate concerns whether Marxism reduces all social, political, cultural, and ideological phenomena to direct expressions of the economic base. Critics, both internal and external, have charged that the base-superstructure metaphor in Marx's 1859 Preface to *A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy* implies a mechanistic, one-directional causality from economy to all other social spheres. Engels himself, in late letters, acknowledged the criticism and insisted that the economic element is determinant only "in the last instance," leaving room for the relative autonomy and reciprocal influence of politics, law, ideology, and culture. Subsequent Marxists developed diverse responses: Gramsci's theory of hegemony, Althusser's concept of overdetermination and structural causality, E.P. Thompson's emphasis on experience and agency, and the Analytical Marxists' attempts at methodological rigor. The debate is foundational because it shapes how Marxists understand the relationship between structural constraint and political possibility, between objective conditions and subjective intervention.
