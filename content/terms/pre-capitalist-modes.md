Pre-capitalist modes of production encompass the diverse forms of social organisation that preceded the capitalist mode of production. Marx outlined several in the *Grundrisse* and *A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy*: the Asiatic (tributary), ancient (slave-based), and feudal modes. Each is characterised by distinct forms of surplus extraction, property relations, and class structures.

Ancient slave societies extracted surplus through direct ownership of persons. Feudal relations operated through serfdom, rent, and the manor system, with guilds organising urban craft production. Tributary systems centralised surplus extraction through state or religious authority. Marx's analysis of the transition from feudalism to capitalism — through enclosure, colonialism, and primitive accumulation — remains foundational. The Asiatic mode of production generated extensive debate about whether Marx's schema was Eurocentric. Historians such as Brenner, Anderson, and Wood have refined understanding of the specific social property relations that generated capitalism's emergence in early modern England.
