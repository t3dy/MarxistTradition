Scientific socialism is the designation Engels gave to the Marxist approach to socialism, distinguishing it from utopian socialism by its grounding in the materialist analysis of history and political economy rather than moral appeals or imaginary social blueprints. Elaborated in *Socialism: Utopian and Scientific*, the concept holds that socialism is not a desirable ideal to be implemented by enlightened reformers but an objective historical possibility emerging from the contradictions of capitalist production itself. The scientific character lies in analyzing the real movement of society: the development of productive forces, the class struggle, and the tendential laws of capitalist accumulation. Critics have challenged the scientistic pretensions of this framework, arguing that it smuggles in deterministic assumptions and marginalizes ethical and political dimensions of socialist commitment. Others defend scientific socialism as essential for distinguishing rigorous social analysis from wishful thinking.
