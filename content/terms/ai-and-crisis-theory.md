AI and crisis theory examines how artificial intelligence interacts with the structural tendencies toward crisis that Marx identified as inherent to capitalist accumulation. The most fundamental connection operates through the organic composition of capital: as AI replaces living labor with fixed capital, it raises the organic composition and reinforces the tendency of the rate of profit to fall. Simultaneously, AI-driven automation expands productive capacity while reducing the purchasing power of displaced workers, intensifying the tendency toward overproduction — the contradiction between capitalism's unlimited drive to expand production and its systematic limitation of effective demand through the suppression of wages.

The specific form of crisis that AI-driven capitalism tends toward is what might be called the information abundance paradox: AI can produce text, images, music, code, and analysis at near-zero marginal cost, but the capitalist mode of production cannot accommodate abundance without scarcity. The response — intellectual property enforcement, subscription paywalls, platform monopolies, artificial limitation of AI capabilities — represents capital's attempt to impose the commodity form on goods that no longer possess the natural scarcity that commodity exchange presupposes. The contradiction between the forces of production that AI represents and the relations of production through which capital attempts to contain them expresses, in its most developed form, what Marx identified as the fundamental contradiction of capitalism: the tension between the social character of production and the private form of appropriation.
