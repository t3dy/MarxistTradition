The concept of the urban-rural proletariat addresses the composition, relationship, and political dynamics between wage laborers in cities and those in the countryside. Marx and Engels noted the antagonism between town and country as one of the fundamental divisions created by the capitalist mode of production, with urban industry dominating rural agriculture and draining the countryside of labor and resources. The agricultural proletariat, while sharing the fundamental class position of industrial workers, often faces distinct conditions including seasonal employment, geographic isolation, and different forms of surplus extraction. Revolutionary movements have grappled with the alliance between urban and rural proletarians, particularly in predominantly agrarian societies where peasants and agricultural laborers constitute the majority. Mao's theory of revolution centered rural mobilization, while classical Marxism generally prioritized the urban industrial proletariat. Contemporary analyses address the blurring of urban-rural boundaries through commuting, periurban development, and the informalization of labor.
