Permanent revolution is the theory, developed by Trotsky from Marx's 1850 "Address to the Communist League" and elaborated in *Results and Prospects* (1906) and *The Permanent Revolution* (1929), that in countries of belated capitalist development the tasks of the bourgeois-democratic revolution — land reform, national independence, democratic rights — cannot be accomplished by the national bourgeoisie, which is too weak, dependent on imperialism, and fearful of the working class to carry through its own historical program. These tasks therefore fall to the proletariat, which, having seized power at the head of the peasant masses, cannot stop at democratic reforms but must proceed to socialist measures, expropriating capital and establishing a workers' state. The revolution, moreover, cannot be completed within national boundaries but must extend internationally or face isolation and degeneration. Trotsky counterposed this perspective to the Stalinist theory of revolution in stages and socialism in one country, arguing that the Russian Revolution confirmed the permanent revolution thesis while its bureaucratic degeneration confirmed the impossibility of national socialism.
