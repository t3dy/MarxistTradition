The relationship between democracy and the state is among the most contested problems in Marxist theory. Marx's critique of Hegel's philosophy of right established that the modern state, including its democratic form, is not the realization of universal reason but the political expression of bourgeois civil society. Parliamentary democracy, in this analysis, manages class conflict while preserving capitalist property relations. Lenin's *State and Revolution* drew on Marx's analysis of the Paris Commune to argue for the smashing of the bourgeois state and its replacement by direct proletarian democracy through soviets. Democratic centralism, as theorized by Lenin, sought to combine disciplined organizational unity with internal debate. Rosa Luxemburg criticized the Bolsheviks' curtailment of democratic freedoms, insisting that socialism without democracy is no socialism at all. Gramsci's theory of hegemony complicated the base-superstructure model by analyzing democratic institutions as terrains of class struggle. Contemporary debates encompass the viability of democratic socialism, the relationship between liberal rights and socialist transformation, and the question of whether the capitalist state can be democratically repurposed.
