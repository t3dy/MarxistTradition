The wage system (*Lohnsystem*) refers to the totality of social arrangements through which labor power is bought and sold, encompassing not only the individual wage contract but also wage differentials, payment methods, and the institutional mechanisms that reproduce the working class's dependence on wages. Marx criticized the wage fund theory of classical political economy, which held that a fixed fund of capital determines total wages, arguing instead that wages are determined by class struggle within limits set by the value of labor power and the requirements of capital accumulation. The system of wage differentials between skilled and unskilled, male and female, and metropolitan and peripheral workers functions to divide the working class while enabling differential rates of exploitation. Wage suppression through unemployment, anti-union legislation, and global labor arbitrage remains a central mechanism of capitalist accumulation. The demand to abolish the wage system, rather than merely improve wages, distinguishes revolutionary from reformist politics.
