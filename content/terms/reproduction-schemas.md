The reproduction schemas, developed by Marx in Part III of *Capital, Volume II*, are formal models of the conditions under which the total social capital can reproduce itself on a simple or expanded scale. Marx divides social production into two great departments: Department I, producing means of production, and Department II, producing means of consumption. The schemas demonstrate that for reproduction to proceed without disruption, the output of each department must exchange with the other in definite proportions — Department I must produce enough means of production to replace those consumed by both departments, while Department II must produce enough consumption goods to sustain the workers and capitalists of both. Any systematic disproportionality between the departments creates the conditions for crisis. Luxemburg famously challenged the schemas by arguing that expanded reproduction requires non-capitalist markets to absorb the surplus product, while later Marxists have debated whether the schemas adequately account for money, credit, and the temporal dynamics of accumulation.
