Fordism designates the regime of capitalist accumulation characterised by mass production, semi-skilled assembly-line labour, rising real wages tied to productivity gains, and mass consumption. Named after Henry Ford's manufacturing innovations, the concept was developed by Gramsci in his analysis of Americanism and later elaborated by regulation school theorists such as Aglietta and Lipietz.

Fordism combined Taylorist labour process control with a macroeconomic mode of regulation involving collective bargaining, welfare state provisions, and Keynesian demand management. Its crisis — marked by declining productivity, profit squeeze, labour militancy, and stagflation — prompted restructuring toward post-Fordist flexible accumulation. Debates concern whether Fordism constituted a distinct stage of capitalism or merely a national variant.
