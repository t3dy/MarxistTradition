Imperialism, in Marxist theory, designates not merely the policy of territorial conquest but a structural stage of capitalist development in which the concentration of production into monopolies, the fusion of banking and industrial capital into finance capital, and the compulsion to export surplus capital drive the great powers toward the colonial partition of the world and inter-imperialist rivalry. Lenin's 1917 pamphlet, drawing on Hobson and Hilferding, provided the canonical formulation, arguing that imperialism is the highest and final stage of capitalism, characterized by parasitism, decay, and the intensification of contradictions that make socialist revolution both possible and necessary. Subsequent Marxist theories — dependency theory, world-systems analysis, theories of unequal exchange, and Harvey's concept of the new imperialism — have extended and revised Lenin's framework to account for decolonization, neocolonial economic domination, and the changing forms of imperial power in the late twentieth and twenty-first centuries. The concept remains central to Marxist analyses of global inequality, military intervention, and the structural relationship between the Global North and South.
