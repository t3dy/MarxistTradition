Democratic centralism is the organizational principle, most fully elaborated by Lenin and adopted by the Bolshevik party, that combines freedom of discussion with unity of action. In theory, all members participate in debating policy and strategy, decisions are reached through majority vote, and once a decision is taken, all members are bound to carry it out regardless of their individual positions. The principle was formulated in opposition to both the loose, federalist structures of social-democratic parties, which Lenin regarded as incapable of disciplined revolutionary action, and the authoritarian centralism of conspiratorial sects that excluded rank-and-file participation. In practice, the balance between democracy and centralism has been contested throughout the history of the communist movement. Critics from the left, including Luxemburg, the council communists, and various libertarian socialist currents, argued that centralism inevitably predominated over democracy, producing bureaucratic party apparatuses that substituted themselves for the working class. The concept remains a point of sharp debate in revolutionary politics.
