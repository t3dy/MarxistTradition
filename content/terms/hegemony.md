Hegemony describes the process by which a ruling class secures and maintains its dominance not only through coercion and state power but through the active consent of subordinate groups, achieved via intellectual, moral, and cultural leadership within civil society. Gramsci developed the concept in the *Prison Notebooks* to explain how bourgeois rule is sustained through institutions such as schools, churches, media, trade unions, and cultural organizations that naturalize the existing order, making it appear not as the imposition of one class's interests but as the expression of universal values. Hegemony is never total or permanent but must be continually constructed, defended, and renewed against challenges from below. Counter-hegemony — the construction of an alternative worldview, set of values, and system of alliances by subaltern classes — is therefore central to any revolutionary strategy in societies with developed civil societies, where a frontal assault on state power (war of maneuver) is insufficient without first winning intellectual and moral leadership (war of position). The concept has been enormously influential beyond Marxism, shaping debates in cultural studies, postcolonial theory, and political science.
