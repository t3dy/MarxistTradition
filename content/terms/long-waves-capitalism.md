Long waves of capitalism theory posits that capitalist development follows roughly fifty-year cycles of expansion and contraction, beyond shorter business cycles. Kondratiev first identified these patterns empirically in the 1920s. Mandel developed the most systematic Marxist account in *Late Capitalism* (1972), arguing that long waves are driven by fluctuations in the rate of profit linked to technological revolutions and class struggle.

Each expansive wave is associated with a new technological paradigm — steam, railways, electricity, electronics — that cheapens constant capital and temporarily restores profitability. Depressive phases are marked by crisis, restructuring, intensified class conflict, and geopolitical instability. Unlike Kondratiev, Mandel argued that the transition from depressive to expansive phases is not automatic but depends on extra-economic factors including class defeats, wars, and the opening of new markets. The framework illuminates neoliberalism as a response to the post-1973 long downturn.
