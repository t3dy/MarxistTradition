Algorithmic management describes the use of automated systems to direct, evaluate, and discipline labor, extending the trajectory from Taylorist scientific management through Fordist assembly lines to digital control systems that operate in real time through software. In warehouses, gig platforms, and call centres, algorithms assign tasks, set pace, monitor performance, and terminate workers without human managerial intervention. This represents what Harry Braverman analysed as the progressive separation of conception from execution in the labor process — the transfer of knowledge and decision-making from workers to management — now mediated by computational systems that can process information and impose discipline at speeds and scales no human manager could achieve.

The related concepts of 'digital Taylorism' and 'cybernetic management' capture different dimensions of this transformation. Digital Taylorism describes the decomposition of cognitive and service work into standardised, measurable micro-tasks that can be monitored and optimised through software — extending to white-collar work the same logic of deskilling that Taylorism applied to manual labor. Cybernetic management describes the feedback loops through which algorithmic systems continuously adjust labour processes in response to real-time data, producing a form of control that is simultaneously more pervasive and less visible than the direct supervision of the human manager. Algorithmic governance extends this logic beyond the workplace to encompass social administration, criminal justice, welfare distribution, and urban planning, raising the question of whether automated decision-making represents a qualitative transformation in how capitalist societies are governed.
