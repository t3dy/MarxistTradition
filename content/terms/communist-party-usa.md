The Communist Party USA (CPUSA), founded in 1919, was the principal Marxist-Leninist party in the United States for much of the twentieth century. It played a significant role in labor organizing, anti-racist struggles, and cultural politics, particularly during the 1930s and 1940s, when it built substantial influence in the Congress of Industrial Organizations (CIO) and among African American communities. The CPUSA's close alignment with the Soviet Union, enforced through the Comintern's discipline, shaped both its strengths in international solidarity and its vulnerabilities to shifts in Soviet foreign policy, most notably the Hitler-Stalin Pact of 1939. McCarthyism and Cold War repression devastated the party in the 1950s. Marxist debates around the CPUSA concern the limits of popular front strategy, the relationship between anti-racism and class struggle in the American context, and the structural constraints on revolutionary organization within advanced capitalist democracies.
