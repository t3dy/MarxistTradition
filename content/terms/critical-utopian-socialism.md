Critical-Utopian Socialism designates the body of socialist thought that Marx and Engels, in the *Communist Manifesto*, attributed to Saint-Simon, Fourier, and Owen. They distinguished these thinkers from both reactionary and bourgeois socialisms by recognizing that the critical-utopians offered genuine insights into the contradictions of capitalist society and anticipated elements of future social organization â€” the abolition of the distinction between town and country, the transformation of the state, and the reconception of labor. However, Marx and Engels argued that these thinkers necessarily remained utopian because they wrote before the proletariat had developed as a revolutionary force, leading them to substitute blueprints for a just society in place of analysis of actual class struggle. The critical-utopians appealed to universal reason rather than to the self-activity of the working class. Their legacy has been reassessed by scholars like Ernst Bloch, who recuperated the utopian dimension as a necessary element of revolutionary consciousness.
