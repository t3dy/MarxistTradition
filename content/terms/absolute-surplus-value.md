Absolute surplus-value is the form of surplus-value produced by extending the length of the working day beyond the time necessary for the worker to reproduce the value of their labor-power. If the necessary labor time is six hours, the capitalist increases surplus-value by compelling the worker to labor for ten, twelve, or more hours, with the additional hours constituting unpaid surplus labor. Marx identifies this as the historically primary method of exploitation, characteristic of early capitalism and the period of manufacture, and traces the political struggles over the legal limitation of the working day as a central arena of class conflict. The production of absolute surplus-value corresponds to the formal subsumption of labor under capital, where the capitalist commands the labor process without yet revolutionizing its technical basis.
