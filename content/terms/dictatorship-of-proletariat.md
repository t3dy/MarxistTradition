The dictatorship of the proletariat designates the transitional political form between capitalist and communist society, in which the working class holds state power and uses it to suppress the resistance of the dispossessed bourgeoisie, reorganize production on a socialist basis, and begin the dissolution of class distinctions. Marx drew the concept from the experience of the Paris Commune of 1871, which he interpreted as a working model of proletarian political power characterized by the election and recall of all officials, the abolition of the standing army in favor of the armed people, and the fusion of legislative and executive functions. Lenin developed the concept extensively in *The State and Revolution*, arguing that the bourgeois state must be smashed rather than reformed and replaced by a commune-type state that would progressively wither away as class antagonisms dissolved. The term has been the subject of intense debate, with critics arguing that "dictatorship" inevitably produces authoritarian rule, while defenders maintain that Marx used the term in its Roman sense to denote emergency class rule, not personal tyranny.
