Technological unemployment describes the displacement of workers by automated systems, a process Marx analysed extensively in Capital's chapters on machinery and modern industry. Marx demonstrated that the introduction of machinery under capitalism serves not to lighten labor but to cheapen it, replacing skilled workers with machines and creating what he called the 'industrial reserve army' — a surplus population of unemployed workers whose existence disciplines the employed through the constant threat of replacement. AI-driven automation extends this logic to cognitive, creative, and communicative tasks previously considered immune to mechanisation, raising the prospect of displacement across professional and service sectors as well as manufacturing.

The related concepts of 'deskilling' and 'reskilling' describe the transformation of the labor process that accompanies automation. Harry Braverman's analysis of deskilling — the systematic degradation of craft knowledge as tasks are broken down and transferred to machines — applies directly to AI systems that decompose cognitive work into trainable subtasks. The counter-narrative of 'reskilling' claims that automation creates new, higher-skilled jobs to replace those it destroys, but the historical evidence suggests that new employment is typically more precarious, lower-paid, and less autonomous than the work it replaces. The concept of 'class recomposition' describes how automation restructures the working class itself, creating new divisions between those who build and control AI systems and those displaced or degraded by them.
