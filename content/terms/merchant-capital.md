Merchant capital (commercial capital) is the form of capital operating in the sphere of circulation, buying commodities to sell at a profit. Marx analysed it in *Capital* Volume 3, Part IV, distinguishing it from industrial capital, which creates surplus value in production. Merchant capital merely realises in circulation the surplus value already produced, receiving a share of total social surplus value through the tendency of profit rates to equalise.

Historically, merchant capital preceded industrial capitalism and played a pivotal role in primitive accumulation, colonial trade, and the dissolution of feudal relations. However, Marx argued that merchant capital alone cannot transform the mode of production — it can coexist with and even reinforce pre-capitalist forms. Only when merchant capital becomes subordinated to industrial capital does the specifically capitalist mode of production emerge. The distinction remains important for analysing contemporary commercial enterprises, retail capital, and platform intermediaries.
