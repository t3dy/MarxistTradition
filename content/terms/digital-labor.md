Digital labor encompasses the diverse forms of productive activity that generate value within the digital economy, from the waged work of content moderators, data labellers, and gig workers to the unwaged activity of social media users whose clicks, posts, and browsing patterns produce the data commodities that platform corporations sell to advertisers. Tiziana Terranova's concept of 'free labor' — simultaneously freely given and unpaid — captured the foundational insight that the internet economy depends structurally on extracting value from activity that falls outside the wage relation. The category also includes what autonomist theorists call 'immaterial labor' (the production of information, communication, and affect) and 'affective labor' (the production of emotional states and social bonds), both of which have become increasingly central to capitalist valorisation.

The concept of the 'digital proletariat' extends class analysis to encompass the full chain of exploitation that sustains the digital economy — from coltan miners and hardware assemblers in the Global South to the click-workers, content moderators, and gig workers who perform the labour that AI systems and platforms require. Christian Fuchs and others have argued that social media users perform productive labor analogous to the audience labor that Dallas Smythe identified in broadcast media, generating the attention-commodity that platforms sell. The debate over whether unwaged digital activity constitutes labor in the Marxist sense — producing surplus value through exploitation — or represents a new form of rent extraction remains one of the central theoretical questions in the Marxist analysis of digital capitalism.
