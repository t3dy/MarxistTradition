Workers' self-management refers to systems in which workers collectively control the enterprises in which they labor, making decisions about production, distribution, and workplace organization without subordination to external owners or state bureaucracies. Historical examples include the Paris Commune, the factory committees of the Russian Revolution, Spanish Civil War collectivizations, and the Yugoslav system of self-management established under Tito. Marx's analysis of the Commune in *The Civil War in France* celebrated workers' direct management of production as a prefiguration of communist social organization. Debates concern whether self-management within individual enterprises can transcend market competition or merely reproduces capitalist dynamics at the firm level, whether workers' cooperatives within capitalism demonstrate the viability of self-management or are absorbed into capitalist logic, and the relationship between workplace democracy and broader economic coordination through planning.
