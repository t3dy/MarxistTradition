The Chartist Movement (c. 1838â€“1857) was the first mass working-class political movement, organized around the six demands of the People's Charter in Britain. For Marx and Engels, Chartism was decisive evidence that the proletariat could act as an independent political force. Engels analyzed it extensively in *The Condition of the Working Class in England*, while Marx saw its combination of democratic and social demands as prefiguring revolutionary politics. The movement encompassed mass petitions, general strike agitation, armed uprisings such as the Newport Rising of 1839, and experiments in cooperative production and land reform under Feargus O'Connor. Its internal tensions between moral-force and physical-force wings, and between purely political demands and broader social transformation, established enduring strategic debates within the workers' movement. Chartism's decline after 1848 raised critical questions about reformism, state repression, and the limits of democratic agitation under capitalism.
