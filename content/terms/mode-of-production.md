A mode of production (*Produktionsweise*) is the historically specific unity of productive forces and relations of production that characterizes a given form of society. It encompasses not only the technical means by which goods are produced but the social relations under which production takes place — property relations, the division of labor, and the specific form through which surplus labor is extracted from the direct producers. Marx identifies several successive modes of production — ancient, feudal, Asiatic, and capitalist — each defined by a distinct form of surplus extraction and class antagonism. The concept is not merely economic but designates a total social form that shapes political institutions, legal systems, ideological frameworks, and cultural production. The transition from one mode of production to another is driven, in Marx's account, by the development of productive forces into contradiction with existing relations of production — a process that unfolds through class struggle and social revolution. The concept remains contested: Althusserians emphasize the mode of production as a theoretical object defined by its internal structure, while historicist Marxists treat it as a descriptive category for empirically observable social formations.
