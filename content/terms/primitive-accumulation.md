Primitive accumulation (*ursprungliche Akkumulation*) designates the historical process by which producers were separated from the means of production, creating the two fundamental preconditions for capitalist relations: a class of propertyless workers compelled to sell their labor-power, and a class of capitalists who have concentrated sufficient wealth to purchase that labor-power and set it to work. Marx devoted Part VIII of *Capital, Volume I* to demolishing the bourgeois fable of "previous accumulation" — the myth that capitalists acquired their wealth through thrift and industry while the poor squandered theirs through profligacy. In its place, he narrates the actual history of enclosure of common lands in England, colonial plunder, the slave trade, public debt, the expropriation of church and monastic property, and the use of state violence to create a disciplined labor force. David Harvey's concept of "accumulation by dispossession" extends Marx's analysis to contemporary processes of privatization, financialization, and the ongoing enclosure of commons across the Global South, arguing that primitive accumulation is not merely a historical precondition but a permanent feature of capitalist development.
