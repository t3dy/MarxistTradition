term: Financialisation
tags: [theory, political economy]
traditions:
  classical: "Financialisation represents capital's response to declining profitability in production, as surplus value increasingly circulates through speculative financial channels rather than being reinvested productively, deepening systemic instability."
  value-form: "Financialisation demonstrates the capacity of the value-form to generate increasingly abstract layers of social mediation through credit, derivatives, and fictitious capital, where money capital's self-referential movement appears to bypass valorisation entirely."
---

Financialisation refers to the growing dominance of financial motives, institutions, and markets in domestic and international economies since the 1970s. Marxist analyses connect financialisation to the crisis of profitability in productive capital, the expansion of fictitious capital, and neoliberal restructuring of class relations.

Key features include the proliferation of financial instruments, subordination of corporations to shareholder value, household debt expansion, and speculation displacing productive investment. Debates divide between those viewing financialisation as a symptom of overaccumulation crisis in production and those seeing it as a relatively autonomous transformation. Writers such as Lapavitsas, Fine, and McNally have analysed financialisation in relation to the falling rate of profit, the transformation of banking, and exploitation through financial extraction from wages.
