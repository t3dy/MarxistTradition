Chartism was the first mass working-class political movement, active in Britain from roughly 1838 to 1857, organized around the People's Charter demanding universal male suffrage, secret ballot, annual parliaments, equal constituencies, payment for MPs, and abolition of property qualifications. Engels analyzed Chartism in *The Condition of the Working Class in England* as evidence that the proletariat was developing independent political consciousness, while Marx saw it as the first movement in which the working class acted as a class for itself. The movement combined political radicalism with economic grievances rooted in the factory system, and its internal tensions between moral force and physical force strategies prefigured later debates on reform and revolution. Chartism's decline after 1848 coincided with the mid-Victorian economic boom, which Marx and Engels connected to Britain's imperial position and the emergence of a labor aristocracy.
