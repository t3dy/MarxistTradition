Analytical Marxism emerged in the late 1970s and 1980s as an attempt to reconstruct Marxist theory using the methods of analytical philosophy, rational choice theory, and neoclassical economics. Associated with G.A. Cohen, Jon Elster, John Roemer, and Erik Olin Wright, the school rejected dialectical reasoning and functionalist explanation in favor of methodological individualism and rigorous logical argument. Cohen's *Karl Marx's Theory of History* defended historical materialism through analytical philosophy, while Roemer recast exploitation theory using general equilibrium models. Critics from across the Marxist spectrum charged that analytical Marxism stripped historical materialism of its dialectical core, reduced class struggle to individual rational calculation, and capitulated to bourgeois social science methodology. The movement nonetheless forced productive clarifications of Marxist concepts and sharpened debates about exploitation, class, and historical explanation.
