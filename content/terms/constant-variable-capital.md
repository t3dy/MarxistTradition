Constant capital is the portion of capital invested in means of production — machinery, raw materials, buildings, and auxiliary materials — whose value is transferred to the product without increase during the production process. These elements of production are consumed and their value reappears in the commodity, but they add no new value beyond what they already possessed. Variable capital is the portion invested in labor-power, which not only reproduces its own value (the wage) but generates additional surplus-value through the expenditure of living labor beyond the necessary labor time. This distinction is fundamental to Marx's theory of exploitation, as it demonstrates that profit originates solely from the variable component of capital — from the difference between the value labor-power creates and the value labor-power costs. Classical political economy obscured this by treating all capital as equally productive, collapsing the qualitative difference between dead and living labor and mystifying the origin of profit.
