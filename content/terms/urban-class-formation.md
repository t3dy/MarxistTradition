Urban class formation examines how the spatial organization of cities shapes the development of class structures, consciousness, and conflict under capitalism. Marx analyzed how capitalist urbanization concentrates workers in industrial cities, creating the conditions for collective organization and political action. Engels's study of Manchester documented how urban geography reflected and reinforced class divisions through segregated housing, differential access to infrastructure, and the physical separation of bourgeois and proletarian quarters. Henri Lefebvre's work on the production of space extended this analysis, arguing that urban space is actively produced through capitalist social relations and class struggle. David Harvey has examined how capital uses urbanization as a means of absorbing surplus value through real estate development and infrastructure investment. Debates concern gentrification, the role of the urban poor and informal settlements, and whether contemporary urban movements constitute a new terrain of class politics.
