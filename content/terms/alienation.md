Alienation describes the estrangement of workers from the products of their labor, from the labor process itself, from their species-being (*Gattungswesen*), and from other human beings. Under capitalism, the worker's activity becomes an external, coerced force that belongs to another rather than an expression of free creative capacity. Marx developed this concept most fully in the *Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844*, identifying four dimensions of alienation that together constitute the lived experience of exploitation. The concept's status within the Marxist tradition is contested: humanist Marxists regard it as the ethical core of Marx's entire project, while Althusserians argue that the mature Marx abandoned the anthropological framework of alienation in favor of the scientific analysis of exploitation and structural determination. The concept persists in transformed form in *Capital* through categories such as commodity fetishism and the real subsumption of labor under capital, suggesting a continuity between the early and late Marx that many commentators have sought to trace.
