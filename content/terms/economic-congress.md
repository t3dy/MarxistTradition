Economic Congresses were assemblies convened by bourgeois economists and political figures in nineteenth-century Europe to discuss questions of trade policy, industrial regulation, currency, taxation, and economic reform. Marx engaged critically with these gatherings as sites where the ideological presuppositions of bourgeois political economy were publicly articulated and contested. These congresses often promoted laissez-faire economics, free trade, and the harmonization of commercial law, presenting the interests of the capitalist class as universal economic rationality. Marx's critical analysis of such events, particularly in his journalism for the *New York Tribune* and the *Neue Rheinische Zeitung*, demonstrated his method of revealing the class content of ostensibly neutral economic discourse. The congresses are significant for understanding the institutional self-organization of the bourgeoisie as a class and the development of economic policy as a terrain of ideological struggle between competing class interests and fractions of capital.
