The May 1968 uprisings in France began as student protests at Nanterre and the Sorbonne before catalysing the largest general strike in European history, with ten million workers occupying factories. The movement combined demands for educational reform, workers' control, sexual liberation, and revolutionary transformation of everyday life.

The French Communist Party and the CGT union federation channelled the strike toward wage demands and the Grenelle Agreements, while the Gaullist state survived through a combination of concessions and electoral counter-mobilisation. The events profoundly influenced the New Left, Situationist theory, autonomist Marxism, and post-structuralist thought. Debates continue over whether May 1968 represented a genuine revolutionary crisis betrayed by reformist leadership, a cultural revolution that transformed social norms without challenging capitalism, or a revolt of the new middle class rather than the traditional proletariat.
