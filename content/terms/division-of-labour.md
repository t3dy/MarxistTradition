The division of labour (Teilung der Arbeit) is a foundational concept in Marx's analysis of capitalism, examined extensively in *Capital* Volume 1, Chapters 14â€“15. Marx distinguishes between the social division of labor (the distribution of productive activities across society) and the manufacturing or detail division of labor (the fragmentation of a single production process into specialized operations). While Adam Smith celebrated the division of labor as the source of productivity and wealth, Marx demonstrated that under capitalism it serves as a mechanism for increasing relative surplus value, deskilling workers, and subordinating labor to capital. The transition from manufacture to modern industry represents the real subsumption of labor, as the worker becomes an appendage of the machine. Marx also analyzed the gendered division of labor and its role in cheapening labor power. The concept connects to broader themes of alienation, as the division of labor separates workers from the product, the process, their fellow workers, and their species-being.
