The relationship between artificial intelligence and Marx's value theory constitutes one of the most contested questions in contemporary Marxist political economy. The central problem is straightforward: if surplus value derives from the exploitation of living labor, and if AI progressively replaces living labor in production, then AI threatens the very foundation of capitalist value production. AI functions as what Marx called fixed capital or constant capital — dead labor that transfers its value to commodities but cannot create new value. As AI raises the organic composition of capital (the ratio of constant to variable capital), it reinforces the tendency of the rate of profit to fall, a tendency that Marx identified as the most fundamental law of capitalist accumulation.

The question of whether AI-driven automation produces relative surplus value — by cheapening the commodities that workers consume, thereby reducing necessary labor time and increasing the surplus — or whether it ultimately undermines surplus value production altogether by eliminating the living labor from which surplus value derives, is not merely theoretical. It bears directly on whether capitalism can sustain itself through a new phase of AI-driven accumulation or whether AI represents a productive force that has outgrown the capitalist relations of production. The tension between AI's capacity to generate material abundance and capital's need to maintain artificial scarcity through market mechanisms, intellectual property, and the wage relation expresses what may be the defining contradiction of twenty-first-century capitalism.
