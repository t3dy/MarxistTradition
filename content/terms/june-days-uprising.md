The June Days Uprising of 1848 in Paris was a decisive moment in European class struggle. After the February Revolution established the Second Republic, the provisional government's closure of the National Workshops — a programme of state-funded employment for the unemployed — provoked an insurrection of Parisian workers from 23 to 26 June.

General Cavaignac crushed the uprising with extreme violence, killing thousands and deporting thousands more. Marx analysed the June Days in *The Class Struggles in France* as the first great battle between proletariat and bourgeoisie, revealing that the democratic republic was the form of state in which the class struggle would be fought to its conclusion. The defeat inaugurated a period of reaction culminating in Louis Bonaparte's coup of December 1851, which Marx anatomised in *The Eighteenth Brumaire*.
