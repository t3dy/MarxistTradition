Commercial Congress refers to the various bourgeois assemblies and conferences convened during the nineteenth century to promote free trade, commercial liberalization, and the harmonization of trade policy across national boundaries. Marx and Engels engaged critically with these congresses, most notably the 1847 Brussels Congress on Free Trade, where Marx delivered his famous speech on free trade, arguing that while free trade accelerated the dissolution of national economic barriers and sharpened class antagonisms, protectionism merely served particular fractions of the bourgeoisie. These events were significant sites of ideological production for the capitalist class, articulating the interests of commercial and industrial capital in universalist terms. Marx's critique of the commercial congresses exemplified his method of analyzing bourgeois ideology as the expression of determinate material interests presented as natural law, revealing the class content concealed behind the rhetoric of mutual benefit and civilizational progress.
