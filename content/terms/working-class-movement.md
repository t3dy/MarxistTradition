The working-class movement encompasses the organised struggles of wage labourers against exploitation, from early trade combinations and mutual aid societies to mass socialist parties, trade unions, and revolutionary organisations. Marx and Engels traced its origins to the resistance of English workers to the Industrial Revolution, through Luddism, Chartism, and the formation of trade unions.

The movement developed through distinct phases: early craft unionism, the formation of mass socialist parties in the Second International period, the revolutionary upheavals of 1917-23, the welfare-state compromises of the post-war era, and the defensive struggles under neoliberalism. Key debates concern the relationship between economic and political struggle, the role of trade unions versus revolutionary parties, the incorporation of the labour movement into capitalist institutions, and whether the contemporary fragmentation of the working class through precarious employment, migration, and deindustrialisation has fundamentally transformed the conditions of collective organisation.
