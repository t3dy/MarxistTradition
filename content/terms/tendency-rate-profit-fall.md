The tendency of the rate of profit to fall is Marx's thesis, developed in Part III of *Capital, Volume III*, that the competitive drive to raise labor productivity through technological innovation systematically undermines the conditions of profitability. As individual capitalists adopt labor-saving machinery to gain a competitive edge, the organic composition of capital — the ratio of constant to variable capital — rises across the economy. Since surplus-value is produced only by living labor (variable capital), a rising proportion of constant capital in the total means that the rate of surplus-value to total capital advanced declines, even as the mass of profit may continue to grow. Marx identifies several counteracting tendencies — the cheapening of elements of constant capital, rising intensity of exploitation, depression of wages below value, foreign trade, and the expansion of joint-stock companies — that offset but do not permanently reverse the tendency. The law has generated more debate than any other proposition in Marxist economics, with critics questioning its logical coherence, its empirical testability, and its relationship to other theories of crisis. Defenders argue that it captures the fundamental contradiction of capitalist accumulation: the same process that drives technological progress simultaneously undermines the social basis of profitability.
