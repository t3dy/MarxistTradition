The relationship between artificial intelligence and ideology operates on multiple levels. At the most immediate, AI systems — recommendation algorithms, search engines, content moderation tools, targeted advertising — function as what Althusser called ideological state apparatuses, shaping the information environment through which subjects form their understanding of the world. The algorithmic curation of news, social media, and search results produces what could be called automated ideology: the systematic shaping of consciousness not through explicit propaganda but through the structural organisation of attention according to engagement metrics that favour emotional intensity over analytical depth. AI does not need to promote a specific political position to perform an ideological function; it need only optimise for engagement to reproduce the conditions under which critical analysis becomes structurally impossible.

At a deeper level, AI transforms the relationship between the economic base and the ideological superstructure. When AI systems generate text, images, and analysis, the production of culture and ideas is directly subsumed under the logic of capital accumulation. The commodification of language through large language models — trained on the collective linguistic production of humanity but owned and controlled by a handful of corporations — represents a new form of enclosure in which the commons of human expression is appropriated as proprietary capital. Algorithmic bias, far from being a technical problem amenable to engineering solutions, constitutes the structural reproduction of existing class, racial, and gender hierarchies through automated systems that encode and operationalise the patterns of domination present in their training data.
