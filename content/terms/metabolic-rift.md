The metabolic rift describes capitalism's systematic disruption of the metabolic interaction between human societies and the natural world. Marx used the concept of metabolism (*Stoffwechsel*) to characterize the labor process as the mediating activity through which humans regulate their material interchange with nature. In *Capital, Volume III*, he argued that capitalist agriculture — by concentrating populations in cities while extracting nutrients from the soil to feed them — creates an irreparable rift in this metabolism, depleting the earth's fertility and rendering sustainable reproduction impossible under existing relations of production. John Bellamy Foster revived and systematized this concept in *Marx's Ecology* (2000), arguing that Marx's analysis of the soil nutrient crisis anticipated contemporary ecological thought and provides the basis for a materialist ecology. The concept has since been extended beyond agriculture to encompass capitalism's disruption of planetary ecological systems — climate, biodiversity, ocean chemistry, nitrogen and phosphorus cycles — providing a framework for understanding the ecological crisis as an inherent consequence of the capitalist mode of production rather than an accidental externality.
