Competition (Konkurrenz) in Marx's analysis is the form in which the immanent laws of capital impose themselves on individual capitals. It is not the cause of capitalist dynamics but their mechanism of enforcement. In *Capital* Volume 3 and the *Grundrisse*, Marx distinguishes between competition as it appears to bourgeois economists â€” as the foundation of market efficiency and price determination â€” and competition as the phenomenal form of deeper structural imperatives: the equalization of profit rates, the compulsion to accumulate, and the tendency of the rate of profit to fall. Competition compels individual capitalists to adopt labor-saving technology, intensify exploitation, and expand production regardless of social need. It also mediates the redistribution of total surplus value among capitals of different organic compositions. Crucially, competition does not create the laws of capital but merely executes them, a distinction central to Marx's critique of classical political economy.
