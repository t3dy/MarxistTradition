Social formation (*Gesellschaftsformation*) designates a concrete, historically specific society understood as an articulation of multiple modes of production and their corresponding political and ideological structures. The concept was developed to address the complexity of real societies, which rarely conform to a single, pure mode of production. Althusser and Balibar gave the term theoretical precision in *Reading Capital*, arguing that every social formation is characterized by the dominance of one mode of production that structures the articulation of subordinate modes. This framework proved influential for analyzing colonial and postcolonial societies where capitalist, feudal, and communal relations coexist. Debates concern the relationship between the abstract concept of mode of production and the concrete analysis of social formations, the mechanisms by which one mode achieves dominance, and whether the concept adequately captures the unity and specificity of particular societies.
