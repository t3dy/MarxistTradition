Western Marxism designates a broad current of Marxist thought that developed in Western Europe from the 1920s onward, characterized by a turn from political economy and revolutionary strategy toward philosophy, aesthetics, and cultural criticism. Perry Anderson's *Considerations on Western Marxism* identified the tradition's key features: the defeat of revolutionary movements after 1923 drove Marxist intellectuals into academic settings, producing sophisticated theoretical work disconnected from working-class politics. Key figures include Lukacs, Korsch, Gramsci, the Frankfurt School, Sartre, and Althusser. Common themes include the reinterpretation of dialectics, the analysis of ideology and consciousness, the critique of instrumental reason, and engagement with non-Marxist philosophies including phenomenology, psychoanalysis, and structuralism. Debates concern whether Western Marxism represents a creative development of Marxist thought in response to changed conditions or an academic retreat from revolutionary commitment.
