The transitional program is a method of revolutionary political strategy developed by Trotsky in 1938 as the founding document of the Fourth International. It proposes a system of demands that originate in the immediate conditions and consciousness of the working class but lead logically toward the question of state power and the expropriation of capital. Unlike the classical social-democratic division between a "minimum program" of reforms achievable under capitalism and a "maximum program" of socialist revolution deferred to an indefinite future, transitional demands — the sliding scale of wages, workers' control of production, the opening of the books, the expropriation of key industries, the workers' militia — are formulated to be objectively necessary for workers' survival but structurally incompatible with the continued functioning of the capitalist system. Their purpose is pedagogical and organizational: through the struggle for transitional demands, workers confront the limits of capitalism and the necessity of revolutionary transformation. The method has been debated extensively within the Trotskyist tradition and beyond, with critics arguing that it risks either opportunist adaptation to reformism or sectarian detachment from actual working-class struggles.
