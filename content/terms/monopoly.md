Monopoly in Marxist theory refers to the concentration of market power in the hands of a few large capitals, arising from the inherent tendency toward centralisation described by Marx in *Capital* Volume 1. As competition drives smaller capitals out, surviving firms grow larger and more powerful, transforming the competitive dynamics of capitalism itself.

Lenin built on Hilferding and Hobson to theorise monopoly capitalism as the highest stage of capitalism, characterised by the dominance of finance capital, the division of the world among great powers, and the export of capital to colonial territories. Baran and Sweezy's *Monopoly Capital* (1966) argued that monopoly capitalism tends toward stagnation rather than crisis, as monopoly firms generate surplus that cannot be profitably reinvested. Debates continue over whether monopoly represents a distinct stage or a recurring tendency, and whether digital platform monopolies (Google, Amazon, Meta) constitute a new form of monopoly power.
