The organic composition of capital is the ratio of constant capital (means of production) to variable capital (labor-power), reflecting the technical conditions of production as expressed in value terms. Marx distinguishes the technical composition (the physical ratio of machinery and materials to workers), the value composition (the ratio expressed in value), and the organic composition, which captures the value composition insofar as it is determined by and reflects changes in the technical composition. As capitalist accumulation proceeds, competition drives individual capitalists to adopt labor-saving technologies, raising the organic composition across the economy — a greater share of total capital is invested in means of production relative to wages. This tendential rise in the organic composition is central to Marx's law of the tendency of the rate of profit to fall: since surplus-value is produced only by living labor (variable capital), a rising proportion of constant capital in the total means that surplus-value falls relative to total capital advanced, depressing the rate of profit even as the mass of profit may continue to grow.
