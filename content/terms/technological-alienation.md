Technological alienation describes the specific forms of estrangement that emerge when digital technologies and AI systems mediate the relationship between workers and their activity, products, and fellow workers. Marx's original analysis of alienation identified how capitalist production transforms workers' own activity and products into alien powers that dominate them; AI intensifies this dynamic by appropriating not merely physical labor but cognitive capacities — language, pattern recognition, decision-making, creativity — and returning them to workers as automated systems that direct, evaluate, and replace them. The knowledge that AI systems encode was produced collectively by human labor, but once captured as proprietary algorithms, it confronts its producers as capital's property.

The related concepts of 'cognitive alienation' and 'alienation from knowledge production' describe how AI transforms the relationship between workers and their own cognitive capacities. When AI systems can write, analyse, design, and create, workers experience their own intellectual capabilities as redundant — not because these capacities have diminished but because capital has developed the means to appropriate them in automated form. The commodification of knowledge through AI represents what Marx might have recognised as the real subsumption of intellectual labor: where formal subsumption merely brings existing cognitive work under capitalist control, real subsumption transforms the nature of cognitive work itself, restructuring it according to the requirements of algorithmic processing and data extraction.
