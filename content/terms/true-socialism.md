True socialism (*wahrer Sozialismus*) was a current in German intellectual life of the 1840s that translated French socialist ideas into the language of German philosophical idealism, particularly Hegelian and Feuerbachian humanism. Marx and Engels criticized true socialism extensively in *The German Ideology* and the *Communist Manifesto*, arguing that it stripped socialist ideas of their revolutionary class content and reduced them to abstract philosophical principles about human essence, love, and species-being. By detaching socialism from the material conditions and class struggles that produced it, true socialists like Karl Grun and Moses Hess rendered it into a sentimental, apolitical doctrine that could serve reactionary purposes. Marx and Engels argued that true socialism reflected the underdeveloped class conditions of Germany, where the bourgeois revolution had not yet occurred and the proletariat barely existed. The critique helped define the distinction between materialist and idealist approaches to social transformation.
