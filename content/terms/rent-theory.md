Marxist rent theory analyzes how landowners extract surplus value through their monopoly over a natural condition of production. Marx distinguished absolute rent, arising from landed property's ability to restrict capital's access to land, from differential rent, arising from variations in fertility or location. Ground rent represents a portion of surplus value redistributed from industrial capital to landowners, constituting a deduction from the total social surplus rather than a creation of new value. Marx used rent theory to critique Ricardo's conflation of rent with natural productivity differences while also challenging the Physiocratic notion that land itself generates value. Contemporary applications extend rent theory to intellectual property, financial rents, and platform monopolies where rentier extraction occurs without direct involvement in production. The concept of the rentier class designates those who live entirely from such appropriations.
