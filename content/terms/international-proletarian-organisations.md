International proletarian organisations are the transnational bodies through which workers have sought to coordinate struggle across national borders. The First International (1864-1876) united diverse tendencies under Marx's intellectual leadership. The Second International (1889-1916) organised mass socialist parties but collapsed when most sections supported their national bourgeoisies in World War I.

The Third International (Comintern, 1919-1943) was founded to advance world revolution but became increasingly subordinated to Soviet foreign policy under Stalin. The Fourth International (1938) was founded by Trotsky to preserve revolutionary Marxism against Stalinist degeneration. Subsequent efforts include various international socialist tendencies and networks. The history of these organisations reflects recurring tensions between national and international organisation, reformism and revolution, and party autonomy versus centralised coordination.
