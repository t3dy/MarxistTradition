Culture and ideology in Marxist theory concern the production, circulation, and contestation of ideas, representations, and practices that sustain or challenge capitalist social relations. Marx's foundational insight in *The German Ideology* â€” that the ruling ideas of any epoch are the ideas of the ruling class â€” was developed in divergent directions by subsequent theorists. Gramsci's concept of hegemony analyzed how consent is organized through cultural institutions. The Frankfurt School, particularly Adorno and Horkheimer, theorized the culture industry as a mechanism for the commodification of consciousness and the neutralization of critical thought. Althusser's concept of ideological state apparatuses emphasized the material practices through which subjects are interpellated. Stuart Hall and British cultural studies examined how media representations encode and negotiate class, race, and gender ideologies. Debord's theory of the spectacle analyzed the colonization of social life by images serving capital accumulation. These analyses converge on the recognition that ideology is not mere illusion but a material force embedded in institutions and everyday practices.
