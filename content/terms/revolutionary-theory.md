Revolutionary theory in the Marxist tradition denotes the systematic analysis of capitalist society aimed at identifying the conditions, agents, and strategies for its overthrow. Marx insisted on the unity of theory and practice, arguing that revolutionary theory is not an abstract blueprint but emerges from and guides concrete class struggles. The concept opposes both spontaneism, which dismisses the need for theoretical preparation, and scholasticism, which divorces theory from practical engagement. Lenin's contribution emphasized that without revolutionary theory there can be no revolutionary movement, positioning theory as essential for navigating complex political conjunctures. Key debates concern the relationship between objective conditions and subjective readiness, the role of intellectuals in producing theory, and whether revolutionary theory must be updated to address transformations in capitalism such as financialization, ecological crisis, and changes in the composition of the working class.
