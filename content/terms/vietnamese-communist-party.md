The Vietnamese Communist Party, founded by Ho Chi Minh in 1930, led one of the most consequential revolutionary movements of the twentieth century, directing the struggles for national liberation against French colonialism and American military intervention. The party applied Marxism-Leninism to the conditions of a colonized, predominantly peasant society, developing strategies that combined national liberation with social revolution. Its organizational capacity, rooted in disciplined cadre structures and mass mobilization, enabled it to wage sustained guerrilla and conventional warfare over decades. The party's theoretical contributions include adaptations of people's war doctrine, analysis of the relationship between anti-colonial and anti-capitalist struggle, and the subsequent Doi Moi economic reforms that introduced market mechanisms while maintaining single-party rule. Debates concern whether these market reforms represent a transition to capitalism, the party's authoritarian character, and the broader lessons of Vietnamese revolutionary experience for anti-imperialist movements.
