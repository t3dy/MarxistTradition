AI and imperialism describes how artificial intelligence reproduces and intensifies existing structures of global domination. The development and deployment of AI systems is concentrated overwhelmingly in the United States and China, with peripheral nations occupying subordinate positions as sources of training data, cheap computational labor, and markets for AI products developed elsewhere. Nick Couldry and Ulises Mejias's concept of 'digital colonialism' captures how this relationship reproduces the structure of historical colonialism: peripheral regions provide raw materials (data, labor) and receive finished products (AI systems, platforms) under terms set by metropolitan capital. The concept of 'AI and global labor arbitrage' describes how AI enables capital to decompose production processes and distribute tasks across global value chains, playing workers in different regions against each other to minimise costs.

The geopolitical competition between the United States and China over AI supremacy — expressed through semiconductor export controls, data localisation policies, and state investment in AI research — reveals the intersection of inter-imperialist rivalry with technological development that Lenin analysed in a previous era. The related concepts of 'techno-nationalism,' 'AI and state capitalism,' and 'AI and the military-industrial complex' describe how states deploy AI as an instrument of geopolitical power, blurring the line between commercial technology development and military capacity. The uneven development of AI capabilities across the world system does not merely reflect existing inequalities but actively reproduces them, as regions without the infrastructure, investment, and educational systems necessary for AI development become increasingly dependent on technology produced and controlled elsewhere.
