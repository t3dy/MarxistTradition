Imperialism theory encompasses Marxist analyses of how capitalist powers dominate peripheral regions through economic, political, and military means. Lenin's *Imperialism, the Highest Stage of Capitalism* identified monopoly capital, finance capital, and the export of capital as defining features. Luxemburg argued imperialism arose from capitalism's need for non-capitalist markets to realise surplus value.

Subsequent theorists developed diverse frameworks: dependency theory (Frank, Amin) analysed core-periphery exploitation and underdevelopment; world-systems theory (Wallerstein) introduced semi-periphery analysis; unequal exchange theory (Emmanuel, Cope) examined how wage differentials transfer value from South to North. Neo-colonialism describes how formal decolonisation left intact structures of economic domination. Contemporary debates address whether imperialism remains primarily nation-state driven or has been superseded by transnational capital and new forms of informal empire.
