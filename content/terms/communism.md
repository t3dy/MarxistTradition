Communism (Kommunismus), as theorized by Marx and Engels, designates not a utopian blueprint but the real movement that abolishes the present state of things. In *The German Ideology*, Marx defined communism as the practical supersession of private property and the division of labor, grounded in the material development of productive forces. The *Communist Manifesto* presents it as the self-conscious political movement of the proletariat toward the abolition of bourgeois property relations. Marx distinguished communism from earlier socialisms by grounding it in the actual contradictions of capitalist production rather than moral critique. In the *Critique of the Gotha Programme*, he outlined a lower and higher phase of communist society, the former still bearing marks of capitalist origin. Key debates concern the relationship between communism and the state, the role of planning versus spontaneous coordination, and whether communism requires abundance or a fundamental transformation of needs and social relations.
