The October Revolution of 1917 (25 October Old Style, 7 November New Style) saw the Bolshevik Party, led by Lenin and Trotsky, overthrow the Provisional Government and establish Soviet power in Russia. The revolution emerged from the crisis of World War I, the failure of the Provisional Government to address demands for peace, land, and bread, and the radicalisation of workers and soldiers expressed through the soviets.

The revolution established the world's first workers' state, inspiring revolutionary movements globally and fundamentally reshaping twentieth-century politics. It generated enduring debates: whether October was a genuine proletarian revolution or a coup; whether socialism could be built in a backward country without international revolution; and how to account for the subsequent Stalinist degeneration. The revolution remains the central reference point for Marxist debates about revolutionary strategy, the party, the state, and the transition to socialism.
