The Chinese Revolution of 1949 culminated in the CCP's military victory over the Kuomintang and the establishment of the People's Republic. It was the largest social revolution of the twentieth century, transforming a semicolonial, semifeudal society and fundamentally reshaping global politics. Marxist analysis of the revolution centers on several contested questions: the class character of the revolution (proletarian, peasant, or bourgeois-democratic), the applicability of the Leninist model to agrarian societies, and the significance of Mao's strategic innovations including the encirclement of cities from the countryside. Trotskyist critics argued that the revolution confirmed the theory of permanent revolution, as democratic tasks could only be completed under proletarian leadership, while Maoists maintained that China's specific conditions necessitated a distinct revolutionary path. The revolution also intensified debates about imperialism, national liberation, and the world-historical role of the colonial and semicolonial periphery.
