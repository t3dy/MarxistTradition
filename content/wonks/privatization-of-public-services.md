Privatisation of public services — water, energy, transportation, healthcare, education, prisons, waste management — has been a central neoliberal policy since the 1980s, justified by claims of efficiency, innovation, and consumer choice. Harvey identifies privatisation as a key mechanism of accumulation by dispossession: the transfer of publicly owned assets and services to private capital for profit extraction.

The record of privatisation contradicts its promises. Privatised water systems (from Bolivia to Britain) raise prices and reduce investment in infrastructure. Private prisons create incentives for mass incarceration. Outsourced public services reduce workers' wages and benefits while generating management fees and profits. The efficiency argument ignores that private firms must generate profit margins on top of service costs, and that the 'discipline of the market' typically means cutting labour costs rather than improving services. Privatisation also reduces democratic accountability: public services answerable to voters become private operations answerable to shareholders. The COVID-19 pandemic exposed the consequences of decades of healthcare privatisation across the Global North.
