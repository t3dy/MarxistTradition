Real estate tax incentives — including tax increment financing, opportunity zones, 1031 exchanges, mortgage interest deductions, and property tax abatements — constitute a vast system of public subsidy for private property development. These incentives are justified as promoting development, job creation, and neighbourhood revitalisation, but systematically direct public resources toward speculative profits.

Opportunity Zones, created by the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, offer capital gains tax deferral for investments in designated low-income areas. In practice, they have funded luxury apartments, self-storage facilities, and stadium developments rather than serving existing communities. Tax increment financing captures increases in property tax revenue to fund further development, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of gentrification. The mortgage interest deduction costs the US Treasury over $30 billion annually while disproportionately benefiting wealthy homeowners. These mechanisms reveal how the state actively produces urban inequality through fiscal policy, subsidising the class that owns property at the expense of those who rent.
