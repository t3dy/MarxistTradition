Startup culture is among the most potent ideological formations of contemporary capitalism. The myth of the visionary founder who creates value through innovation and risk-taking performs a crucial function: it obscures the actual source of value in labor and presents capital accumulation as the product of individual genius. In reality, most startups are vehicles for venture capital investment, structured to extract maximum surplus through intensive labor, equity-based compensation that defers wages, and eventual acquisition or IPO that enriches investors while often discarding the workforce.

The entrepreneurial ideology extends Marx's concept of commodity fetishism into the domain of labor relations. Workers in startups are encouraged to see themselves not as employees selling labor-power but as co-creators sharing in a mission. Stock options substitute for wages, long hours are reframed as passion, and precarious employment becomes a lifestyle choice. This is the real subsumption of labor under capital at its most complete -- workers internalize the imperatives of accumulation as their own desires, becoming self-exploiting subjects who need no external discipline.

The structural function of startup ideology within the broader political economy is to legitimate the venture capital model of accumulation. Public research funding generates foundational technologies, startup labor develops commercial applications, and venture capital extracts the surplus through financial engineering. The entrepreneur is not the protagonist of this story but its ideological mask, personalizing and heroizing a process of class exploitation that operates through impersonal structures of finance capital.
