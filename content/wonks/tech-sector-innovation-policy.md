Tech sector innovation policy embodies a fundamental contradiction that Marxist analysis makes visible: the state socializes the risks and costs of research while private capital monopolizes the returns. The internet, GPS, touchscreen technology, and foundational AI research all emerged from publicly funded programs, yet the profits flow to a handful of corporations that exercise monopoly control over the resulting platforms. This is not market innovation but state-subsidized rent extraction dressed in the language of entrepreneurship.

The concentration of digital capital follows the tendencies Marx identified in the general law of capitalist accumulation. Network effects, data monopolies, and intellectual property regimes produce winner-take-all dynamics that make competition a temporary stage on the way to monopoly. Tech giants like Google, Amazon, and Meta function less as innovative firms than as toll collectors on the infrastructure of social communication and exchange, extracting rents from every transaction that passes through their platforms.

Innovation policy discourse obscures these class dynamics by presenting technology as a neutral force that benefits everyone. In reality, the gains from digital technology are distributed according to existing class relations: productivity increases accrue to capital through intensified labor surveillance and deskilling, while workers face algorithmic management and precarious employment. A Marxist technology policy would demand public ownership of digital infrastructure, democratic governance of data, and the socialization of returns from publicly funded research.
