Municipal budget constraints — balanced budget requirements, state-imposed spending caps, tax limitation measures, and debt restrictions — systematically limit local governments' capacity to fund public services. These constraints are presented as fiscal prudence but operate as mechanisms of class discipline, forcing cities to choose between cutting services and subsidising capital.

The ideology of balanced budgets obscures how municipal fiscal crises are produced: property tax caps (like California's Proposition 13) starve local governments of revenue while protecting wealthy homeowners; tax increment financing diverts public funds to developers; and the competitive pressure to offer corporate incentives erodes the tax base further. When fiscal crisis arrives, austerity falls on the working class through cuts to schools, transit, public safety, and social services — never on developer subsidies or corporate tax breaks. Detroit's bankruptcy, Flint's water crisis, and the chronic underfunding of urban school systems demonstrate how municipal fiscal constraints reproduce class and racial inequality through the built environment.
