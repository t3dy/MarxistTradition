Inflation policy debates reveal sharp class antagonisms beneath technical economic language. The dominant framework holds that inflation is caused by excess demand or wage-price spirals, justifying interest rate increases that suppress employment. The concept of NAIRU (Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment) explicitly theorises a minimum level of unemployment necessary for price stability — effectively mandating worker insecurity as economic policy.

Marxist analysis identifies inflation as a site of class struggle over the distribution of the social product. The 2021-2023 inflation episode demonstrated how corporate profit margins expanded as firms raised prices beyond input cost increases — 'greedflation' or sellers' inflation. Yet the policy response was interest rate hikes targeting workers' purchasing power rather than price controls targeting corporate pricing power. Kalecki's analysis remains relevant: capitalists prefer unemployment to full employment because tight labour markets strengthen workers' bargaining position. Anti-inflation policy is class war conducted through monetary technocracy.
