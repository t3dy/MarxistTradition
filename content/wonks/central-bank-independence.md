Central bank independence — the institutional separation of monetary policy from elected government — is presented as a technical necessity for sound economic management. The doctrine, dominant since the 1980s, holds that independent central banks can credibly commit to price stability, reducing inflationary expectations and creating conditions for growth.

Marxist analysis reveals this as class politics disguised as technocracy. Central bank independence shields monetary policy from democratic demands for full employment, higher wages, and public investment. Interest rate decisions directly affect the distribution of income between creditors and debtors, capital and labour. The Federal Reserve, ECB, and Bank of England consistently prioritise inflation control (protecting the value of financial assets) over employment (protecting workers). The 2008 financial crisis exposed central banks as willing to deploy unlimited resources to rescue finance capital while imposing austerity on the working class — a class bias built into their institutional mandate.
