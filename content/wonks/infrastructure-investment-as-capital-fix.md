Infrastructure investment is celebrated across the political spectrum as a win-win proposition, but Marxist analysis reveals it as a mechanism for managing capitalism's contradictions rather than resolving them. Harvey's concept of the spatial fix explains how surplus capital that cannot find profitable outlets in production is absorbed into the built environment -- highways, bridges, broadband networks, energy grids -- temporarily resolving overaccumulation crises while creating the physical conditions for the next round of accumulation. Infrastructure spending is not neutral stimulus; it is a class-determined allocation of social resources.

The question of who captures the value created by public infrastructure investment is systematically obscured by bipartisan enthusiasm for building. When the state constructs a highway, the immediate beneficiaries include construction firms, real estate developers whose land values increase, and corporations whose logistics costs decline. Workers benefit indirectly through employment and improved commutes, but the long-term value accrues to capital through enhanced conditions of exploitation and circulation. Public investment socializes costs while privatizing returns.

From a Marxist perspective, the critical question is not whether to build infrastructure but under what social relations. Infrastructure built for capital circulation -- highways serving suburban sprawl, data networks enabling platform monopolies, energy systems powering extractive industry -- reproduces the conditions of exploitation. Infrastructure built for social need -- public housing, mass transit, community energy systems under democratic control -- points toward an alternative logic. The form of investment reveals the class character of the state.
