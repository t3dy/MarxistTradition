Zoning reform proposals typically proceed from a supply-and-demand logic: remove regulatory barriers, allow denser construction, and prices will fall. But Marx's theory of ground rent reveals why this logic is insufficient. Land is a monopoly good -- its supply cannot be increased, and its value derives not from the labor of the landowner but from social development, infrastructure investment, and locational advantage. When zoning is liberalized, the increased development potential is capitalized into land prices, generating windfall gains for existing owners while raising the floor cost of new construction.

The rent gap theory developed by Neil Smith demonstrates how upzoning creates speculative opportunities. The difference between current land use and the potential rent under higher-density zoning attracts investment capital, drives displacement of existing tenants, and produces gentrification. Zoning reform without public land ownership or rent capture mechanisms simply transfers value from one class of property owners to another -- from incumbent homeowners to developers and financial investors -- without fundamentally altering the affordability crisis.

A Marxist analysis of zoning and land value insists that the problem is not regulatory but structural: land under capitalism functions as fictitious capital, a claim on future ground rent that is bought and sold speculatively. No reform of land-use regulations can overcome this dynamic so long as land remains private property. The socialist alternative -- public ownership of land, community land trusts, and socialized housing development -- addresses the root of the housing crisis by removing land from the commodity circuit entirely.
