Financial deregulation — the removal of restrictions on banking, securities, and capital flows — has been a defining feature of neoliberal capitalism since the 1970s. Key milestones include the end of Bretton Woods capital controls, the Big Bang deregulation of the City of London (1986), and the repeal of Glass-Steagall in the US (1999).

Marx's analysis of fictitious capital in *Capital* Volume 3 anticipated how the financial sphere develops a logic of its own, generating speculative bubbles that eventually collapse. Deregulation intensified this tendency, enabling the proliferation of derivatives, securitisation, shadow banking, and leveraged speculation that culminated in the 2008 financial crisis. The crisis demonstrated that financial deregulation socialises risk while privatising profit: banks received public bailouts while millions lost homes, jobs, and savings. Post-crisis re-regulation has been minimal, as the structural power of finance capital over the state ensures that meaningful reform remains politically constrained.
