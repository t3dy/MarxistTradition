The debate between the Green New Deal and market-based climate policy (carbon taxes, cap-and-trade) represents a fundamental strategic divide. Carbon pricing, favoured by mainstream economists, proposes to internalise environmental costs through market mechanisms. The Green New Deal, associated with Ocasio-Cortez and the Sunrise Movement, proposes massive public investment in renewable energy, infrastructure, and a just transition for workers in fossil fuel industries.

Marxist analysis identifies the limits of both approaches while recognising the Green New Deal as the more progressive terrain. Carbon pricing treats climate change as a market failure rather than a structural feature of capitalism's growth imperative, and evidence from existing carbon markets shows they have failed to reduce emissions meaningfully. The Green New Deal's emphasis on public investment, planning, and labour rights challenges the neoliberal orthodoxy but stops short of confronting the profit motive that drives ecological destruction. A Marxist ecological politics must go further, questioning whether infinite accumulation on a finite planet is compatible with any form of capitalism.
