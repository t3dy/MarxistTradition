American suburbanisation was not a natural outcome of consumer preference but a state-engineered spatial transformation driven by the needs of capital accumulation. Federal highway construction ($500 billion since 1956), FHA mortgage guarantees, tax deductions for mortgage interest, and racially discriminatory redlining policies created the conditions for white middle-class flight from cities, generating enormous profits for construction, automobile, oil, and consumer goods industries.

Harvey's analysis of the spatial fix illuminates how suburbanisation absorbed the surplus capital of the post-war boom: the built environment of highways, subdivisions, and shopping malls constituted a massive circuit of fixed capital investment. Simultaneously, suburbanisation fragmented the urban working class along racial lines, undermining the political solidarity that had produced the New Deal coalition. Contemporary consequences include car dependency, environmental degradation, the fiscal crisis of both suburbs and abandoned urban cores, and the extreme spatial segregation by race and class that structures American metropolitan life. The housing crisis, sprawl, and transportation inequality are not policy failures — they are the system working as designed.
