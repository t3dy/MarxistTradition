Automation policy debates concern how governments should respond to the displacement of human labour by machines, robots, and AI systems. Mainstream discussions focus on retraining programmes, education reform, and social safety nets, while more radical proposals include universal basic income, robot taxes, and reduced working hours.

Marx's analysis of machinery in *Capital* provides the foundational framework: under capitalism, automation increases relative surplus value by reducing the value of labour-power, but it also displaces workers into the reserve army of labour and tends to lower the rate of profit by raising the organic composition of capital. The 'Fragment on Machines' in the *Grundrisse* anticipates a contradiction in which automated production makes direct labour increasingly marginal to wealth creation, potentially undermining value production itself. Policy debates that treat automation as a technical problem requiring adjustment miss its character as a class strategy for disciplining labour and restoring profitability.
