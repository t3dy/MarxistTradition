Workforce development programmes — job training, career pathways, apprenticeships, coding bootcamps, community college partnerships — are the standard policy response to unemployment, deindustrialisation, and technological displacement. The underlying premise is that unemployment results from a 'skills gap' between workers' qualifications and employers' needs.

Marxist analysis challenges this premise fundamentally. The reserve army of labour is not a skills problem but a structural feature of capitalism: capital requires unemployment to discipline workers and suppress wages. Retraining programmes shift responsibility for joblessness from the system to the individual worker, who is told to 'upskill' or 'reskill' rather than organise collectively. The evidence supports the Marxist critique: retraining programmes consistently show poor outcomes because they address supply (workers' skills) while ignoring demand (whether jobs exist and at what wages). Meanwhile, employers demand public funding for training that serves their specific needs, socialising the cost of labour-power reproduction while capturing the productivity gains.
