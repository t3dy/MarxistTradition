Tax write-offs and subsidised capital investment — including accelerated depreciation, bonus depreciation, investment tax credits, and section 179 expensing — allow businesses to deduct the cost of capital purchases from taxable income, often at rates far exceeding actual economic depreciation. The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act allowed 100% immediate expensing of capital investments, effectively eliminating corporate tax on invested income.

These provisions constitute a massive transfer of revenue from the public to capital. The justification — that tax incentives encourage productive investment — is contradicted by evidence that much of the tax savings flows to stock buybacks and dividends rather than new productive capacity. The system also favours capital-intensive over labour-intensive production, incentivising automation and the replacement of workers with machines. For Marxists, accelerated depreciation reveals the state's structural role in maintaining profitability: by reducing the cost of constant capital through tax expenditures, the state counteracts the rising organic composition of capital at public expense.
