The ideology of 'policy pragmatism' — the claim that good policy is about 'what works' rather than ideology — is itself a powerful ideological formation. By presenting capitalist social relations as the unquestioned framework within which policy operates, pragmatism forecloses the possibility of structural transformation and reduces politics to technocratic management.

Marxist ideology critique reveals that pragmatism is not the absence of ideology but its most effective form. When policymakers claim to be 'evidence-based' and 'non-ideological,' they naturalise the entire edifice of capitalist property relations, market mechanisms, and class power as simple reality. The 'Overton window' of acceptable policy options is itself ideologically determined: single-payer healthcare is 'unrealistic,' while trillion-dollar bank bailouts are 'necessary.' The demand to be 'pragmatic' functions to delegitimise systemic critique and channel political energy into marginal reforms that leave fundamental power relations intact. As Marx showed, the ideas of the ruling class are the ruling ideas — and 'pragmatism' is the form this takes when explicit ideological justification has lost credibility.
