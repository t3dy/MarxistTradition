Opportunity Zones represent a remarkably transparent form of state-facilitated accumulation by dispossession. The program offers capital gains tax deferrals and exclusions to investors who channel funds into designated low-income census tracts, ostensibly to promote development in underserved communities. In practice, investment overwhelmingly flows to areas already undergoing gentrification -- luxury apartments, self-storage facilities, and commercial developments that serve incoming affluent residents rather than existing community needs.

The class logic is unambiguous: the state identifies working-class neighborhoods where the rent gap between current use and potential use is widest, then subsidizes wealthy investors to exploit that gap. Capital gains tax benefits ensure that the profits from displacement accrue to investors tax-free, while the communities being "developed" face rising rents, eviction, and the destruction of existing social networks. This is primitive accumulation operating through fiscal policy -- the enclosure of urban space for capital through the mechanism of the tax code.

What Opportunity Zones reveal about the capitalist state is that poverty itself becomes an investment opportunity. The areas designated as Opportunity Zones are poor precisely because of decades of disinvestment, redlining, and deindustrialization -- historical processes of capital withdrawal. Now capital returns not to remedy these conditions but to profit from them, buying low in abandoned neighborhoods and selling high once gentrification is complete. The cycle of disinvestment and reinvestment is a feature of capitalist urbanization, not a bug that tax incentives can fix.
