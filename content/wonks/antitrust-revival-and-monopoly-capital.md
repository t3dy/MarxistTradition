The antitrust revival, associated with the 'neo-Brandeisian' movement led by figures like Lina Khan and Tim Wu, seeks to revive competition policy against corporate concentration, particularly in the tech sector. While Marxists share concern about monopoly power, they offer a fundamentally different diagnosis.

Marx demonstrated that competition inherently tends toward monopoly through the concentration and centralisation of capital. Lenin's theory of imperialism identified monopoly as capitalism's mature form. From this perspective, antitrust policy is structurally limited: breaking up monopolies through regulation treats a symptom while leaving intact the dynamics of accumulation that regenerate concentration. The Monthly Review school (Baran, Sweezy) argued that monopoly capitalism tends toward stagnation and waste, problems no amount of antitrust enforcement can resolve. The revival nonetheless opens space for class-conscious demands around public ownership and democratic control of concentrated industries.
