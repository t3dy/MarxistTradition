Pension fund capitalism refers to the system in which workers’ retirement savings — trillions of dollars globally — are invested in financial markets, making pension funds among the largest institutional investors. This creates a structural contradiction: workers’ retirement security depends on the profitability of the very system that exploits them.

Pension funds invest in stocks, bonds, real estate, and private equity, generating returns through the extraction of surplus value from other workers. Fund managers pursue maximum returns regardless of social consequences: pension capital funds union-busting companies, gentrification-driving real estate, and environmentally destructive industries. The shift from defined-benefit pensions (employer-guaranteed retirement income) to defined-contribution plans (individual investment accounts) has transferred all financial risk to workers while enriching asset management firms through fees. The 2008 crisis devastated workers’ retirement savings, revealing that pension fund capitalism makes the working class bear the costs of the financial system’s crises.
