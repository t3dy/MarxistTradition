The YIMBY movement frames the housing crisis as fundamentally a supply problem solvable through deregulation, but a Marxist analysis reveals deeper structural dynamics. As Engels argued in *The Housing Question*, the housing shortage is not an accident but a necessary feature of capitalism, in which land functions as a basis for rent extraction and housing is produced as a commodity for profit rather than for use. Simply building more units within capitalist property relations does not guarantee affordability -- it guarantees new opportunities for capital accumulation in the built environment.

YIMBY urbanism treats the price mechanism as a neutral allocator while ignoring that land markets are shaped by monopoly ground rent, speculative finance, and class power. Developers respond to effective demand, not social need, which is why market-rate construction overwhelmingly serves the upper segments of the housing market. The filtering theory that YIMBYs invoke -- that new luxury construction eventually makes older housing affordable -- operates on timescales that leave working-class tenants facing displacement in the present.

The deeper ideological function of YIMBY discourse is to redirect anger about housing costs away from the commodity form of housing itself and toward regulatory barriers. This conveniently aligns the interests of development capital with a progressive veneer, obscuring the class antagonism between landlords and tenants that structures the housing market. A genuinely socialist housing politics would demand public ownership, decommodification, and democratic planning -- not simply deregulation for developers.
