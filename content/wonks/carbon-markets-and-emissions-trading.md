Carbon markets represent the most audacious extension of the commodity form in recent history: the transformation of the atmosphere into a tradeable asset. From a Marxist perspective, emissions trading schemes do not solve the ecological crisis but financialize it, creating a new fictitious commodity -- the carbon credit -- that can be speculated upon, securitized, and traded in derivative markets. The pollution permit becomes a form of rent, extracted not from productive activity but from the state-granted right to degrade the commons.

The ideological brilliance of carbon markets lies in their claim that capitalism can solve the problems it creates through its own mechanisms. Yet the record of existing cap-and-trade systems reveals systematic failure: prices too low to incentivize genuine decarbonization, offsets that count forest preservation in the Global South as permission for continued fossil fuel extraction in the North, and carbon trading desks at investment banks generating profit from the very instruments meant to constrain emissions. The market does not internalize externalities -- it creates new opportunities for accumulation from crisis.

A Marxist climate politics recognizes that the atmosphere cannot be subjected to the value form without reproducing the contradictions that caused the crisis. What is required is democratic planning of production, direct limits on fossil fuel extraction, and public ownership of energy systems -- measures that subordinate the logic of accumulation to ecological and social need rather than inviting capital to profit from planetary destruction.
