Corporate tax cuts have been a central element of neoliberal fiscal policy since the 1980s, justified by supply-side arguments that lower taxes encourage investment, job creation, and growth that ultimately benefit all. The US corporate rate fell from 46% in 1986 to 21% in 2017 under the Trump tax reform, while the global average has declined from 40% to 23%.

Marxist analysis identifies corporate tax cuts as a straightforward mechanism of class redistribution. The 'trickle-down' justification — that profits retained by corporations will be invested productively — is contradicted by the evidence: corporations used the 2017 tax windfall primarily for stock buybacks and dividends, enriching shareholders while wages stagnated. Tax cuts erode the fiscal capacity for public services, education, and infrastructure that serve the working class, effectively socialising the costs of reproduction while privatising the returns to capital. International tax competition further constrains progressive fiscal policy as jurisdictions compete to attract mobile capital.
