The electric vehicle revolution is the paradigmatic case of green capitalism: a strategy that addresses ecological crisis through commodity substitution rather than systemic transformation. From a Marxist standpoint, replacing internal combustion engines with batteries does not alter the fundamental logic of capital accumulation driving environmental destruction. It merely opens new commodity frontiers -- lithium mining in Bolivia, cobalt extraction in the Congo, rare earth processing in China -- each carrying its own burden of labor exploitation and ecological devastation.

The EV transition preserves the privatized, automobile-centered transportation system that serves capital's need for dispersed, individualized consumption. It requires massive state subsidies that flow to corporations like Tesla while public transit remains starved of investment. The class character of this policy is evident: EV tax credits disproportionately benefit affluent consumers, while the working class continues to depend on deteriorating buses and trains. The supposed democratization of green technology follows the familiar pattern of socialized costs and privatized benefits.

What Marxist ecology demands is not a greener commodity but a fundamentally different relationship between production and nature. The metabolic rift between capitalist production and ecological sustainability cannot be closed by swapping one energy source for another within the same growth-dependent mode of production. Genuine decarbonization requires reduced private automobile use, expanded public transit, reorganized urban space, and democratic control over energy systems -- transformations incompatible with the imperatives of capital.
