Supply chain resilience policy emerged as a major policy concern after COVID-19 disruptions, the Suez Canal blockage, and semiconductor shortages exposed the fragility of just-in-time global production networks. Governments now pursue reshoring, friend-shoring, and strategic stockpiling to reduce dependence on geopolitically rival suppliers.

Marxist analysis identifies the deeper contradictions. Global supply chains were constructed to exploit international wage differentials and regulatory arbitrage — they are instruments of surplus value extraction, not neutral logistics. 'Resilience' policy is shaped by inter-imperialist competition: the US CHIPS Act and EU Critical Raw Materials Act are as much about containing China as about supply security. Reshoring under capitalism will not improve working conditions without strong labour organisation; it will simply relocate exploitation to domestic zones of low wages and weak regulation. The fundamental tension between capital's need for cost minimisation (lean supply chains) and system stability (redundant supply chains) cannot be resolved within a profit-driven framework.
