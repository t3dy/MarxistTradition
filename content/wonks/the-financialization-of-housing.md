The financialization of housing represents the most complete subordination of a basic human need to the logic of capital accumulation. When private equity firms acquire tens of thousands of single-family homes, when pension funds invest in real estate investment trusts, and when mortgage-backed securities circulate through global financial markets, housing ceases to function primarily as shelter and becomes a financial asset -- a bearer of fictitious capital whose value derives from expected future rent extraction.

This process follows the dynamics Marx analyzed in Volume III of *Capital*: the tendency for money capital to seek returns through financial claims rather than productive investment. Housing financialization accelerates the separation of ownership from use, concentrating property in the hands of institutional investors while tenants face rising rents, deteriorating maintenance, and the constant threat of eviction. The 2008 crisis demonstrated the catastrophic instability inherent in this model, as speculative mortgage lending produced a global financial collapse that destroyed millions of working-class households' primary store of wealth.

The policy response to financialized housing consistently protects asset values over tenants. Central bank interventions to prop up housing prices, tax advantages for real estate investment, and regulatory frameworks that favor institutional landlords all reflect the structural dominance of finance capital over the state. A Marxist approach demands the definancialization of housing through public ownership, strict rent controls, and the prohibition of housing as a speculative asset -- measures that directly challenge the prerogatives of capital.
