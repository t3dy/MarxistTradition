Capital gains tax policy — the taxation of profits from the sale of assets such as stocks, bonds, and real estate — is a central site of class struggle conducted through fiscal policy. In most capitalist countries, capital gains are taxed at lower rates than ordinary income from wages, a preference that overwhelmingly benefits the wealthy since the top percentiles hold the vast majority of financial assets.

Marxist analysis reveals this preference as a direct expression of class power embedded in the tax code. The justification that lower capital gains taxes 'encourage investment' treats capital as inherently productive, obscuring that investment returns derive from surplus value extracted from labour. Warren Buffett's observation that he pays a lower tax rate than his secretary illustrates how the system operates. Progressive reform proposals — equalising capital gains and income rates, taxing unrealised gains, or implementing wealth taxes — face structural resistance from a capitalist state dependent on private investment for economic activity.
