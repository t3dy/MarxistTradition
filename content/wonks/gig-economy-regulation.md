Gig economy regulation concerns the legal status and labour protections of workers on platforms like Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, and Instacart. The central question — whether gig workers are employees entitled to benefits and protections, or independent contractors responsible for their own costs — has enormous implications for millions of workers.

Marxist analysis cuts through the legal formalism: regardless of classification, gig workers sell their labour-power to platform companies that extract surplus value through algorithmic management. The 'independent contractor' designation is a legal fiction that allows platforms to avoid minimum wage laws, unemployment insurance, health benefits, and workers' compensation while maintaining effective control over the labour process through app-based surveillance and dynamic pricing. California's Proposition 22, where Uber and Lyft spent $200 million to exempt themselves from labour law, demonstrates how capital deploys political power to maintain exploitative arrangements. The gig economy represents not a new form of work but the intensification of capitalism's oldest tendency: to maximise exploitation by minimising the cost of labour-power.
