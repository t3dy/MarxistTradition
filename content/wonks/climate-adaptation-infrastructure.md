Climate adaptation infrastructure — seawalls, flood barriers, resilient power grids, cooling centres, water management systems — raises fundamental questions about who is protected and who is abandoned as climate change intensifies. Mainstream policy treats adaptation as a technical planning challenge, but Marxist analysis reveals it as a site of class and racial struggle.

Adaptation investment follows the logic of capital: high-value real estate in Manhattan gets a $335 million seawall while low-income communities in the Global South receive nothing. Insurance markets withdraw from vulnerable areas, accelerating disinvestment in working-class communities. Harvey's concept of the spatial fix illuminates how adaptation infrastructure can serve accumulation — construction contracts, land value increases, and gentrification — rather than genuine community protection. The fundamental question is whether adaptation serves to protect existing concentrations of wealth and power or becomes a vehicle for redistributive transformation.
