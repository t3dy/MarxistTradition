Pharmaceutical pricing and patent policy exemplify the contradiction between capitalism's profit motive and human need. Drug companies charge monopoly prices for medicines often developed with public research funding: the NIH invested billions in the basic research behind COVID-19 vaccines, yet Pfizer and Moderna captured the profits. Insulin, discovered a century ago, costs hundreds of dollars per vial in the US due to patent evergreening and market manipulation.

The TRIPS agreement globalised pharmaceutical patent protections, restricting generic competition and denying affordable medicines to billions. During the COVID-19 pandemic, wealthy nations hoarded vaccines while the Global South was denied access, demonstrating how intellectual property regimes produce what has been called 'vaccine apartheid.' Marxist analysis identifies pharmaceutical monopolies as a form of rent extraction from the sick: the marginal cost of producing most drugs is minimal, and monopoly pricing represents pure surplus transfer. Public pharmaceutical manufacturing, compulsory licensing, and the abolition of drug patents are the logical policy conclusions.
