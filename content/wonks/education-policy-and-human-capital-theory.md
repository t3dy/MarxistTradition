Human capital theory, developed by Becker and Schultz in the 1960s, treats education as an investment in individual productivity, justifying market-oriented education reforms including standardised testing, school choice, charter schools, student debt financing, and the defunding of public universities. The framework reduces education to a factor of production whose value is measured by future earnings.

Marxist critique reveals what human capital theory conceals: education under capitalism primarily serves to reproduce class relations. Bowles and Gintis demonstrated the 'correspondence principle' — schools mirror the hierarchical social relations of capitalist production, training students in obedience and punctuality rather than critical thinking. Althusser identified education as the dominant ideological state apparatus of mature capitalism. The student debt crisis, which has reached $1.7 trillion in the US, demonstrates how human capital theory operates in practice: students are compelled to financialise their own futures, assuming debt on the premise that education is an investment, while structural unemployment and precarious work render the promised returns increasingly fictitious.
