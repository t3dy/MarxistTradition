Density has become a rallying cry for urbanist policy wonks who present compact development as simultaneously efficient, sustainable, and equitable. But a Marxist analysis reveals that the politics of density cannot be separated from the class relations of urban land. Under capitalism, densification primarily serves to intensify ground rent extraction -- packing more rent-paying units onto each parcel of land, increasing the total surplus captured by landowners and developers. The class geography of density is stark: luxury towers for the affluent and overcrowded tenements for the poor are both expressions of capital's spatial logic.

The urbanist celebration of density abstracts from the question of who builds, who owns, and who profits. When density is produced through market mechanisms, it follows effective demand rather than social need, clustering amenities in high-income neighborhoods while concentrating poverty and environmental burdens in others. The displacement that accompanies market-driven densification -- rising rents, evictions, demolition of affordable housing -- is not an unfortunate side effect but a structural feature of capital-driven urban development.

A Marxist approach to urban form distinguishes between density as a tool of capital accumulation and density as a feature of democratically planned communities. Socialist urbanism has historically produced dense, livable neighborhoods with public amenities, affordable housing, and collective spaces. The difference lies not in the built form but in the social relations of production: whether density serves rent extraction or collective life depends entirely on whether housing is organized as a commodity or as a social right.
