Trade agreements — from NAFTA/USMCA to the WTO framework, TPP, and bilateral investment treaties — are presented as mutual benefit arrangements that promote growth through comparative advantage. Marxist analysis reveals them as instruments of imperial economic power.

Marx critiqued free trade ideology in his 1848 Brussels speech, arguing that free trade accelerates capitalism's destructive tendencies and thereby hastens revolution. Contemporary trade agreements go far beyond tariff reduction: they enforce intellectual property monopolies (TRIPS), restrict state-owned enterprises, protect investor rights against democratic regulation (ISDS provisions), and prohibit capital controls. The result is a global legal architecture that locks in the prerogatives of transnational capital while constraining the policy space of developing nations. NAFTA's devastation of Mexican agriculture through subsidised US corn imports exemplifies how 'free trade' between unequal economies transfers wealth from periphery to core. Labour and environmental provisions remain weak and largely unenforceable.
