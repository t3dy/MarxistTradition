Intellectual property regimes — patents, copyrights, trademarks, and trade secrets — create legally enforced monopolies over knowledge, information, and cultural production. The WTO's TRIPS agreement globalised strong IP protections, imposing Western IP norms on developing countries and restricting access to medicines, seeds, and technology.

Marxist analysis identifies IP as a form of monopoly rent extraction. Knowledge is inherently non-rivalrous — sharing it does not diminish it — making artificial scarcity through legal enclosure a pure exercise of class power. Pharmaceutical patents exemplify the contradiction: drugs developed with public research funding are monopoly-priced by private firms, generating enormous profits while millions lack access to essential medicines. The COVID-19 vaccine patent debates brought this into sharp relief. Digital commons movements, open-source software, and campaigns for patent pools represent resistance to knowledge enclosure. The fundamental question is whether knowledge should be treated as a commodity generating private profit or a commons serving human needs.
