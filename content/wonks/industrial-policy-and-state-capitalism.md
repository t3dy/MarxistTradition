Industrial policy — state intervention to promote strategic industries through subsidies, tariffs, procurement, and regulation — has returned to prominence after decades of neoliberal orthodoxy that insisted markets alone should allocate investment. The CHIPS Act, Inflation Reduction Act, and European Green Deal represent billions in public subsidies for semiconductor manufacturing, clean energy, and critical minerals.

Marxist analysis interrogates who benefits from industrial policy. Historically, every successful industrialisation — Britain, the US, Germany, Japan, South Korea, China — relied on extensive state intervention, contradicting free-market mythology. But under capitalism, industrial policy socialises investment risk while privatising returns: TSMC receives $6.6 billion in CHIPS Act subsidies while retaining all profits. The strategic competition framing — US versus China — channels class-relevant questions about ownership and control into nationalist narratives. A socialist industrial policy would combine public investment with public ownership, directing production toward social needs rather than private accumulation.
