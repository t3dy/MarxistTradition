The neoliberal shift from public housing construction to housing vouchers represents a fundamental transformation in the state's relationship to the commodity form of shelter. Public housing, for all its historical failures of underfunding and racialized management, represented a partial decommodification of housing -- units provided based on need, owned publicly, and removed from the speculative market. Vouchers, by contrast, channel public funds directly to private landlords, subsidizing the rental market while leaving the commodity form of housing intact.

The class logic of voucher programs becomes clear when we follow the money. Public housing expenditure builds durable public assets that can serve working-class communities for generations. Voucher payments flow to landlords as rent -- a form of surplus extraction that enriches property owners without creating any lasting public benefit. The landlord captures the subsidy as ground rent, and the tenant remains dependent on both the state and the market. The deliberate destruction of public housing stock in cities like Chicago and St. Louis was an act of primitive accumulation, converting public assets into private real estate opportunities.

A Marxist housing politics rejects the false choice between inadequate vouchers and deliberately degraded public housing. The historical underfunding of public housing was a political decision, not an economic inevitability. The alternative is massive public investment in permanently affordable, democratically managed, decommodified housing -- removing shelter from the circuit of capital and treating it as the social right it must become.
