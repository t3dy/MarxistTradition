Municipal technology platforms and civic apps — 311 systems, participatory budgeting tools, smart city dashboards, and citizen engagement platforms — are promoted as tools for democratic governance and government efficiency. Cities spend millions on these systems, often contracted to private technology vendors.

Marxist analysis identifies several problems. First, civic tech typically treats political problems as information problems: a 311 app can report a pothole but cannot address the structural underfunding that produces potholes. Second, private vendors (Google's Sidewalk Labs, IBM, Palantir) extract data from public interactions while retaining proprietary control over the platforms. Third, 'smart city' initiatives often serve surveillance and gentrification rather than community empowerment. The question is whether digital infrastructure should be publicly owned and democratically controlled, or whether it will become another avenue for private capital to profit from public goods while reshaping urban governance in its interests.
