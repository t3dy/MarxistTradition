NIMBYism is typically framed as irrational resistance to progress, but Marxist analysis reveals it as a coherent expression of petit-bourgeois class interest. For the homeowning stratum, property is simultaneously a use-value (shelter) and their primary store of exchange-value (wealth). Zoning restrictions, building height limits, and exclusionary land-use rules function as mechanisms to protect and inflate this asset value. The suburban homeowner defending single-family zoning is defending the conditions of monopoly ground rent that sustain their class position.

This dynamic exposes a fundamental contradiction within liberal urbanism. The same property regime that produces NIMBYism also produces the housing crisis: both are expressions of the commodity form of land and shelter. Homeowners are not simply being selfish -- they are acting rationally within a system that ties retirement security, intergenerational wealth transfer, and social status to residential property values. The mortgage-asset economy creates structural incentives for supply restriction that no amount of moral suasion can overcome.

From a Marxist perspective, the NIMBY-YIMBY debate obscures the real antagonism: not between homeowners and renters as individuals, but between the logic of housing as a commodity and housing as a social right. Both NIMBYism and market-urbanist responses operate within bourgeois property relations. A materialist approach demands attention to the class structure of land ownership and the state's role in reproducing spatial inequality through tax policy, mortgage subsidies, and racialized zoning histories.
