Universal basic income — an unconditional cash payment to all citizens regardless of employment — has gained support across the political spectrum, from Silicon Valley techno-optimists to left social democrats. Marxist debates about UBI are sharply divided.

Proponents argue that UBI could strengthen workers' bargaining power by reducing dependence on wage labour, providing a material floor from which to organise and resist exploitation. Critics counter that UBI would function as a subsidy to low-wage employers, allowing them to pay below-subsistence wages; that it would replace the collective provision of services (healthcare, housing, education) with individual market consumption; and that it would stabilise capitalism by managing technological unemployment without challenging ownership of the means of production. The Silicon Valley enthusiasm for UBI — from Mark Zuckerberg to Elon Musk — suggests it is compatible with and even useful to capital. A Marxist approach might instead demand universal public services, shorter working hours, and democratic control of automation.
