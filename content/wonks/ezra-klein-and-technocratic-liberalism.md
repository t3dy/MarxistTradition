Technocratic liberalism presents policy as a matter of evidence-based optimization, stripping political questions of their class content. Figures like Ezra Klein frame governance as a problem of institutional design and information flow, yet this framing systematically excludes the structural antagonism between capital and labor. The "abundance agenda" promises growth for all while leaving untouched the relations of production that determine who captures the surplus. What appears as pragmatism is in fact a deeply ideological commitment to managing capitalism more efficiently.

From a Marxist perspective, technocracy is not the absence of politics but the politics of the ruling class presented as neutral expertise. The professional-managerial stratum that produces and consumes wonk media occupies a contradictory class position, mediating between capital and labor while identifying with neither. Their preferred mode of intervention -- regulatory tweaks, tax incentives, behavioral nudges -- operates entirely within the commodity form, never questioning why housing, healthcare, and education are commodities in the first place.

The deeper function of technocratic liberalism is ideological reproduction. By framing every crisis as a failure of policy design rather than a contradiction of capitalism, it forecloses the possibility of systemic transformation. As Marx argued, the ruling ideas of any epoch are the ideas of the ruling class -- and the ideology of pragmatism serves precisely to naturalize capitalist social relations as the permanent horizon of political possibility.
