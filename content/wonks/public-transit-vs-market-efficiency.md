The neoliberal framing of public transit as an inefficient subsidy that should be disciplined by market forces fundamentally misunderstands its social function. Transit systems are not failed businesses but collective infrastructure for the reproduction of labor-power -- they move workers to workplaces, reduce the cost of living for the working class, and represent a socialized component of the wage. Subjecting transit to profitability metrics privileges exchange-value over use-value, cutting routes that serve low-income communities while investing in corridors that serve property development.

The history of transit privatization confirms Marx's analysis of primitive accumulation applied to public goods. Systems built through public investment and collective labor are transferred to private operators who extract profit by cutting service, suppressing wages, and deferring maintenance. The automobile-highway complex that replaced American streetcar systems was not a market outcome but a deliberate act of capitalist class power, subsidized by the state through highway construction and oil industry lobbying.

A Marxist approach to transit insists that mobility is a social need, not a commodity. The relevant question is not whether a bus route covers its operating costs but whether the transportation system serves the collective needs of the working class. Socialist planning traditions demonstrate that transit can be organized as a public good -- free at the point of use, democratically governed, and integrated into broader systems of urban planning that prioritize human need over capital circulation.
