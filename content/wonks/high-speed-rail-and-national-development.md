High-speed rail debates illuminate fundamental questions about state capacity, public versus private investment, and the spatial organisation of capitalism. China's 40,000km HSR network, built through centralised state planning, contrasts sharply with the US's inability to complete a single line (California's troubled project), revealing how different political-economic systems handle infrastructure development.

Marxist analysis identifies several dynamics. First, the US failure reflects the structural power of automobile, airline, and fossil fuel capital over transportation policy — industries whose profitability depends on car-dependent sprawl. Second, rail infrastructure requires the kind of long-term, large-scale public investment that neoliberal capitalism has systematically undermined through privatisation and austerity. Third, the comparison with China raises questions about whether effective infrastructure development requires forms of state capacity incompatible with financialised capitalism's short-term profit horizons. The US infrastructure deficit is not a technical failure but a class outcome.
