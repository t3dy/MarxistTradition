Smart city initiatives — sensor networks, data-driven governance, AI-optimised traffic and utilities, predictive policing, digital public services — represent the intersection of tech capital and urban governance. Companies like Google (Sidewalk Labs), IBM, Cisco, and Palantir market smart city platforms to municipalities seeking efficiency gains and modernisation.

Marxist analysis identifies smart cities as a new frontier of accumulation and control. Data extracted from residents' movements, consumption, and interactions becomes a commodity controlled by private platforms. Predictive policing algorithms reproduce and intensify racial profiling. Google's failed Quayside project in Toronto revealed the contradiction between corporate data extraction and democratic urban governance. Smart city technology concentrates power in unaccountable tech firms while creating digital infrastructure that serves surveillance and gentrification. The alternative — publicly owned, democratically controlled urban technology serving community needs — is systematically marginalised by the structural power of tech capital over cash-strapped municipalities.
