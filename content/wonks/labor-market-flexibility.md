Labour market flexibility — the neoliberal programme of weakening employment protections, reducing union power, facilitating hiring and firing, and promoting temporary, part-time, and zero-hours contracts — has been a central policy objective since the 1980s. The OECD, IMF, and World Bank have consistently promoted 'structural reforms' to make labour markets more 'flexible.'

Marxist analysis identifies flexibility as a class strategy to restore the reserve army of labour's disciplinary function. Marx showed that capital requires a pool of unemployed and underemployed workers to suppress wages and enforce workplace discipline. Post-war labour protections partially insulated workers from market dependence; flexibility reforms reverse these gains. The result is widespread precarity: the growth of zero-hours contracts, gig work, temporary agency employment, and involuntary part-time work. Standing's concept of the 'precariat' captures the resulting class condition, though Marxists debate whether this represents a new class or the intensification of capitalism's normal tendency to commodify labour-power.
