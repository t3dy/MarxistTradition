Amazon's logistics empire — encompassing warehouses, delivery networks, cloud infrastructure (AWS), and the marketplace platform — represents a new form of monopoly capital built on control of distribution and data infrastructure rather than production. Amazon's dominance of e-commerce logistics allows it to extract rents from third-party sellers, undercut competitors through below-cost pricing funded by AWS profits, and discipline its massive workforce through algorithmic management.

Warehouse workers face intense exploitation: algorithmically monitored productivity quotas, high injury rates, anti-union surveillance, and precarious employment conditions. Amazon's model externalises costs onto workers (who bear delivery vehicle expenses), municipalities (who provide tax incentives for warehouse siting), and the postal system (which subsidises last-mile delivery). The Teamsters' organising campaigns and the Amazon Labor Union's Staten Island victory represent working-class resistance to this model. Amazon-style logistics capitalism raises fundamental questions about whether critical infrastructure — the systems through which goods reach people — should be privately owned and operated for profit.
