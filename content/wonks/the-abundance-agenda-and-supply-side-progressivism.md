The abundance agenda represents neoliberalism's latest ideological mutation: a supply-side economics rebranded in progressive language. Its core claim -- that deregulation, faster permitting, and increased production can deliver broad prosperity -- recycles the fundamental error of bourgeois political economy that Marx identified: treating distribution as a function of aggregate output rather than class relations. Producing more housing, energy, or infrastructure within capitalist property relations does not alter who captures the surplus; it merely expands the field of accumulation.

Supply-side progressivism systematically confuses the commodity form of scarcity with scarcity itself. Housing is not expensive because too few units exist in the abstract but because it is produced as a commodity for profit and allocated through a market in which effective demand, not need, determines access. The same logic applies to healthcare, education, and energy. The abundance framework treats these as production problems when they are fundamentally problems of social relations -- of who owns, who profits, and who is excluded.

What makes this ideology particularly effective is its appropriation of progressive concerns about inequality and access. By promising that growth can substitute for redistribution, the abundance agenda forecloses the terrain of class struggle. It tells workers that they need not fight over the distribution of the surplus because a bigger surplus will eventually trickle down -- the same promise that has structured bourgeois economics since Ricardo, and that Marx demonstrated to be structurally impossible under capitalist relations of production.
