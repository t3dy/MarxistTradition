Data centres — the physical infrastructure of cloud computing, AI, and digital platforms — have become one of the fastest-growing sources of energy consumption globally. A single large data centre can consume as much electricity as a small city, and the AI boom is projected to dramatically increase demand. Tech corporations receive billions in tax breaks and subsidies to site data centres, while communities bear the costs of increased energy prices, water depletion, and grid strain.

Marxist analysis reveals the class dynamics: Big Tech externalises energy and environmental costs while capturing the profits of digital accumulation. Public utilities are effectively subsidising private capital when they provide below-cost electricity to data centres. The energy demands of AI training and cryptocurrency mining demonstrate that ‘dematerialised’ digital capitalism has an enormous material footprint. The question of who controls energy infrastructure — and whether it serves public needs or private accumulation — is sharpened by the data centre boom.
