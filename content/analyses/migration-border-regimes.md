Migration under capitalism is not primarily a product of individual choice but of the structural dynamics of uneven development. The penetration of capitalist relations into subsistence economies, the dispossession of peasant producers through land grabs and structural adjustment, and the concentration of investment and employment opportunities in metropolitan centers together produce the material compulsion to migrate. These processes, which Rosa Luxemburg analyzed as the ongoing character of primitive accumulation, continue to displace populations from the conditions of their social reproduction and drive them toward labor markets where they must sell their labor power under conditions they do not control.

Border regimes serve a dual function within capitalist accumulation. On one hand, they regulate the flow of labor to match capital's variable demand, admitting workers during periods of expansion and restricting entry during contraction. On the other hand, they produce categories of legal vulnerability, particularly undocumented status, that render migrant workers subject to heightened exploitation, lower wages, and exclusion from legal protections. The undocumented worker is not outside the capitalist system but occupies a structurally produced position within it, performing necessary labor under conditions of exceptional precarity that exert downward pressure on the wages and conditions of the entire working class.

The racialization of migration operates as a powerful mechanism for dividing workers along lines of national origin, ethnicity, and legal status. By constructing migrants as threats to national culture, security, or economic welfare, dominant ideological apparatuses obscure the class dynamics that produce migration and redirect working-class grievances away from capital and toward fellow workers. Any Marxist analysis of contemporary border regimes must therefore address the intersection of class exploitation with racial and national oppression, understanding these not as separate systems but as mutually constitutive dimensions of capitalist social relations in their contemporary global form.
