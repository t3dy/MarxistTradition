The introduction of AI coding assistants, automated legal research, AI-generated design, and machine-written analysis represents a direct confrontation between capital's drive to reduce socially necessary labor time and the class position of knowledge workers. For decades, professionals in software engineering, law, finance, and media occupied a privileged position in the labor market, their wages sustained by the high SNLT required for their specialized output. Tools like Claude Code, Copilot, and their successors are compressing that SNLT at a speed unprecedented since the mechanization of textile production.

The implications follow Marx's analysis with uncomfortable precision. When machines reduce the labor time socially necessary to produce a commodity, the value of that commodity falls, and with it the wages of the workers who produce it. The knowledge worker's diploma, once a barrier to entry functioning like a guild charter, becomes worthless when a machine can replicate the output. What we are witnessing is the proletarianization of the professional-managerial class: the same process that turned skilled artisans into machine-tenders now threatens to turn software engineers into AI prompt-operators, paid not for their expertise but for their role as a human-in-the-loop whose own labor time is being progressively squeezed toward zero.

The political consequences are profound. The professional-managerial class has historically served as a buffer between capital and labor, identifying upward with ownership rather than downward with the working class. As AI erodes their material basis, this class faces a choice: solidarity with the broader working class in demanding collective control over the means of production (including AI systems), or a desperate scramble to maintain individual advantage through credentialism, gatekeeping, and alignment with capital. The outcome of this choice will shape the class dynamics of the coming decade.
