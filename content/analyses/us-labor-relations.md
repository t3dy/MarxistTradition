The period following 2020 has witnessed a significant resurgence of labor militancy in the United States, with successful unionization campaigns at Amazon, Starbucks, and other major corporations, alongside powerful strike actions by UPS workers and the United Auto Workers. This wave of organizing emerges from the material conditions of intensified exploitation: decades of declining real wages, the erosion of benefits, the proliferation of precarious gig work, and the experience of the pandemic, which revealed both the essential character of working-class labor and the disposability with which capital treats the workers who perform it. These struggles confirm the Marxist understanding that class conflict is not an aberration but the fundamental dynamic of capitalist society.

The contradiction between labor scarcity and wage suppression is particularly acute in this period. Despite widespread labor shortages that demonstrate the indispensability of workers, capital continues to resist wage increases through automation, the threat of relocation, and the systematic dismantling of the institutional frameworks for collective bargaining. The erosion of the National Labor Relations Board under successive administrations reveals the state's structural alignment with capital, functioning not as a neutral arbiter but as an instrument for the reproduction of exploitative relations of production. Gramsci's analysis of hegemony helps explain how decades of anti-union ideology have been internalized by significant sections of the working class itself, creating a cultural barrier to solidarity that must be overcome alongside the material barriers.

The significance of these labor struggles extends beyond the immediate demands for higher wages and better conditions. Each successful unionization campaign and each strike that wins concessions from capital chips away at the ideological edifice that has declared class struggle obsolete and unions anachronistic. The new labor movement, led disproportionately by young workers, women, and workers of color, carries the potential to reconstitute the working class as a political subject capable of challenging not merely individual employers but the capitalist system of production itself. Whether this potential is realized depends on the movement's capacity to develop organizational forms and political consciousness adequate to the scale of the contradictions it confronts.
