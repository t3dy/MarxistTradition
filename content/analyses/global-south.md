The condition of the Global South in the twenty-first century cannot be understood apart from the history of colonialism and the continuing structures of imperial extraction that followed formal decolonization. The mechanisms have evolved from direct colonial administration to a sophisticated architecture of financial discipline: sovereign debt, structural adjustment programs, bilateral investment treaties, and trade agreements that collectively function to transfer wealth from the periphery to the core. The International Monetary Fund and World Bank, ostensibly institutions of development assistance, operate as enforcers of neoliberal restructuring, compelling debtor nations to privatize public assets, slash social spending, deregulate labor markets, and open their economies to foreign capital on terms that systematically disadvantage domestic producers.

The extraction of resources from the Global South by multinational corporations constitutes a contemporary form of primitive accumulation, in which communities are dispossessed of land, water, minerals, and agricultural resources through a combination of legal manipulation, state violence, and market coercion. The surplus value generated by super-exploited labor in mines, plantations, and sweatshops across Africa, Asia, and Latin America flows upward through global value chains to enrich shareholders in New York, London, and Frankfurt. This system of unequal exchange, in which the commodities of the periphery are chronically undervalued relative to those of the core, reproduces the colonial division of labor in which the South provides raw materials and cheap labor while the North monopolizes technology, finance, and the appropriation of value.

The debt trap deserves particular attention as a mechanism of neo-colonial control. Nations that borrowed under conditions of structural coercion now transfer more wealth to creditors in debt service than they receive in new aid or investment, creating a net flow of capital from the poorest to the richest countries. This is not a failure of development but its intended outcome within the logic of imperial capitalism. Rosa Luxemburg's insight that capital must continually incorporate non-capitalist territories and populations to sustain itself finds confirmation in the ongoing enclosure of commons, the proletarianization of peasant communities, and the commodification of every dimension of social and natural life across the Global South. Liberation from this system requires not reform of international institutions but their abolition and replacement with structures of genuine solidarity and collective self-determination.
