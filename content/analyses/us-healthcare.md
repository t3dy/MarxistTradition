The US healthcare system represents one of the most extreme examples of the commodification of a basic human need under capitalism. Health insurance companies and pharmaceutical corporations function as intermediaries that extract surplus from the social reproduction of labor-power, inserting themselves between patients and care in order to capture profit. The result is a system in which the United States spends far more per capita on healthcare than any comparable nation while producing significantly worse outcomes for the majority of its population. This paradox is explicable only in terms of the class logic of the system, which is organized around profit extraction rather than the provision of care.

The pharmaceutical industry exemplifies the contradictions of capitalist medicine. Publicly funded research produces the knowledge base for drug development, but the resulting products are patented by private firms that charge monopoly prices far exceeding production costs. The opioid crisis demonstrated how pharmaceutical capital actively creates and exploits health crises for profit, with devastating consequences for working-class communities. Insurance companies, meanwhile, function as rent-extracting gatekeepers whose administrative overhead constitutes a massive transfer of value from patients and providers to shareholders.

The class character of health outcomes in the United States is stark. Life expectancy, chronic disease rates, and access to care are all strongly correlated with class position, with poor and working-class Americans facing dramatically worse health across every metric. This is not a market failure but the predictable result of a system in which healthcare is allocated by purchasing power. The ideological framing of private healthcare as consumer choice obscures the fundamental reality that for the working class, the choice is often between financial ruin and forgoing necessary care.
