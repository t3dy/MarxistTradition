The transformation of higher education into a commodity represents one of the most consequential developments in late capitalist class reproduction. What was once partially decommodified through public funding has been progressively subjected to market logic, with students recast as consumers purchasing credentials and universities restructured as firms competing for tuition revenue. The result is a system in which access to education, long presented as the primary vehicle of social mobility, is mediated by the capacity to assume enormous debt, effectively mortgaging future labor power to finance present credentialing.

Student debt functions as a powerful mechanism of class discipline, binding graduates to the labor market with an urgency that forecloses alternatives to wage employment. The debtor must accept whatever work is available, suppressing the reservation wage and undermining collective bargaining power among educated workers. This debt-driven compulsion operates alongside the ideological apparatus of meritocracy, which attributes differential outcomes to individual effort and talent rather than to structural class position, thereby legitimating inequality even as the material basis for the meritocratic promise erodes.

The contradiction between credentialism and declining returns to education has become increasingly acute. As credential inflation devalues degrees and the labor market fails to absorb graduates into the professional positions their education promised, the ideological function of education as a legitimating institution comes under strain. The growing movement for debt cancellation and free public higher education represents a recognition, however partial, that the commodification of education is incompatible with the reproduction of a viable working class, and that the crisis of student debt is inseparable from the broader crisis of capitalist accumulation.
