The 2003 invasion of Iraq stands as one of the clearest demonstrations of imperialism as a structural feature of monopoly capitalism in the twenty-first century. The war was driven by the convergence of multiple fractions of the American ruling class: the oil industry sought control over Iraq's vast petroleum reserves, the military-industrial complex profited from the enormous expenditure on weapons and logistics, and finance capital anticipated the opening of new markets through the forced liberalization of the Iraqi economy. The fabrication of intelligence regarding weapons of mass destruction served as the ideological pretext for what was fundamentally an act of imperial resource seizure and market expansion.

The occupation that followed the invasion enacted a sweeping program of neoliberal restructuring that laid bare the economic logic underlying the military campaign. Coalition Provisional Authority Order 39 opened virtually all sectors of the Iraqi economy to foreign ownership, Order 40 permitted foreign banks to operate with minimal regulation, and Order 81 restructured agricultural patent law to benefit multinational agribusiness. This wholesale privatization of a sovereign nation's economy, carried out under military occupation, represents what David Harvey has theorized as accumulation by dispossession, the use of extra-economic force to create new domains for capital accumulation when the internal dynamics of the system reach their limits.

The human cost of the war, over one million Iraqi deaths, millions displaced, and the destruction of an entire society's infrastructure, reveals the barbarism that underlies the civilizing rhetoric of liberal imperialism. The war enriched Halliburton, Blackwater, Lockheed Martin, and the major oil companies while devastating working-class communities in both Iraq and the United States, where the costs were borne through military casualties, austerity, and the diversion of social spending to war budgets. The Iraq War thus confirms the Marxist thesis that imperialist war is a class phenomenon, waged by the bourgeoisie of the imperial core against the peoples of the periphery and against their own working class simultaneously.
