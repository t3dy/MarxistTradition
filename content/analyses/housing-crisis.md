The global housing crisis represents one of the clearest illustrations of the contradiction between use-value and exchange-value in contemporary capitalism. Housing, a basic necessity for the reproduction of labor-power, has been systematically transformed into a financial asset class. As David Harvey has argued, real estate serves as a critical sink for overaccumulated capital, absorbing surplus that cannot find profitable investment in productive sectors. The result is a speculative cycle in which housing prices are driven far beyond what wages can sustain, producing a permanent affordability crisis for the working class.

The rise of institutional landlordism, in which private equity firms and real estate investment trusts acquire vast portfolios of rental properties, represents a new phase of rent extraction. This process mirrors what Marx described as primitive accumulation: the dispossession of populations from the means of subsistence in order to create new circuits of profit. Evictions, gentrification, and the conversion of public housing into private assets are contemporary forms of enclosure that concentrate wealth while producing homelessness and precarity on a mass scale.

The class dimensions of the housing crisis are unmistakable. While property owners benefit from rising asset values, renters and the unhoused bear the costs of a system organized around profit rather than need. The ideological framework of homeownership as a path to middle-class security obscures the structural reality that housing markets are designed to extract value from those who can least afford it, transferring wealth upward to landlords, developers, and financial institutions.
