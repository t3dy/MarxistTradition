The US-led war against Iran represents a continuation of over a century of imperial intervention in the Middle East, driven fundamentally by the strategic imperative to control the region's petroleum reserves and maintain dollar hegemony over global energy markets. The military-industrial complex functions as a key circuit of capital accumulation, where defense contractors extract enormous profits from public expenditure while the destruction of Iranian infrastructure creates future opportunities for reconstruction contracts and market penetration. This war, like those before it, demonstrates that imperialism is not a policy choice but a structural necessity of capital in its monopoly phase.

The class character of the conflict is laid bare in the composition of the fighting forces and the distribution of war's costs and benefits. Working-class soldiers, disproportionately recruited from economically devastated communities, bear the physical burden of combat while the bourgeoisie reaps windfall profits from arms sales, energy price spikes, and speculative financial instruments tied to wartime volatility. The war economy simultaneously disciplines domestic labor by channeling social spending into military budgets and suppressing dissent under the banner of national security.

Rosa Luxemburg's insight that capitalism requires non-capitalist territories for its continued expansion finds renewed relevance here. The destruction of Iranian state sovereignty and the forced integration of its economy into neoliberal frameworks constitute a violent episode of primitive accumulation, dispossessing an entire population of collective resources to feed the insatiable appetite of global capital. The geopolitical competition between the US, China, and Russia over Iranian resources further confirms that imperialist war is the concentrated expression of the contradictions inherent in the capitalist world system.
