The Trump administration's militarized campaign against Mexican drug cartels must be understood not as a genuine effort to eliminate narcotics trafficking but as a political technology for managing labor markets and consolidating state power. The border functions as a mechanism for producing a stratified workforce in which undocumented migrants, perpetually threatened by deportation, accept wages and conditions far below what documented workers can demand. This artificial segmentation of the working class serves capital by depressing wages across the board while providing a racialized scapegoat that diverts working-class anger away from the bourgeoisie.

The political economy of the drug trade itself reveals deep structural connections to legitimate circuits of capital. Cartel profits flow into US and international banks, real estate markets, and consumer economies, making the narcotics industry a functional component of capital accumulation rather than an external threat to it. The billions spent on border enforcement and military operations generate their own profits for defense contractors, surveillance technology firms, and private prison corporations, creating a self-sustaining complex with powerful material interests in the perpetuation of the drug war.

The racialized character of the anti-cartel discourse performs crucial ideological work by constructing Latin American migrants as an existential threat rather than as fellow workers subjected to the violence of capital on both sides of the border. This nationalist framing obscures the role of US trade policy, particularly NAFTA and its successors, in devastating Mexican agriculture and manufacturing, which displaced millions of workers and created the conditions of desperation that cartels exploit. A Marxist analysis reveals that the border war is ultimately a class war waged against the most vulnerable segments of the international proletariat.
