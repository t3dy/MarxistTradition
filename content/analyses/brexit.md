The 2016 Brexit referendum represents a textbook case of class misdirection, in which the legitimate grievances of a deindustrialized and economically abandoned working class were channeled into a nationalist project that served the interests of a specific fraction of British capital. Decades of neoliberal policy, pursued by both Conservative and Labour governments, had devastated manufacturing communities across northern England, Wales, and the Midlands, producing mass unemployment, crumbling public services, and a profound sense of dispossession. Rather than directing their anger at the capitalist class responsible for these conditions, workers were offered the European Union as a scapegoat, with immigration serving as the ideological bridge between material deprivation and nationalist resentment.

The class composition of the Brexit coalition reveals the complexity of the conjuncture. The Leave campaign was funded and led by a faction of the British bourgeoisie, centered in hedge funds and deregulation-oriented capital, that sought freedom from European labor and environmental protections. This elite project was grafted onto genuine working-class discontent through a Gramscian war of position waged in the tabloid press and social media, constructing a populist narrative of national sovereignty that obscured the material interests at stake. The concept of commodity fetishism is instructive here: just as the commodity form conceals the social relations of exploitation behind the appearance of exchange between things, the sovereignty discourse concealed class relations behind the appearance of a conflict between nations and bureaucracies.

The aftermath of Brexit has confirmed the Marxist analysis. The promised benefits to working-class communities have not materialized; instead, the removal of EU regulatory frameworks has intensified the precarity of labor, weakened environmental and consumer protections, and produced new barriers to the free movement of workers while preserving the free movement of capital. The Brexit episode demonstrates that nationalism within capitalist society functions primarily as an ideological mechanism for managing class contradictions, offering the working class symbolic sovereignty while deepening its material subordination. Any genuinely emancipatory politics must begin by exposing this mystification and rebuilding class solidarity across national boundaries.
