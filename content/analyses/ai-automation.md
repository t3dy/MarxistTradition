The rapid development of artificial intelligence represents a qualitative transformation in the forces of production with profound implications for the capital-labor relation. From a Marxist perspective, AI is best understood as a dramatic increase in the organic composition of capital, the ratio of constant capital (machinery, technology) to variable capital (living labor). As AI systems replace human labor across sectors from manufacturing to white-collar work, capital achieves massive productivity gains while reducing its dependence on wage labor. This dynamic intensifies what Marx identified as the tendency of the rate of profit to fall, since it is living labor alone that generates surplus-value.

The contradiction between rising productivity and declining demand constitutes the central crisis tendency of AI-driven capitalism. As workers are displaced by automated systems, the consumer base necessary to realize the value of commodities contracts. Capital attempts to manage this contradiction through credit expansion, precarious employment, and the creation of new markets, but these are temporary fixes that defer rather than resolve the underlying problem. The result is a deepening polarization between a shrinking class of highly compensated technical workers and a growing surplus population consigned to precarious gig work or unemployment.

AI surveillance represents a new frontier in labor discipline. Workplace monitoring systems powered by machine learning track worker productivity, communication, and even emotional states in real time, creating a panoptic regime of control that extends far beyond the factory floor. This technological intensification of exploitation is presented ideologically as efficiency and optimization, obscuring the fact that it serves the class interest of capital in maximizing extraction from labor while minimizing the possibility of organized resistance.
