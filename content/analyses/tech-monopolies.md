The rise of tech monopolies such as Amazon, Google, and Meta represents a new phase in the concentration and centralization of capital. These firms have achieved dominant positions not primarily through the production of commodities in the traditional sense but through the control of platforms that mediate exchange, communication, and information. By establishing themselves as indispensable infrastructure for economic and social life, they extract monopoly rents from businesses that depend on their platforms, from workers whose labor is organized through their systems, and from users whose data is harvested as a raw material for advertising and surveillance.

Network effects function as barriers to entry that create new forms of enclosure analogous to the historical enclosure of common lands. Once a platform achieves dominance, the cost of switching for users and businesses becomes prohibitive, locking participants into ecosystems controlled by a single firm. Data, harvested at scale from billions of users, functions as a means of production that cannot be replicated by competitors, granting incumbents a structural advantage that antitrust frameworks designed for industrial-era competition are ill-equipped to address. The result is a tendency toward monopoly that is not an aberration but an expression of capital's inherent drive toward concentration.

The political implications of tech monopoly extend beyond market power. These firms exercise enormous influence over the circulation of information, the organization of labor, and the structure of public discourse. Their capacity to shape algorithmic environments constitutes a form of ideological power that operates beneath the level of conscious deliberation, structuring what is visible, valued, and actionable within digital space. Any Marxist analysis of contemporary capitalism must grapple with the ways in which tech monopolies have reorganized the circuits of capital, the labor process, and the terrain of political struggle.
