Platform capitalism, exemplified by companies like Uber, Amazon, and DoorDash, represents a significant restructuring of the capital-labor relation. These platforms have developed new mechanisms for extracting surplus-value by disaggregating the traditional employment relationship, classifying workers as independent contractors to avoid the costs of benefits, protections, and collective bargaining rights. This misclassification is not a legal technicality but a class strategy that shifts the burden of reproduction costs, from healthcare to vehicle maintenance, onto workers themselves while the platform captures the surplus generated by their labor.

Algorithmic management represents a qualitative advance in workplace discipline. Where the factory foreman and the middle manager once served as the visible agents of capitalist control, the algorithm performs this function invisibly and continuously. Workers are monitored, evaluated, and disciplined in real time through rating systems, automated dispatch, and dynamic pricing, creating a regime of control more pervasive than anything the traditional workplace could achieve. The result is a deepening of alienation, as workers are reduced to data points in a system whose logic is entirely opaque to them.

The erosion of collective bargaining under platform capitalism is not incidental but structural. By atomizing the workforce into isolated individuals competing against one another for algorithmically allocated tasks, platforms prevent the formation of the collective solidarity necessary for labor organization. The political challenge for the working class is to develop new forms of organization adequate to these conditions, recognizing that the platform is not a neutral technology but a class instrument designed to maximize extraction while minimizing resistance.
