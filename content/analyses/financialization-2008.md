The financialization of the global economy from the 1970s onward represents one of the most significant structural transformations in the history of capitalism. As rates of profit in productive industry declined, surplus capital increasingly migrated into financial circuits where it could expand through lending, speculation, and the creation of ever more elaborate financial instruments. This process generated the appearance of self-sustaining accumulation through money begetting money, but it remained ultimately dependent on the extraction of surplus value from labor in the productive economy. The growing gap between the volume of financial claims and the underlying capacity to generate value created the conditions for systemic crisis.

The 2008 financial crisis exposed the fictitious character of much of the wealth accumulated through financialization. The collapse of the subprime mortgage market and the subsequent implosion of securitized debt instruments revealed that the elaborate architecture of financial innovation had been built upon the precarious foundation of working-class household debt. The crisis cascaded through global financial networks, demonstrating both the interconnectedness of contemporary capital and the fragility of a system in which speculative claims had vastly outstripped the real economy's capacity to generate the value needed to honor them.

The state response to the crisis revealed the class dynamics of capitalist governance with exceptional clarity. Massive public resources were mobilized to rescue financial institutions deemed too big to fail, while the costs of the crisis were transferred onto the working class through austerity programs, wage suppression, and the erosion of social provisions. The socialization of losses and privatization of gains that characterized the bailout period demonstrated that the state functions not as a neutral arbiter but as an instrument for managing the contradictions of accumulation in the interests of the dominant class fraction. The failure to fundamentally restructure the financial system after 2008 ensured that the underlying dynamics of financialization continued, setting the stage for future crises rooted in the same structural contradictions.
