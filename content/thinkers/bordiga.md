Amadeo Bordiga was the founder and first general secretary of the Communist Party of Italy (PCd'I) in 1921, and the most uncompromising representative of the left communist current within the international communist movement. Bordiga rejected parliamentary participation, anti-fascist united fronts, and national liberation movements as concessions to bourgeois politics, insisting on the programmatic invariance of communist principles against all tactical accommodations to existing conditions. His concept of "organic centralism" opposed both the democratic centralism of the Bolsheviks and the loose federalism of social democracy, envisioning the party as a historically determined organ of the class whose program is scientifically derived from the objective movement of capital rather than democratically decided by its members.

Expelled from the PCI leadership by the Comintern in favor of Gramsci's more flexible line, Bordiga spent the fascist period in internal exile and political silence. He returned to activity after 1945, producing a remarkable body of theoretical work on the capitalist nature of the Soviet Union, the agrarian question, urban planning, and the ecological contradictions of capitalism — themes that anticipated concerns of later Marxist thought. His reading of Marx emphasizes the critique of political economy as an impersonal science of capitalist development, rejecting humanist, voluntarist, and democratic interpretations. Bordiga's influence persists in the Italian communist left, the International Communist Party, and among theorists drawn to his rigorous anti-humanism and his insistence that communism is not a political program but the real movement of history.
