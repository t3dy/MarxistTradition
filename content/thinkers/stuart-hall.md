Stuart Hall was a Jamaican-born British cultural theorist and sociologist who was one of the most influential intellectuals of the postwar British left. A founding editor of *New Left Review* and longtime director of the Centre for Contemporary Cultural Studies at the University of Birmingham, he played a pivotal role in establishing cultural studies as an academic discipline and in bringing questions of race, identity, and popular culture into Marxist analysis.

Hall drew on Gramsci's concept of hegemony to analyze how consent is manufactured through cultural institutions, media, and everyday practices. In *Policing the Crisis* (1978), he and his co-authors analyzed how the moral panic around mugging served to legitimate authoritarian state measures. His analysis of Thatcherism as an "authoritarian populism" that restructured common sense demonstrated how the right could win consent for policies that served ruling-class interests. Hall's insistence that race is not reducible to class, but is articulated with it in specific historical conjunctures, expanded the analytical reach of Marxist cultural theory.
