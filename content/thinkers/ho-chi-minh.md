Ho Chi Minh was a Vietnamese revolutionary leader who founded the Viet Minh independence movement and served as president of the Democratic Republic of Vietnam. He spent decades abroad in France, the Soviet Union, and China, becoming a committed Marxist-Leninist who saw the struggle against colonialism as inseparable from the international class struggle. His leadership guided Vietnam through wars against French and American intervention.

Ho Chi Minh adapted Marxism-Leninism to the conditions of a colonized, predominantly peasant society, synthesizing the national liberation struggle with socialist revolution. He argued that colonial oppression constituted a form of class exploitation on a global scale and that the liberation of colonized peoples was integral to the world proletarian revolution. His practical and theoretical contributions made Vietnam a central reference point for anti-colonial Marxism and for debates about the relationship between nationalism and socialism in the Third World.
