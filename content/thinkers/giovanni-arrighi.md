Giovanni Arrighi was an Italian sociologist and political economist whose work on the cyclical dynamics of historical capitalism placed him among the most original world-systems theorists. After early research on labor and development in Africa, he moved to the United States where he taught at Johns Hopkins University and developed his theory of systemic cycles of accumulation.

In *The Long Twentieth Century* (1994), Arrighi argued that the history of capitalism is structured by successive cycles of accumulation, each led by a different hegemonic power (Genoa, the Dutch Republic, Britain, the United States), and that each cycle culminates in a phase of financialization signaling hegemonic decline. In *Adam Smith in Beijing* (2007), he explored the possibility that the rise of China represents a fundamental shift in the global order. Arrighi's synthesis of Braudel, Marx, and Smith provided a powerful framework for understanding capitalism as a world-historical system governed by recurring patterns of expansion, crisis, and hegemonic transition.
