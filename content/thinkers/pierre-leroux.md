Pierre Leroux was a French philosopher and political thinker who is often credited with coining the word "socialism" in 1834. Originally a follower of Saint-Simon, he broke with the Saint-Simonian movement and developed his own philosophical system blending social criticism with religious and humanistic themes. He served in the French National Assembly after the 1848 revolution and spent years in exile following Louis-Napoleon's coup.

In his major work *On Humanity*, Leroux argued that human progress depends on the recognition of solidarity and mutual dependence among individuals. He rejected both liberal individualism and authoritarian collectivism, seeking a synthesis grounded in a quasi-religious conception of humanity's spiritual unity. While Marx criticized Leroux's idealism, Leroux's emphasis on the social nature of human existence and his critique of competitive individualism contributed to the broader intellectual ferment from which scientific socialism emerged.
