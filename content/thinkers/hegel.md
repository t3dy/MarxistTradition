Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel developed the most comprehensive system of dialectical philosophy in the Western tradition, and his thought constitutes the indispensable intellectual background to Marx's own method. Hegel's dialectic — the movement of thought through contradiction, negation, and sublation (*Aufhebung*) — provided Marx with the conceptual framework he would later claim to have "inverted," placing the dialectic on a materialist rather than idealist foundation. The *Phenomenology of Spirit* (1807) traces the development of consciousness from immediate sense-certainty through self-consciousness, reason, and spirit to absolute knowledge, a journey that Marx reinterpreted as the historical process of human self-production through labor.

Hegel's *Science of Logic* profoundly influenced the structure of *Capital*, particularly the movement from abstract to concrete and the analysis of how categories generate their opposites through internal contradiction. His *Philosophy of Right* provided the theory of civil society and the state that Marx subjected to critique in his earliest political writings. The Young Hegelians — Feuerbach, Bauer, Stirner — developed Hegel's philosophy in materialist and critical directions that Marx both absorbed and transcended. Lenin's reading of the *Science of Logic* during World War I, recorded in the *Philosophical Notebooks*, renewed interest in the Hegel-Marx relationship and remains a touchstone for debates about dialectical materialism. Whether Marx's relationship to Hegel represents continuity or rupture remains one of the most contested questions in Marxist philosophy.
