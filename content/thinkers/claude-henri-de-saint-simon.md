Claude Henri de Saint-Simon was a French aristocrat and social theorist whose vision of an industrially organized society made him a foundational figure of both socialism and sociology. Having fought in the American Revolution and survived the French Revolution, he devoted his later years to developing a science of society that would guide the transition from the feudal-military order to a new industrial civilization led by scientists, engineers, and productive workers.

In works such as *The Industrial System* and *New Christianity*, Saint-Simon argued that the key social antagonism lay between the productive classes (industriels) and the idle parasites who lived off rent and privilege. He called for the reorganization of society to serve the welfare of the poorest and most numerous class. Engels credited Saint-Simon with recognizing that politics is the science of production and that the state would eventually give way to the administration of things. His influence extended to Marx's conception of class conflict and to positivist sociology through his student Auguste Comte.
