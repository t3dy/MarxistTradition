Samir Amin was an Egyptian-French economist and one of the most prominent theorists of dependency and unequal exchange in the Marxist tradition. Born in Cairo and educated in Paris, Amin spent most of his career in Dakar, Senegal, where he directed the Third World Forum and the African Institute for Economic Development and Planning, combining theoretical work with practical engagement in African economic policy and South-South cooperation.

Amin's *Accumulation on a World Scale* (1970) argues that the capitalist world system is structured by a fundamental asymmetry between center and periphery, in which the peripheral economies are not merely "behind" the advanced countries on a common developmental path but are structurally dependent — their underdevelopment is the necessary counterpart of metropolitan accumulation. Drawing on Marx's theory of value and extending it to the international division of labor, Amin developed a theory of unequal exchange in which the transfer of value from periphery to center is built into the structure of world trade, perpetuating global inequality regardless of the development strategies adopted by individual nations.

His concept of "delinking" — the strategic disengagement of peripheral economies from the world market to pursue autonomous, nationally determined development — challenged both free-trade orthodoxy and the Soviet model of integration into a socialist world market. *Eurocentrism* (1988) extended his critique to the ideological domain, arguing that Western social science universalizes the particular historical experience of Europe and naturalizes capitalist modernity as the telos of all human development. Amin's work remains a central reference for debates about globalization, imperialism, and the prospects for development in the Global South.
