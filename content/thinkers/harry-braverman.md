Harry Braverman was an American Marxist writer and metalworker whose analysis of the labor process under monopoly capitalism reinvigorated a neglected area of Marxist research. A skilled craftsman who worked in coppersmithing and steel before becoming an editor at Monthly Review Press, he brought firsthand experience of industrial work to his theoretical analysis.

In *Labor and Monopoly Capital* (1974), Braverman argued that the twentieth-century transformation of work was driven by management's systematic effort to separate conception from execution, concentrating knowledge and control in managerial hands while reducing workers to interchangeable operators of predetermined tasks. Drawing on Marx's analysis of the labor process and Frederick Taylor's principles of scientific management, he demonstrated how deskilling extended from manufacturing into clerical, service, and retail work. The book sparked an extensive "labor process debate" and remains foundational for understanding how capitalist control reshapes the organization of work and the experience of the worker.
