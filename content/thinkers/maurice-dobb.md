Maurice Dobb was a British Marxist economist at Cambridge University whose work on economic history and value theory established him as one of the most important Marxist economists in the English-speaking world. A member of the Communist Party, he combined scholarly rigor with political commitment and helped train a generation of economists and historians.

His *Studies in the Development of Capitalism* (1946) sparked the famous "transition debate" by arguing that feudalism declined primarily due to internal class conflicts rather than the external stimulus of expanding trade, as had been argued by Paul Sweezy and others. In *Theories of Value and Distribution since Adam Smith* (1973), Dobb provided a comprehensive survey of the history of economic thought that highlighted the class content embedded in competing theories of value. His defense of the labor theory of value and his historical analysis of the transition from feudalism to capitalism remain key reference points in Marxist economic thought.
