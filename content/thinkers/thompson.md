Edward Palmer Thompson was a British historian and political activist whose *The Making of the English Working Class* (1963) transformed the practice of social history and remains one of the most widely read works in the Marxist historiographical tradition. Thompson insisted that the working class was not a static sociological category or a structural position but a historical relationship — something that happened in the process of class formation, as working people made sense of their shared experiences of exploitation through inherited traditions, institutions, and cultural forms. His famous formulation that the working class was "present at its own making" placed human agency and lived experience at the center of historical materialism.

Thompson's approach developed in explicit opposition to both the base-superstructure determinism of orthodox Marxism and the structuralist anti-humanism of Althusser, whom he subjected to a devastating polemic in *The Poverty of Theory* (1978). He argued that Althusser's theoretical practice eliminated history, agency, and moral experience from Marxism, producing a scholastic system incapable of engaging with the actual struggles of working people. Thompson's insistence on the irreducibility of human experience and moral judgment to structural determination has been criticized as culturalist empiricism by his opponents, but his historiographical legacy — the recovery of the history of those whom he described as having been "condescended to by posterity" — reshaped social history worldwide.

Thompson was also a prominent figure in the Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament and the European peace movement of the 1980s.
