Henryk Grossman was a Polish Marxist economist and historian who produced one of the most rigorous theoretical accounts of capitalist crisis and breakdown. Active in the socialist movement in Galicia before World War I, he later joined the Frankfurt Institute for Social Research and fled Nazi Germany, eventually settling in East Germany after the war.

His major work, *The Law of Accumulation and Breakdown of the Capitalist System* (1929), argued that capitalism tends toward breakdown due to the falling rate of profit, which he demonstrated through a detailed mathematical model building on Marx's reproduction schemas. Against underconsumptionist and disproportionality theories of crisis, Grossman insisted that crisis is rooted in the production process itself and in the overaccumulation of capital relative to available surplus value. His work was largely neglected for decades but has been rediscovered as a key contribution to the debate over whether capitalism contains inherent limits to its own reproduction.
