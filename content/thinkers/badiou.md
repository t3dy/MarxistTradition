Alain Badiou is a French philosopher whose work represents one of the most ambitious attempts to reconstruct the philosophical foundations of communist politics after the collapse of the Marxist-Leninist party-state model. His philosophical system, developed across *Being and Event* (1988) and *Logics of Worlds* (2006), grounds ontology in mathematical set theory and argues that genuine change — what Badiou calls an "event" — is a rupture in the established order of being that cannot be predicted, deduced, or explained by the situation in which it occurs. Truth is the process of remaining faithful to such an event, working out its consequences against the resistance of the existing order.

Badiou identifies four domains of truth — science, art, love, and politics — and insists that emancipatory politics operates through a logic irreducible to the other three. His political thought, shaped by French Maoism and the experience of May 1968, rejects the parliamentary road, the party-state model, and the identification of politics with the management of existing social arrangements. *The Communist Hypothesis* (2009) argues that communism should be understood not as a historical program tied to a specific organizational form but as an eternal idea — the hypothesis that collective egalitarian organization is possible — whose historical forms (the revolutionary movement, the party, the state) are experiments to be evaluated and renewed rather than abandoned.

Badiou's work has been influential in continental philosophy and political theory, though critics argue that his mathematical ontology is disconnected from the material analysis of capitalism and class struggle that defines the Marxist tradition.
