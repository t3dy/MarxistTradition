Lucien Goldmann was a Romanian-French Marxist philosopher and literary sociologist who developed the method of "genetic structuralism" for analyzing the relationship between literary works and the social structures from which they emerge. A student of Jean Piaget and deeply influenced by Lukacs's *History and Class Consciousness*, he worked at the Ecole Pratique des Hautes Etudes in Paris.

In *The Hidden God* (1955), Goldmann analyzed the tragic vision of Pascal and Racine as an expression of the worldview of the noblesse de robe, a declining social group caught between the monarchy and the rising bourgeoisie. His method sought to identify homologies between the structures of literary works and the structures of consciousness of particular social classes, without reducing literature to a direct reflection of economic conditions. In *Towards a Sociology of the Novel* (1964), he extended this approach to show how the form of the novel corresponds to the structure of exchange in market society. Goldmann's work provided a sophisticated alternative to vulgar sociological approaches to literature and culture.
