Karl Beck was a Hungarian-born German-language poet associated with the Young Germany literary movement. His collection *Songs of the Poor Man* (Lieder vom armen Mann, 1846) expressed sympathy for the suffering of the lower classes through romantic and sentimental verse, gaining him a reputation as a poet of social conscience.

Marx and Engels criticised Beck in *The German Ideology* and the *Communist Manifesto* as representative of 'True Socialism' — a tendency that translated French socialist ideas into the language of German philosophical idealism, producing sentimental appeals to humanity rather than concrete class analysis. Beck's poetry exemplified what Marx saw as the substitution of literary sentimentality for scientific understanding of class relations and revolutionary politics. His work illustrates the broader phenomenon of radical literary culture in the Vormärz period that expressed social discontent without developing a materialist analysis of its causes.
