Arnold Ruge was a German political writer and philosopher who was a leading figure of the Young Hegelian movement and an early collaborator of Karl Marx. He edited the influential *Hallische Jahrbucher* (later *Deutsche Jahrbucher*), which served as the principal organ of Left Hegelian criticism of Prussian absolutism and religious orthodoxy. In 1844, he and Marx co-edited the *Deutsch-Franzosische Jahrbucher* in Paris, though they soon parted ways politically.

Ruge represented the democratic-republican wing of the Young Hegelians who sought to realize Hegel's philosophy through political reform and the extension of civil liberties. Marx's break with Ruge marked a decisive turning point in his intellectual development: while Ruge remained committed to political emancipation within a liberal framework, Marx argued that genuine human emancipation required the overthrow of the social relations that underlie political oppression. Ruge's significance in the Marxist tradition lies primarily in his role as an interlocutor whose limitations helped Marx clarify the distinction between bourgeois democracy and proletarian revolution.
