Amilcar Cabral was the revolutionary leader of the independence movement in Guinea-Bissau and Cape Verde and one of the most original Marxist theorists produced by the African liberation struggles. As secretary-general of the PAIGC (African Party for the Independence of Guinea and Cape Verde), he led a successful guerrilla war against Portuguese colonialism while simultaneously developing a body of theoretical work that creatively adapted Marxist analysis to African conditions without mechanical application of European categories.

Cabral's most distinctive theoretical contribution was his analysis of culture as a site of resistance and his concept of "return to the source" — the argument that colonized peoples must reconnect with their own cultural traditions, not as nostalgic retreat but as a basis for revolutionary consciousness and national reconstruction. He argued that the driving force of history is the mode of production rather than class struggle as such, a formulation that allowed him to analyze societies without fully developed class structures in Marxist terms. His analysis of the role of the petty bourgeoisie in national liberation movements was remarkably honest: he argued that this class, which necessarily leads the anti-colonial struggle because of its education and organizational capacity, must commit "class suicide" — subordinating its own interests to those of the peasant and working masses — or risk reproducing colonial domination under a new flag.

Cabral was assassinated in January 1973, months before Guinea-Bissau declared independence. His theoretical legacy has influenced revolutionary movements across Africa, Latin America, and the Black radical tradition.
