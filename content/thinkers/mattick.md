Paul Mattick was a German-born, American-based Marxist theorist who combined council communist politics with a rigorous defense of Marx's crisis theory grounded in the tendency of the rate of profit to fall. Active in the German workers' movement as a teenager during the revolutionary upheavals of 1918-21, Mattick emigrated to the United States in 1926, where he became involved in the IWW and the council communist movement, editing the journal *Living Marxism* (later *New Essays*) through the 1930s and 1940s.

His major work, *Marx and Keynes* (1969), argues that Keynesian demand management and state intervention cannot overcome the fundamental contradictions of capital accumulation but merely postpone and intensify crisis. Mattick contends that the postwar boom, far from refuting Marx's crisis theory, was sustained by war spending, imperial expansion, and credit creation that temporarily offset the underlying decline in profitability — measures that would eventually exhaust themselves and produce a return of systemic crisis. *Economic Crisis and Crisis Theory* (1974) provides a comprehensive survey and critique of competing Marxist explanations of crisis, defending the falling-rate-of-profit thesis against underconsumptionist and disproportionality theories.

Mattick's work gained renewed attention after the 2008 financial crisis, as his analysis of the limits of Keynesian intervention appeared vindicated by events. His son, Paul Mattick Jr., has continued the theoretical project with *Business as Usual* (2011), applying his father's framework to contemporary capitalism.
