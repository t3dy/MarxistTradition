Ernesto "Che" Guevara was an Argentine-born revolutionary who played a central role in the Cuban Revolution and became one of the most iconic figures of twentieth-century Marxism. Trained as a physician, his travels across Latin America exposed him to the continent's deep inequalities and radicalized his politics. He served as a key military commander during the guerrilla campaign that overthrew the Batista regime in 1959 and subsequently held important positions in the Cuban government.

Guevara's theoretical contributions include his doctrine of guerrilla warfare as a strategy for revolution in the Third World, arguing that a small revolutionary foco could catalyze popular insurrection without waiting for objective conditions to ripen. In *Man and Socialism in Cuba*, he articulated a vision of the "new man" formed through revolutionary struggle, emphasizing moral incentives over material ones in the transition to socialism. His insistence on internationalism and his death fighting in Bolivia in 1967 cemented his status as a symbol of revolutionary commitment, though his foco theory has been widely debated within the Marxist tradition.
