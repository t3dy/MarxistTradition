James Mill was a Scottish historian, economist, and philosopher who played a central role in systematizing and popularizing the ideas of David Ricardo. His *Elements of Political Economy* (1821) presented Ricardian economics in a condensed, deductive form that aimed at textbook clarity. He was also a leading utilitarian thinker and political reformer who advocated for parliamentary reform and was closely associated with Jeremy Bentham.

Marx engaged critically with James Mill's economic writings, particularly in his early Paris notebooks of 1844. Marx saw Mill as a vulgarizer who stripped Ricardo's insights of their analytical depth, reducing political economy to a set of dogmatic propositions that served bourgeois apologetics. Marx's excerpts and commentary on Mill's work were important in developing his early critique of alienation, money, and exchange relations. Mill's role in the Marxist tradition is primarily as a representative of the ideological tendencies within classical political economy that Marx sought to expose and overcome.
