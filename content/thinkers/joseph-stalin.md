Joseph Stalin was the leader of the Soviet Union from the mid-1920s until his death in 1953, and his codification of Marxism-Leninism became the official ideology of Communist parties worldwide. Originally a Bolshevik organizer in the Caucasus, he rose through the party apparatus to consolidate power after Lenin's death, defeating rivals including Trotsky, Bukharin, and Zinoviev.

Stalin's theoretical contributions include the doctrine of "socialism in one country," which broke with the Marxist expectation of international revolution, and *Dialectical and Historical Materialism* (1938), which codified Marxism into a rigid system of universal laws. He oversaw the rapid industrialization and forced collectivization of the Soviet Union, accompanied by mass repression and the gulag system. Within the Marxist tradition, Stalin's legacy is deeply contested: official Marxism-Leninism upheld his contributions to socialist construction, while Trotskyists, council communists, and many Western Marxists argued that Stalinism represented a bureaucratic degeneration or outright betrayal of the revolutionary project, reducing Marxism to a state ideology that legitimized authoritarian rule.
