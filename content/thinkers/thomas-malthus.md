Thomas Malthus was a British clergyman and political economist whose *Essay on the Principle of Population* (1798) argued that population growth inevitably outstrips the growth of food supply, leading to poverty, famine, and social misery. He used this thesis to oppose poor relief and radical reform, contending that the suffering of the lower classes was a natural law rather than a consequence of social organization.

Marx regarded Malthus as one of the most ideologically motivated of the classical economists, a shameless apologist for the landed aristocracy who deployed pseudo-scientific arguments to justify inequality. Marx countered Malthus's population theory by arguing that what appears as overpopulation under capitalism is actually a relative surplus population produced by the dynamics of capital accumulation itself. The reserve army of labor is not a natural demographic phenomenon but a structural feature of the capitalist mode of production. Malthus's influence persists as a foil against which Marxist analysis of poverty and crisis continues to define itself.
