Deng Xiaoping was a Chinese communist leader who served as the paramount leader of the People's Republic of China from the late 1970s until the early 1990s. A veteran of the Long March and a senior party official under Mao, he was purged twice during the Cultural Revolution before returning to power after Mao's death in 1976 and launching a sweeping program of economic reform.

Deng's reforms introduced market mechanisms into the Chinese economy while maintaining the political monopoly of the Communist Party, a program he justified under the formula "socialism with Chinese characteristics." He argued that developing the productive forces was the primary task of socialism and that China must pass through a prolonged initial stage requiring pragmatic economic policies. His approach generated intense debate within Marxist thought: supporters argue he creatively adapted socialism to Chinese conditions, while critics contend that his reforms represented a restoration of capitalism under the ideological cover of Marxism-Leninism, making China a case study in the tensions between socialist politics and market economics.
