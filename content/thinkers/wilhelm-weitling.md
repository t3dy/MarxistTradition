Wilhelm Weitling was a German tailor and early communist theorist whose *Guarantees of Harmony and Freedom* (1842) was one of the first works of German socialism. Self-educated and from the artisan class, Weitling developed a utopian communist programme combining Christian egalitarianism with proposals for the abolition of private property and money.

Marx initially praised Weitling's work as evidence of the German proletariat's theoretical capacity but broke with him at the famous 1846 Brussels meeting, where Marx insisted that communist politics must be grounded in scientific analysis rather than moral appeals and conspiratorial schemes. Weitling represented the tradition of artisan communism — rooted in craft solidarity and millenarian egalitarianism — that Marx sought to supersede with historical materialism. After emigrating to the United States, Weitling's influence waned, but his career illustrates the pre-Marxist communist tradition from which scientific socialism emerged and against which it defined itself.
