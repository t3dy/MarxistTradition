Anton Pannekoek was a Dutch astronomer, Marxist theoretician, and leading figure of the council communist movement, which rejected both social-democratic parliamentarism and Leninist vanguardism in favor of workers' councils as the organizational form of proletarian revolution. Pannekoek's early career in the pre-war SPD left wing placed him alongside Luxemburg and Gorter in opposing revisionism and advocating mass action. After the Russian Revolution, he became one of the most articulate critics of Bolshevism from the left, arguing that the substitution of party for class — the vanguard's seizure of state power on behalf of the workers rather than the workers' own self-organization — inevitably produced bureaucratic dictatorship rather than socialist emancipation.

His major work, *Workers' Councils* (1946), argues that the emancipation of the working class can only be achieved through the direct, democratic self-organization of workers at the point of production, without the mediation of political parties, trade union bureaucracies, or the state. *Lenin as Philosopher* (1938) critiques the epistemological foundations of dialectical materialism as codified by Lenin and the Soviet philosophical establishment, arguing that Lenin's materialism remained trapped within a pre-Marxist, bourgeois-scientific framework. Pannekoek's insistence on the autonomous self-activity of the working class and his critique of all forms of substitutionism — party, state, or union acting in place of the class itself — have influenced autonomist Marxism, operaismo, and contemporary anti-authoritarian socialist currents.
