Charles Fourier was a French social theorist whose elaborate critiques of civilization and visionary proposals for communal living made him one of the most original utopian socialists. Working as a traveling salesman, he developed a comprehensive system for reorganizing society into cooperative communities called phalansteries, where labor would be made attractive by aligning work with human passions and desires. He rejected the repressive morality of bourgeois society and offered strikingly modern insights into the alienation produced by commercial civilization.

Engels praised Fourier's devastating critique of bourgeois society and his dialectical understanding of historical development. Fourier's observation that the status of women serves as an index of social progress was adopted by Marx and the socialist tradition more broadly. While his cosmological speculations and detailed blueprints for ideal communities were dismissed as fanciful, his psychological insights into the distortions that capitalism inflicts on human desire have attracted renewed interest from thinkers seeking to connect Marxism with questions of everyday life and affect.
