Pierre-Joseph Proudhon was a French socialist theorist and the first person to call himself an anarchist. His declaration that 'property is theft' in *What Is Property?* (1840) made him famous, while his mutualist economic programme proposed replacing capitalism with a system of free credit and cooperative exchange without state intervention.

Marx initially admired Proudhon's critique of property but broke with him decisively in *The Poverty of Philosophy* (1847), a polemical response to Proudhon's *The Philosophy of Poverty*. Marx criticised Proudhon for failing to understand the labour theory of value, for proposing to reform commodity exchange rather than abolish it, and for substituting utopian schemes for scientific analysis of capitalist production. The Marx-Proudhon conflict established the fundamental divide between Marxist and anarchist traditions that would split the First International. Proudhon's mutualism, his opposition to strikes, and his reactionary views on women and race have continued to generate debate within the broader socialist movement.
