David Ricardo was the most rigorous of the classical political economists and the thinker whose labor theory of value Marx both built upon and subjected to fundamental critique. Ricardo's *Principles of Political Economy and Taxation* (1817) systematized Adam Smith's insights into a coherent theoretical framework, arguing that the relative value of commodities is determined by the quantity of labor necessary for their production and developing the theory of differential rent and the theory of comparative advantage in international trade.

Marx regarded Ricardo as the summit of classical political economy — the thinker who pushed the labor theory of value to its logical conclusions with the greatest scientific honesty, even when those conclusions pointed toward class antagonism between wages and profits. Ricardo demonstrated that wages and profits stand in an inverse relationship: what the worker gains, the capitalist loses, and vice versa. Yet Marx argued that Ricardo's framework remained trapped within the horizons of bourgeois thought, naturalizing capitalist categories as eternal laws rather than recognizing them as historically specific social forms. Ricardo treated value as a quantitative magnitude determined by labor time without asking why labor takes the form of value, why the products of labor take the form of commodities, and why the labor process is organized under the command of capital. This distinction between Ricardo's labor theory of value and Marx's critique of political economy — between asking how much value and asking why value at all — remains fundamental to understanding Marx's theoretical achievement.
