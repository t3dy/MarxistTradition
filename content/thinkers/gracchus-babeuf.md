Gracchus Babeuf was a French revolutionary agitator who led the Conspiracy of Equals in 1796, one of the first organized movements calling for the abolition of private property and the establishment of communal ownership. Born into poverty, he radicalized during the French Revolution and came to see political equality as meaningless without economic equality. He was arrested, tried, and executed by the Directory government in 1797.

Babeuf's *Manifesto of the Equals* articulated a vision of a society in which the earth belongs to no one and its fruits belong to everyone. His insistence that revolution must go beyond liberal political reforms to restructure property relations made him a precursor to modern communism. Marx and Engels acknowledged Babeuf as an early representative of the proletarian movement, and his conspiratorial methods influenced Blanquism and later debates about revolutionary organization.
