John Stuart Mill was a British philosopher and political economist who represented the culmination and internal crisis of the classical liberal tradition. Educated intensively by his father James Mill and influenced by Bentham's utilitarianism, he became the most prominent public intellectual of Victorian Britain. His *Principles of Political Economy* (1848) synthesized classical economics while introducing sympathies toward socialism and workers' cooperatives.

Mill attempted to separate the laws of production, which he regarded as natural and fixed, from the laws of distribution, which he considered socially modifiable. This distinction allowed him to advocate redistributive reforms while retaining the basic framework of classical economics. Marx sharply criticized this separation, arguing that production and distribution are inseparable aspects of a single mode of production. Marx saw Mill as an eclectic thinker who attempted to reconcile bourgeois political economy with working-class demands, producing superficial harmonizations rather than genuine scientific analysis of capitalism's contradictions.
