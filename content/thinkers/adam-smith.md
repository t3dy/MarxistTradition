Adam Smith was a Scottish moral philosopher and political economist whose *Wealth of Nations* (1776) laid the foundations of classical political economy. As a professor at the University of Glasgow, he developed a comprehensive analysis of the emerging capitalist order, examining the division of labor, the nature of value, the accumulation of capital, and the role of free markets in promoting economic growth.

Smith advanced a labor theory of value, arguing that labor is the real measure of the exchangeable value of all commodities, though he inconsistently also proposed a cost-of-production theory. He identified the division of labor as the primary engine of productivity and analyzed the class structure of capitalist society in terms of wages, profits, and rents. Marx acknowledged Smith as a major scientific predecessor who grasped essential features of capitalist production, while criticizing Smith's failure to consistently distinguish between value and price, and his tendency to naturalize historically specific capitalist relations as eternal laws of human society.
