Guy Debord was a French theorist, filmmaker, and founding member of the Situationist International (1957-1972), whose concept of the spectacle provided one of the most penetrating critiques of consumer capitalism and media culture in the twentieth century. *The Society of the Spectacle* (1967), written in 221 aphoristic theses, argues that in advanced capitalism, all of social life has been colonized by the image — that lived experience has been replaced by its representation, and that the spectacle is not merely a collection of images but a social relation between people mediated by images. The spectacle is the commodity form at the stage of its complete domination of social life, where everything that was directly lived has been displaced into representation.

Debord drew on Marx's theory of commodity fetishism, Lukacs's concept of reification, and the avant-garde artistic tradition of Dada and Surrealism to develop a practice of cultural subversion — *detournement* (the hijacking and repurposing of cultural material) and the *derive* (the unplanned drifting through urban environments) — aimed at disrupting the spectacular organization of everyday life. The Situationist International's influence on the May 1968 uprising in France, and the subsequent recuperation of its imagery and slogans by advertising and consumer culture, demonstrated both the power and the vulnerability of cultural critique.

Debord's analysis of how capitalism transforms rebellion into spectacle, dissent into content, and critique into brand identity has become more rather than less relevant in an era of social media, influencer culture, and algorithmic attention economies.
