Louis Blanc was a French socialist politician and historian whose work *The Organization of Work* (1839) proposed state-funded cooperative workshops as the solution to the exploitation of labour. He served in the Provisional Government of 1848 and presided over the Luxembourg Commission, a forum for workers' grievances.

Blanc's programme of social workshops (ateliers sociaux) — cooperative enterprises financed and guaranteed by the state — represented an influential form of state socialism that Marx criticised for failing to challenge the fundamental relations of capitalist production. The National Workshops actually established by the government were a deliberate caricature of Blanc's proposals, designed to discredit socialism. Their closure provoked the June Days uprising. Marx saw Blanc as exemplifying the illusion that the bourgeois state could serve as an instrument of working-class emancipation, a critique that informed his subsequent theorisation of the state as an organ of class domination.
