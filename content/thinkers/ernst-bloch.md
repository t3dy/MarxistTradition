Ernst Bloch was a German Marxist philosopher whose work on utopia, hope, and the "not-yet-conscious" represents one of the most original contributions to twentieth-century Marxist thought. Forced into exile by the Nazis, he lived in the United States before returning to East Germany, where he fell afoul of the authorities and eventually settled in West Germany.

In his magnum opus, *The Principle of Hope* (1954-1959), Bloch developed an ontology of the "not-yet," arguing that reality contains latent possibilities that have not been realized and that the function of utopian thinking is to anticipate and activate these possibilities. He examined utopian impulses across an enormous range of human cultural production, from daydreams and fairy tales to architecture and music. *The Spirit of Utopia* (1918) had earlier combined expressionist aesthetics with messianic Marxism. Bloch's insistence that Marxism must incorporate a forward-looking, utopian dimension distinguished his work from both orthodox materialism and the pessimism of the Frankfurt School, and his philosophy of hope has influenced liberation theology and contemporary critical theory.
