Jean-Baptiste Say was a French economist best known for formulating Say's Law, the proposition that supply creates its own demand, meaning that general overproduction is impossible in a market economy. His *Treatise on Political Economy* (1803) popularized Adam Smith's ideas in France while departing from Smith and Ricardo on key theoretical questions, particularly by advancing a theory of value based on utility rather than labor.

Marx identified Say as a pivotal figure in the degeneration of political economy from a genuine science into bourgeois apologetics. By denying the possibility of general crises of overproduction, Say's Law provided ideological justification for the claim that capitalism is a self-regulating, harmonious system. Marx devoted considerable effort to refuting this proposition, arguing that the separation of purchase from sale in commodity exchange creates the ever-present possibility of crisis. Say's substitution of utility for labor as the basis of value also represented, for Marx, a retreat from the insights of classical political economy.
