Robert Brenner is an American Marxist historian and economist at UCLA who is a central figure in the tradition of Political Marxism. His interventions in debates over the transition from feudalism to capitalism and the dynamics of contemporary global capitalism have been among the most influential in late twentieth-century Marxist thought.

In what became known as the "Brenner Debate," he argued that the transition to capitalism cannot be explained by demographic pressure, expanding trade, or technological change, but only by the transformation of class-property relations in the English countryside that made producers dependent on the market. In *The Economics of Global Turbulence* (2006), Brenner offered an account of the long downturn in the world economy since the 1970s rooted in overcapacity and intensified international competition rather than in the falling rate of profit or financialization alone. His work insists on the primacy of social-property relations and class conflict as the drivers of economic development and crisis.
