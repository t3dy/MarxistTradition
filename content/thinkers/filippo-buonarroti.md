Filippo Buonarroti was an Italian-born revolutionary who became a key figure in the French revolutionary underground. A participant in the French Revolution and close associate of Gracchus Babeuf, he was involved in the Conspiracy of Equals in 1796, one of the earliest attempts to organize a communist insurrection. After the conspiracy's failure and Babeuf's execution, Buonarroti spent decades in exile keeping the conspiratorial tradition alive.

His major work, *Babeuf's Conspiracy for Equality* (1828), provided a detailed account of the 1796 plot and its egalitarian program. The book served as a bridge between the French Revolution and nineteenth-century socialism, transmitting the idea that political revolution must be accompanied by social and economic transformation. Buonarroti's emphasis on disciplined secret organization influenced Auguste Blanqui and later revolutionary traditions that stressed the role of a dedicated vanguard in achieving communism.
