Henri Lefebvre was a French Marxist philosopher and sociologist whose wide-ranging work on space, everyday life, and urbanization opened new domains for Marxist analysis. A member of the French Communist Party until his expulsion in 1958, he was a prolific author whose career spanned more than six decades and whose ideas influenced the Situationist International, the May 1968 movement, and contemporary urban theory.

In *Critique of Everyday Life* (1947-1981), Lefebvre argued that capitalism colonizes not only the workplace but the entirety of daily experience, turning leisure, consumption, and domestic life into sites of alienation and social reproduction. His most influential work, *The Production of Space* (1974), contended that space is not a neutral container but is actively produced through social relations, and that each mode of production generates its own distinctive spatial form. This framework transformed how Marxists think about urbanization, architecture, and the built environment, showing that the struggle over space is a central dimension of class struggle.
