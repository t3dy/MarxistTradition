Slavoj Zizek is a Slovenian philosopher and cultural critic who has become one of the most prominent and controversial public intellectuals on the contemporary left. His theoretical project synthesizes Lacanian psychoanalysis, Hegelian dialectics, and Marxist ideology critique into a distinctive philosophical framework that has been applied to politics, cinema, theology, and popular culture with restless, encyclopedic energy.

Zizek's breakthrough work, *The Sublime Object of Ideology* (1989), argues that ideology functions not through false consciousness — people mistakenly believing what is not true — but through a cynical distance that is itself ideological. Drawing on Lacan's concept of *jouissance* (enjoyment) and Marx's analysis of commodity fetishism, Zizek contends that subjects can know perfectly well that their social reality is structured by illusion and yet continue to act as if they did not know, because ideology is embedded in practice rather than belief. The formula is not "they do not know what they do" but "they know very well what they are doing, and they do it anyway." This insight has been enormously productive for analyzing how power operates in liberal democracies and consumer societies where explicit ideological commitment has been replaced by performative compliance and ironic detachment.

His later works, particularly *Less Than Nothing* (2012), undertake a comprehensive rereading of Hegel through Lacan, arguing that dialectical materialism must be reconstructed on the basis of psychoanalytic theory. Critics accuse Zizek of intellectual inconsistency, political ambiguity, and a tendency to substitute theoretical provocation for sustained political engagement, while admirers regard him as the most inventive dialectician of his generation.
