Alexandre Auguste Ledru-Rollin was a French radical republican politician who served as Minister of the Interior in the Provisional Government of 1848 and subsequently led the democratic-socialist Montagne faction in the National Assembly. He advocated universal suffrage and social reform within a republican framework.

Marx analysed Ledru-Rollin extensively in *The Eighteenth Brumaire* and *The Class Struggles in France* as the representative figure of the democratic petty bourgeoisie — the Montagne. Marx showed how Ledru-Rollin's faction imagined itself above class antagonisms, believing that democratic institutions could reconcile the interests of capital and labour. The failure of the Montagne's attempted insurrection of 13 June 1849 demonstrated the political impotence of petty-bourgeois democracy when confronted with the organised power of the bourgeoisie. Ledru-Rollin fled to exile in London, where his trajectory illustrated the limits of democratic republicanism as a vehicle for social transformation.
