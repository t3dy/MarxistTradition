Eric Hobsbawm was a British Marxist historian whose sweeping studies of modern history made him one of the most widely read historians of the twentieth century. Born in Alexandria and raised in Vienna and Berlin, he witnessed the rise of fascism firsthand before settling in Britain. A member of the Communist Party Historians' Group, he remained a committed Marxist throughout his life while maintaining wide intellectual influence.

His four-volume history of the modern world, spanning from the French Revolution to the fall of the Soviet Union, provided a magisterial account of the rise and transformation of capitalism, imperialism, and revolution. Hobsbawm combined structural analysis of economic and social change with attention to popular movements, nationalism, and cultural transformation. His concept of "the invention of tradition" illuminated how ruling classes construct historical narratives to legitimize their power. His work demonstrated that Marxist historiography could operate at the highest level of scholarly achievement while remaining committed to understanding history as shaped by class conflict and material forces.
