Robert Owen was a Welsh industrialist and social reformer whose experiments in cooperative living made him one of the founding figures of utopian socialism. As manager of the New Lanark cotton mills in Scotland, he demonstrated that improving workers' conditions could be compatible with profitability, introducing shorter hours, better housing, and schooling for workers' children. He later attempted to establish a model community at New Harmony, Indiana, which ultimately failed.

Owen argued that human character is formed by social environment rather than innate nature, and that rational reorganization of society along cooperative lines would eliminate poverty, crime, and ignorance. Engels identified Owen as one of the three great utopian socialists alongside Fourier and Saint-Simon. While Marx criticized Owen's reliance on moral persuasion rather than class struggle, Owen's practical experiments in cooperative production and his labor theory of value influenced the early British labor movement and the cooperative tradition.
