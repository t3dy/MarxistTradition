Jose Carlos Mariategui was the founder of the Peruvian Communist Party and the most original Marxist thinker produced by Latin America. Self-educated and largely self-taught in Marxist theory during a formative sojourn in Europe from 1919 to 1923, Mariategui returned to Peru determined to develop a Marxism that could address the specific realities of Latin American societies — above all, the centrality of the indigenous question and the persistence of semi-feudal agrarian relations alongside imperialist penetration.

His masterwork, *Seven Interpretive Essays on Peruvian Reality* (1928), applies historical materialism to the analysis of Peruvian economy, land tenure, education, religion, and literature, arguing that the fundamental problem of Peru is the agrarian question and the exploitation of the indigenous majority. Mariategui rejected the orthodox Marxist assumption that Latin American countries must pass through a stage of bourgeois-democratic revolution before socialism becomes possible, arguing instead that indigenous communal traditions — the *ayllu* — could serve as a basis for socialist construction, bridging pre-capitalist and post-capitalist social forms without recapitulating the trajectory of European development.

This creative synthesis of Marxism, indigenism, and anti-imperialism anticipated later debates about combined and uneven development, peasant revolution, and the relationship between indigenous movements and socialist politics. Mariategui died of tuberculosis at thirty-five, but his insistence that Marxism must be a "heroic creation" adapted to specific national realities rather than a catechism mechanically imported from Europe has made him a foundational reference for the Latin American left.
