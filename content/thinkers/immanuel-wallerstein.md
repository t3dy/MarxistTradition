Immanuel Wallerstein was an American sociologist who created world-systems analysis, one of the most influential macro-sociological frameworks of the late twentieth century. Originally a scholar of post-colonial Africa, he came to argue that individual societies cannot be understood in isolation but only as parts of a single capitalist world-system that has structured global inequality since the sixteenth century.

In his multi-volume *The Modern World-System* (1974-2011), Wallerstein traced how a European-centered capitalist world-economy expanded to encompass the entire globe, organized into a hierarchy of core, semi-peripheral, and peripheral zones. He argued that this structure is maintained through unequal exchange and that the development of the core is structurally linked to the underdevelopment of the periphery. In *Historical Capitalism* (1983), he offered a concise theoretical statement of capitalism as a historical system oriented toward the endless accumulation of capital. His work generated extensive debate about the relationship between exchange and production, the role of the state, and the analytical boundaries of the capitalist system.
