G. A. Cohen was a Canadian-British philosopher who founded the school of Analytical Marxism, which sought to reconstruct Marxist theory using the methods of Anglo-American analytical philosophy. Educated at McGill and Oxford, he held the Chichele Chair of Social and Political Theory at Oxford and was one of the most prominent political philosophers of his generation.

In *Karl Marx's Theory of History: A Defence* (1978), Cohen provided a rigorous reconstruction of historical materialism as a functional explanation in which the productive forces have explanatory primacy over the relations of production. He argued that relations of production prevail when and because they promote the development of productive forces, and that when they cease to do so, they are eventually replaced. While this technological-determinist reading was challenged by other Marxists, Cohen's analytical precision forced a clarification of fundamental concepts. His later work shifted toward normative political philosophy, engaging with questions of justice, equality, and community that he argued Marxism could not avoid.
