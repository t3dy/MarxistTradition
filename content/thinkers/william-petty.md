William Petty was an English economist and polymath whom Marx called the founder of political economy in England. A physician, surveyor, and member of Parliament, Petty pioneered the use of quantitative methods in economic analysis, which he termed "political arithmetic." His *Treatise of Taxes and Contributions* (1662) examined the sources of state revenue and the nature of economic value.

Petty advanced one of the earliest formulations of the labor theory of value, arguing that labor and land together constitute the basis of wealth. He sought to express the value of all commodities in terms of these two fundamental factors. Marx regarded Petty's work as a crucial early step toward understanding value as rooted in labor, despite its unsystematic character. Petty's attempts to measure national wealth, analyze the division of labor, and understand the relationship between money and commodities anticipated problems that would be central to classical political economy and to Marx's own critique of it.
