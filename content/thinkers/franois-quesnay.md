Francois Quesnay was a French economist and physician to King Louis XV who founded the Physiocratic school of political economy, the first systematic attempt to analyze economic production and circulation. His *Tableau Economique* (1758) was a pioneering diagram of how wealth flows between different social classes, representing the economy as an interconnected system of production, distribution, and consumption.

Quesnay argued that agriculture was the sole source of net product (surplus), while manufacturing and commerce merely transformed or circulated existing wealth. This framework, though limited by its agrarian focus, introduced concepts that proved essential to later political economy. Marx credited Quesnay with making the crucial breakthrough of analyzing surplus production and social reproduction as a systematic process. The *Tableau Economique* served as a direct precursor to Marx's reproduction schemas in Volume II of *Capital*, and Quesnay's attempt to ground economics in material production rather than mercantile exchange anticipated the materialist orientation of Marxist political economy.
