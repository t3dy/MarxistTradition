Karl Kautsky was the leading theoretician of the German Social Democratic Party (SPD) and the Second International, widely regarded during his lifetime as the foremost interpreter of Marx and Engels's legacy. His commentary on the Erfurt Program (1892) provided the canonical popularization of Marxist theory for a generation of socialists, and *The Agrarian Question* (1899) offered the most systematic Marxist analysis of capitalist agriculture and the peasant question. As editor of *Die Neue Zeit*, the SPD's theoretical journal, Kautsky shaped the intellectual formation of the international socialist movement for three decades.

Kautsky's central role in Marxist history is inseparable from the great debates that split the movement. He led the theoretical offensive against Eduard Bernstein's revisionism in the 1890s, defending the necessity of revolutionary transformation against Bernstein's argument that capitalism could be gradually reformed into socialism. Yet when war and revolution put these questions to a practical test, Kautsky adopted positions that Lenin, Luxemburg, and Trotsky denounced as a capitulation to reformism. His opposition to the October Revolution and his pamphlet *The Dictatorship of the Proletariat* (1918), which defended parliamentary democracy against the Bolshevik model, provoked Lenin's savage polemic *The Proletarian Revolution and the Renegade Kautsky*. Kautsky's trajectory from orthodox revolutionary to democratic socialist exemplifies the political crisis of the Second International and remains central to debates about reform, revolution, and the relationship between socialism and democracy.
