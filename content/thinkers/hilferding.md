Rudolf Hilferding was an Austrian Marxist economist and politician whose *Finance Capital* (1910) provided one of the most influential analyses of the transformation of competitive capitalism into monopoly capitalism. Hilferding argued that the concentration of industrial production and the centralization of banking had produced a new form of capital — finance capital — in which banks assumed a dominant role in directing industrial investment, organizing cartels and trusts, and driving the imperialist expansion of capital into colonial territories. His analysis of the joint-stock company, the credit system, and the stock exchange as mechanisms through which finance capital coordinates and controls the productive economy anticipated many features of twentieth-century capitalism.

Hilferding's earlier work, *Bohm-Bawerk's Criticism of Marx* (1904), mounted a systematic defense of Marx's value theory against the marginalist critique, and remains one of the most rigorous engagements between Marxist and neoclassical economics. His concept of "organized capitalism" — the thesis that monopoly and state intervention had created the possibility of conscious economic planning within capitalism — influenced both social-democratic reformism and later debates about state capitalism.

Hilferding served twice as finance minister of the Weimar Republic. He fled Germany after the Nazi seizure of power, was captured by the Vichy police in 1941, and died in Gestapo custody in Paris. His theoretical legacy endures through Lenin's *Imperialism*, which drew heavily on *Finance Capital*, and through contemporary analyses of financialization.
