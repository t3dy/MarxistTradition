Ludwig Feuerbach was the pivotal figure in the transition from Hegelian idealism to the materialism that Marx and Engels would develop into historical materialism. His *Essence of Christianity* (1841) argued that theology is anthropology in disguise: the attributes human beings project onto God — omniscience, omnipotence, infinite love — are in fact the species-powers of humanity itself, alienated into a transcendent being and worshipped as an external authority. This transformative critique of religion electrified the Young Hegelian movement and provided Marx with the template for his own theory of alienation, extended from religion to politics and political economy.

Feuerbach's materialist inversion of Hegel — his insistence that thought arises from sensuous, embodied existence rather than the reverse — was the decisive philosophical event of Marx's early intellectual formation. However, Marx's *Theses on Feuerbach* (1845) subjected Feuerbach to a critique that would define the boundary between contemplative materialism and the new revolutionary philosophy. Marx argued that Feuerbach's materialism remained abstract and ahistorical, treating human nature as a fixed essence rather than as a product of social relations and practical activity. Where Feuerbach saw alienation as a philosophical error to be corrected by enlightenment, Marx insisted it was rooted in material conditions that could only be transformed through revolutionary praxis. Feuerbach thus occupies an essential but transitional position in the genealogy of Marxist thought — the thinker who made materialism possible but could not make it historical.
