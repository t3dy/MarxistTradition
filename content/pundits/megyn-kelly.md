Megyn Kelly's career arc — from Fox News anchor to failed NBC host to independent podcast personality — maps the political economy of the media career as capital accumulation strategy. Each platform shift represented not an ideological transformation but a repositioning of her personal brand within shifting media markets. Her Fox tenure established the commodity; the NBC experiment attempted to capture a centrist audience; the podcast return recognised that her highest-value market was the conservative audience she had temporarily abandoned. The consistency throughout is not political conviction but brand optimisation.

Kelly's particular ideological function is the deployment of white feminism as a legitimating discourse for reaction. Her feminism extends precisely far enough to defend her own professional advancement while stopping short of any structural critique that might challenge racial or economic hierarchies. The famous Trump confrontation elevated her brand value by performing independence, but her subsequent trajectory — anti-trans commentary, COVID scepticism, culture-war programming — reveals that the confrontation was a market event rather than a political one. She demonstrates how the category of the professional woman under neoliberalism can be mobilised to discipline feminism itself, defining women's empowerment as individual success within existing structures rather than transformation of those structures.
