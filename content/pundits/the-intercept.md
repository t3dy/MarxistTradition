The Intercept crystallizes the contradiction of adversarial journalism funded by capital. Pierre Omidyar's billions — accumulated through eBay's platform monopoly — finance investigations into surveillance, corporate malfeasance, and state violence. This is not philanthropy but ideological investment: the billionaire funds critique of the system that produced his fortune, ensuring that opposition remains within parameters compatible with capital's reproduction. The investigative exposé, however devastating, operates within a liberal framework that targets individual abuses rather than systemic structures.

The outlet's internal crises — Greenwald's departure rightward, editorial conflicts, staff reductions — are not merely organizational but reflect the structural impossibility of its founding premise. Capital cannot sustainably fund its own negation. Greenwald's trajectory from NSA critic to Tucker Carlson collaborator illustrates how anti-establishment postures untethered from class analysis are easily recuperated by reaction. The Intercept's remaining journalists do important work within the limits of investigative liberalism, but those limits are set by the very class power the outlet nominally challenges.
