<!-- Note: Change My Mind as spectacle of false dialogue; conservative comedy as ideological reproduction; the media entrepreneur -->

Steven Crowder's "Change My Mind" format is a paradigmatic instance of Debord's spectacle: the appearance of open dialogue concealing a predetermined ideological operation. The setup — an experienced media professional with prepared arguments confronting unprepared passersby — is structurally rigged to produce the commodity it sells: the humiliation of progressive positions. The table, the sign, the camera all constitute a mise-en-scene of false democratic exchange that reifies political contestation into consumable entertainment.

Crowder's broader enterprise reveals the media entrepreneur as a figure of late-capitalist ideological production. His contract disputes and platform migrations expose the contradiction between the culture warrior persona and the underlying logic of capital accumulation. Conservative comedy, as Adorno would note, performs the culture industry's function with particular efficiency: laughter directed at the vulnerable naturalizes domination while foreclosing the critical reflection that genuine humor might provoke. The audience laughs with power, not against it — the precise inversion of comedy's emancipatory potential.
