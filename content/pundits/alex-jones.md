Alex Jones is the most revealing figure in the political economy of conspiracy. Infowars operates as a circuit of ideological commodity production with unusual transparency: paranoia is manufactured, packaged as content, and monetized through supplement sales that extract surplus value directly from the anxieties the content itself generates. The supplements are not incidental to the conspiracy — they complete the commodity circuit. Fear is the raw material; the broadcast is the production process; the pill bottle is the finished commodity.

Conspiracy thinking, as Fredric Jameson suggested, represents the cognitive mapping of class domination in alienated form. Jones's audience correctly intuits that powerful forces shape their lives against their interests — this is, after all, simply the experience of living under capitalism. But the conspiracy framework mystifies this structural reality into personalized narratives of evil cabals, foreclosing the materialist analysis that might lead to organized class action. Jones thus performs a dual function for capital: he absorbs and neutralizes proto-class-conscious energy while simultaneously extracting profit from it. The spectacle of paranoia substitutes for political praxis.
