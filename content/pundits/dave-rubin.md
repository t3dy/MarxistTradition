Dave Rubin's political trajectory from progressive media to conservative commentary is less an intellectual journey than a market optimization. His invocation of the "marketplace of ideas" unwittingly reveals the truth it seeks to conceal: that in a capitalist media landscape, ideas really do function as commodities, and the market structurally rewards those that reproduce dominant ideology. Rubin discovered that reactionary content commands higher exchange value in the attention economy, and adjusted his production accordingly.

This makes Rubin a clarifying case study in the political economy of media. The grifter, far from being an aberration, is the rational actor of ideological commodity production — responding to market signals with the same logic as any other capitalist enterprise. His platform's emptiness is not a flaw but a feature: the absence of substantive analysis creates a frictionless surface for the circulation of ruling-class ideas. What Gramsci called "common sense" — the unreflective absorption of hegemonic assumptions — finds in Rubin its perfect transmitter, precisely because he appears to arrive at conservatism through open inquiry rather than class interest.
