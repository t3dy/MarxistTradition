Tucker Carlson represents the most sophisticated form of right-populism as ruling-class strategy. Born into wealth and educated at elite institutions, he performs a class ventriloquism — adopting the grievances of the working class while systematically redirecting them away from capital. His critique of corporations never extends to the profit motive itself; his defense of workers never arrives at the question of exploitation. This is what Marx recognized as the ideological inversion: a consciousness that appears to oppose the ruling order while reinforcing its foundations.

The Carlson phenomenon illustrates Gramsci's concept of trasformismo — the absorption of potentially oppositional energies into the hegemonic bloc. By articulating real economic anxieties to nationalist and reactionary politics, he forecloses class consciousness precisely at the moment it threatens to emerge. His migration from Fox News to independent media further demonstrates how capital adapts its ideological delivery mechanisms: the content remains structurally identical, but the platform shift creates an illusion of independence from corporate power, deepening the mystification.
