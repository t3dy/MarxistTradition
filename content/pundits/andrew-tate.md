Andrew Tate represents the commodification of toxic masculinity within platform capitalism. His brand — luxury consumption, misogyny, 'hustler' identity — packages petty-bourgeois aspiration as masculine empowerment, selling courses and subscriptions to young men seeking meaning in an economy that offers them precarity. His business model, including the affiliate marketing pyramid of 'Hustlers University,' is itself a microcosm of capitalist exploitation: his wealth derives from extracting fees from the very alienated young men he claims to be liberating.

Tate's ideology is the logical endpoint of neoliberal subjectivity applied to gender: if the market is the measure of all value, then masculinity is measured by the capacity to accumulate — money, women, status. His arrest on trafficking and organised crime charges reveals the continuity between 'hustle culture' ideology and actual exploitation. The manosphere he represents channels the legitimate grievances of young men facing economic precarity not toward systemic critique but toward the domination of women — a classic mechanism of displacement that redirects class anger into gender violence.
