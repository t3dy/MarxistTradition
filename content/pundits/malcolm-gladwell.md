Malcolm Gladwell is the premier ideologist of liberal meritocracy. His narrative method — extracting compelling anecdotes from social science research — performs a systematic transformation of structural phenomena into individual stories. The "10,000 hours" thesis is exemplary: it acknowledges that success requires material conditions (access to practice, resources, time) while reframing this structural insight as individual effort, converting class determination into motivational commodity. The airport book is itself a significant commodity form — knowledge packaged for the professional-managerial class, consumed in the transit spaces of global capital.

Gladwell's work for the New Yorker and his podcast ventures represent what Adorno would recognize as the middlebrow culture industry: complexity gestured at but never truly engaged, structural critique evoked but always resolved into heartwarming narrative. His well-documented corporate speaking career — commanding enormous fees from the very institutions his journalism ostensibly scrutinizes — reveals the class function with unusual clarity. Capital pays handsomely for intellectuals who can make its contradictions appear as fascinating puzzles rather than systemic violence.
