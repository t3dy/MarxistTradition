Logan Paul and Jake Paul exemplify the influencer as commodity form in its most transparent expression. Their careers — spanning YouTube stunts, boxing spectacles, crypto promotions, and energy drink brands — reveal how platform capitalism transforms personality itself into a site of accumulation. Each controversy, from the Aokigahara Forest video to the CryptoZoo fraud, functions not as a reputational cost but as a mechanism for generating the attention that constitutes their primary commodity. Their audience, predominantly young men navigating economic precarity, is simultaneously the consumer and the product being sold to advertisers.

The Pauls' business ventures — Prime energy drinks, failed crypto projects, NFT scams — recapitulate the structure of financial exploitation at the level of fan engagement. Their audiences pay not merely for products but for the parasocial fantasy of proximity to wealth. The boxing spectacles, pitting influencers against ageing athletes, literalise the commodification of masculine aggression while generating pay-per-view revenues that dwarf the purses. What the Pauls sell is not content but the ideological proposition that exploitation is entertainment and that the entrepreneur-subject is the only authentic form of masculinity available under late capitalism.
