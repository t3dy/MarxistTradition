Glenn Greenwald's political trajectory — from civil liberties journalist, Snowden collaborator, and *Intercept* co-founder to Rumble commentator aligned with right-wing populism — illustrates a structural phenomenon: the rightward drift of civil libertarianism divorced from class analysis. His reporting on NSA surveillance was genuinely important, exposing state power in ways that served working-class interests. But civil libertarianism as a political framework lacks the structural analysis to distinguish between state power that serves capital and progressive regulation that constrains it.

The economic dimension is revealing: Greenwald's migration from *The Guardian* to *The Intercept* to Substack to Rumble tracks the restructuring of media production from institutional journalism to individual content creation. Each platform shift moved him toward a more right-leaning audience whose subscription revenue increasingly shaped his political output — a process of audience capture that demonstrates how platform economics overdetermine political content. His current alignment with figures like Tucker Carlson represents the logical endpoint of anti-establishment politics without class consciousness: opposition to liberal institutions that converges with capital's own hostility to regulation.
