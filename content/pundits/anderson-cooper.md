Anderson Cooper is the liberal media's most transparent class artifact: a Vanderbilt heir presenting himself as an objective journalist. This is not merely biographical irony but structural revelation. The ruling class has always produced its own narrators, and Cooper embodies the process by which class position is mystified through professional ideology. The claim to journalistic objectivity — what Herman and Chomsky identified as a key filter in the manufacture of consent — functions precisely to render invisible the class interests that structure every editorial decision.

CNN centrism, which Cooper exemplifies, is not the absence of ideology but ideology in its most effective form. By positioning itself between "extremes," liberal media naturalizes the existing order as the reasonable center, making structural critique appear as radical deviation. Cooper's empathetic reporting style — the concerned gaze, the emotional connection with victims — performs what Gramsci called the ethical dimension of hegemony: domination experienced as compassion. The Vanderbilt fortune recedes behind the CNN desk, and class rule is transmitted as concerned professionalism.
