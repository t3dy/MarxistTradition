<!-- Note: Nathan Robinson and left media aesthetics; the magazine as counter-hegemonic project; the labor disputes contradiction -->

Current Affairs, under Nathan Robinson's editorship, represents an attempt to fuse left politics with aesthetic sophistication — the lavish magazine design, the literary prose style, the engagement with culture alongside politics. This is a conscious counter-hegemonic strategy: recognizing, as Gramsci did, that political struggle is also cultural struggle, and that the left must contest the terrain of form as well as content. The publication's willingness to produce long, argumentative essays challenges the attention-economy logic that fragments political thought into consumable fragments.

Yet the magazine's labor disputes — in which Robinson fired staff who attempted to convert Current Affairs into a worker cooperative — exposed a contradiction that Marx would find structurally predictable. A socialist publication operating within capitalist relations of production will tend to reproduce those relations internally, regardless of its editorial politics. The incident revealed the gap between ideological production and material practice that haunts all left media under capitalism. The magazine nonetheless contributes genuinely to the counter-hegemonic project through its sustained critique of liberal and conservative ideology alike.
