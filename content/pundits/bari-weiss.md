Bari Weiss exemplifies how 'cancel culture' discourse serves class interests. Her *Free Press* platform and public persona centre on defending 'free speech' and 'open inquiry' against what she frames as illiberal progressive orthodoxy. From a Marxist perspective, this operates as a class displacement mechanism: the real anxieties of professional-managerial class members about their institutional authority are translated into a narrative of cultural persecution.

The 'intellectual dark web' commodity that Weiss helped popularise packages conventional centre-right positions as heterodox thinking, turning the defence of existing power relations into a brand of courageous dissent. Her trajectory — from New York Times opinion writer to independent Substack entrepreneur — reflects the broader restructuring of media production, where individual brand-building replaces institutional affiliation. But the 'independence' is ideological: her network of donors and subscribers constitutes a class constituency whose interests she serves while presenting this service as principled journalism. The free speech framework systematically avoids questions of economic power and class domination.
