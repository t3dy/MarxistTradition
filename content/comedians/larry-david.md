<!-- Note: Curb Your Enthusiasm as a study in bourgeois neurosis, property relations, and the social contract of the owning class -->

Larry David's *Curb Your Enthusiasm* is the most sustained examination of bourgeois social relations in the history of American comedy. Every episode revolves around the unwritten contractual obligations of upper-class social life -- tipping conventions, gift reciprocity, dinner party protocols, the micro-economies of favor and obligation. Larry's violations of these norms expose them as what they are: the ideological infrastructure through which the owning class reproduces its social world.

The comedy is grounded in property relations. Larry is a man of enormous wealth whose conflicts nevertheless center on questions of ownership, exchange, and entitlement -- the same categories that structure capitalist social relations generally. His neurotic attention to fairness in trivial matters reveals how thoroughly bourgeois consciousness is organized around the principle of equivalent exchange, even in the supposedly non-economic sphere of personal relationships.

That the show is semi-improvised is structurally significant. The improvisational method produces comedy from the genuine friction of social interaction rather than from scripted scenarios, making visible the real mechanisms of class performance. Larry David playing a version of himself blurs the distinction between character and person, fiction and reality -- a formal ambiguity that mirrors the way ideology presents contingent social arrangements as natural and inevitable.
