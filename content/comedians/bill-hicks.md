<!-- Note: Radical critique of consumer capitalism, the military-industrial complex, and media manipulation -->

Bill Hicks remains the closest American stand-up has produced to an explicitly anti-capitalist comedian. His material systematically attacked consumer culture, the advertising industry, the military-industrial complex, and the ideological machinery that sustains them. Where most comedians treat politics as material, Hicks treated the entire spectacle of American capitalism as his subject.

His critique of advertising as a form of ideological domination anticipated much of what Debord theorized about the society of the spectacle. Hicks understood that marketing does not merely sell products but reproduces the social relations necessary for capital accumulation. His attacks on the Gulf War exposed the connection between militarism and capital that mainstream comedy would not touch for another decade.

Yet Hicks's radicalism remained trapped in an individualist framework. His vision was of personal awakening rather than collective action, the enlightened individual seeing through ideology rather than a class organizing to overthrow it. This limitation marks the boundary between cultural critique and political praxis.
