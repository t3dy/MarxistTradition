Richard Pryor is the most important comedian in American history from a Marxist perspective. His work made visible the lived experience of the Black lumpenproletariat and working class — poverty, police violence, addiction, incarceration — with a radical honesty that exposed the class and racial structure of American capitalism. Pryor's comedy was materialist in the deepest sense: it began from the body, from concrete experience, from the street.

His characters — winos, junkies, pimps, workers — constituted a counter-archive of American life, revealing what bourgeois culture rendered invisible. Pryor's use of his own body as a site of struggle — the self-immolation, the drug addiction, the confrontation with white audiences — enacted the violence that capitalism inflicts on Black bodies. His influence on subsequent comedy is total, yet the radical class content of his work has been domesticated through posthumous canonisation as a 'genius' — a term that individualises what was fundamentally a collective experience of oppression given extraordinary expression.
