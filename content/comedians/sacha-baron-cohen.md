Sacha Baron Cohen's work constitutes the most effective ideological critique in contemporary comedy. His method — inhabiting fictional characters (Borat, Ali G, Bruno) to elicit unguarded responses from real people — exposes the ideological unconscious of his subjects. Borat's naive foreignness provokes Americans into revealing the racism, sexism, and authoritarianism that liberal politeness ordinarily conceals.

Ali G's class performance — a white suburban man performing Black working-class identity — exposed how cultural consumption mediates class position. *Who Is America?* pushed further, demonstrating that elected officials would endorse arming toddlers and support fabricated programmes if presented by someone they perceived as ideologically aligned. This is comedy as Althusserian experiment: by disrupting the normal functioning of ideological interpellation, Baron Cohen reveals the machinery of belief that subjects ordinarily cannot see. The limitation is that exposure alone does not produce transformation — his targets return to their positions unchanged, and audiences consume the spectacle of ideological revelation as entertainment.
