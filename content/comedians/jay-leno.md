<!-- Note: Comedy as pure commodity form; the Tonight Show as factory of consent; Leno vs Conan as capitalist succession crisis -->

Jay Leno's Tonight Show was the most perfectly commodified form of comedy in American television. Every element was optimized for the broadest possible audience: the jokes targeted the lowest common denominator, the interviews promoted whatever product the guest was selling, and the entire apparatus functioned as a nightly ritual of consensual participation in consumer culture. Leno was not a comedian who happened to host a talk show; he was a delivery mechanism for the commodity form.

The Leno-Conan conflict of 2010 was instructive precisely because it stripped away the illusion of individual creative expression. NBC's decision to reinstall Leno was a pure calculation of surplus value extraction. The network owned the means of production -- the set, the timeslot, the brand -- and the comedian was a replaceable input. Conan's creative claims meant nothing against the logic of capital accumulation.

Leno's massive personal wealth, accumulated through decades of performing this function, makes him a figure of the labor aristocracy -- a worker whose exceptional compensation aligns his interests with capital rather than with other workers in the entertainment industry. His anti-union history confirms this structural position.
