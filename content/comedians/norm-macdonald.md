<!-- Note: Anti-comedy as formal resistance to commodity structure; the moth joke as refusal of efficient content delivery -->

Norm Macdonald's comedy was a sustained act of formal resistance to the commodity logic of the entertainment industry. His anti-comedy -- long, digressive stories with deliberately anticlimactic punchlines; jokes that refuse efficient delivery; bits that test the audience's patience -- constituted a refusal to optimize the comedic product for maximum exchange-value. In an industry that demands ever-more-efficient content delivery, Norm insisted on waste, digression, and the sovereignty of the process over the product.

The moth joke is paradigmatic. A three-minute shaggy dog story told on a late night show where time is the most valuable commodity, it deliberately squanders that time, building elaborate narrative architecture that collapses into a punchline of sublime stupidity. This is sabotage of the content production line -- the comedian using his platform to produce something that resists the logic of that platform.

His Weekend Update tenure, terminated after he repeatedly targeted a powerful executive's friend, revealed the material limits of comedic resistance within corporate media. Norm's refusal to respect the unwritten rules of power made visible what the comedy industry prefers to obscure: that the apparent freedom of the comedian is always circumscribed by the interests of capital. His firing was a disciplinary action by the ownership class against a worker who exceeded his permitted autonomy.
