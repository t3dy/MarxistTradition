*The Colbert Report* (2005-2014) was the most structurally incisive political satire in American television history. By performing a fictional right-wing pundit modelled on Bill O'Reilly, Colbert exposed conservative ideology as ideology — as a constructed system of beliefs, affects, and performances that presents itself as natural truth. The concept of 'truthiness' — feeling something is true regardless of evidence — identified the epistemological core of right-wing media with surgical precision.

The show's method was essentially Brechtian: the performer inhabited the role with visible artifice, preventing the audience from naturalising the character's beliefs while making the machinery of ideological production visible. The problem of ironic distance — that some conservative viewers enjoyed the show without recognising the satire — raised genuine questions about the limits of ironic critique as a political tool. Can ideology be effectively challenged through comedy that some audiences consume as confirmation? The show's cancellation and Colbert's transition to CBS represented the structural limits of institutional satire: the culture industry ultimately absorbs even its most effective critics.
