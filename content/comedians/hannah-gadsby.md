<!-- Note: Nanette as deconstruction of comedy's ideological form; refusing the tension-release commodity structure -->

Hannah Gadsby's *Nanette* is significant less for its content than for its formal intervention. By refusing the tension-release structure of comedy -- setting up jokes and then declining to deliver the expected punchline -- Gadsby exposed the ideological machinery of the comedic form itself. The argument, essentially, is that comedy's requirement for resolution forces the comedian to package trauma as entertainment, domesticating genuine suffering into consumable content.

This is a structural critique with Marxist resonance. The commodity form demands that all human experience be rendered exchangeable, and comedy's formal requirements perform this operation at the level of narrative: pain must be converted into laughter, which is to say, into a product the audience can consume. Gadsby's refusal to complete this conversion disrupts the circuit of comedic commodity production.

The difficulty is that this deconstruction was itself massively commodified. Netflix distributed *Nanette* globally, and its reception as a cultural event reproduced the very dynamics it critiqued. The refusal of comedy became the most talked-about comedy special of its year -- a contradiction Gadsby's subsequent, more conventional specials did little to resolve.
