Dave Chappelle's career is a case study in the political economy of racial comedy in America. His early work on Chappelle's Show brilliantly exposed how race functions as ideology -- the sketches anatomized the mechanisms through which racial categories are produced, reproduced, and commodified. His 2005 departure, walking away from fifty million dollars, was a genuine act of refusal against the commodification of Black cultural labor, a recognition that the laugh itself had become a tool of racial domination.

The Netflix-era Chappelle presents a starkly different figure. The specials increasingly deploy reactionary cultural positions, punching down at transgender people and aligning with conservative cultural politics. This trajectory is not simply personal evolution but reflects the class position of extreme wealth. Chappelle the multi-millionaire occupies a fundamentally different relation to the means of production than Chappelle the insurgent comic.

His career thus illustrates a core Marxist insight: consciousness is determined by social being. As material conditions changed, so did the political content of the comedy -- from exposing racial capitalism to defending the prerogatives of the wealthy.
