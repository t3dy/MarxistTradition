<!-- Note: The one-liner as minimum viable comedy commodity; surrealism as escape from realist ideology -->

Mitch Hedberg's comedy operates at the intersection of formal minimalism and surrealist defamiliarization. The one-liner is the minimum viable unit of comedic commodity -- setup and punchline compressed to maximum efficiency, infinitely portable and reproducible. In this sense, Hedberg perfected comedy as commodity form: discrete, exchangeable, stripped of all excess.

Yet the content of these minimal units is quietly subversive. Hedberg's observations take the familiar objects and rituals of consumer capitalism -- escalators, receipts, fast food, brand names -- and render them strange through lateral logic. This is a form of defamiliarization that, however apolitical in intention, momentarily disrupts the naturalized relationship between consumer and commodity. When the mundane becomes absurd, the ideological work that makes it appear normal is briefly exposed.

His performance style -- eyes closed or averted, mumbled delivery, visible discomfort with the audience relation -- registered a genuine alienation from the spectacle of live performance. Hedberg's body refused the confident self-presentation that the stand-up commodity form demands, producing a contradiction between the polished jokes and their halting delivery that expressed, perhaps unintentionally, the violence of converting living labor into entertainment product.
