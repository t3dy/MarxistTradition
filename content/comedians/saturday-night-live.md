Saturday Night Live, running since 1975, functions as one of American liberalism's most effective ideological apparatuses. Its political comedy defines the parameters of acceptable dissent: politicians are mocked for personality and competence, never for the class interests they serve. The show's political satire operates within an unwavering two-party framework that treats the Democratic-Republican duopoly as the natural horizon of political possibility.

The revolving door between SNL and power is telling: cast members become senators (Franken), write for campaigns, and host politicians as guests who humanise themselves through self-deprecating humour. The show's ownership by NBC/Comcast — one of the largest media conglomerates — determines its ideological limits. SNL's treatment of Trump was instructive: endless mockery that functioned as free publicity, treating fascist tendencies as comedy material rather than systemic crisis. Lorne Michaels's institution has survived for five decades precisely because it manages political discontent by channelling it into a weekly cathartic spectacle that leaves the structure of power untouched.
