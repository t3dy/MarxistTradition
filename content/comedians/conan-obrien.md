The Tonight Show struggle of 2010 was, beneath the celebrity drama, a labor dispute. Conan O'Brien's removal exposed what the late night format obscures: the comedian is a worker whose creative labor-power is owned by the network. When NBC calculated that Leno generated more surplus value, Conan was discarded regardless of contractual claims or audience loyalty. The episode revealed the real relations of production behind the spectacle of televisual entertainment.

Conan's subsequent career migration -- from NBC to TBS to podcasting -- traces the restructuring of media capital itself. Each move represented not artistic freedom but adaptation to new regimes of accumulation. The podcast form, celebrated as liberation from network gatekeepers, in fact intensifies self-exploitation: the comedian now performs production, distribution, and marketing functions previously handled by separate workers.

His absurdist comedy style, pointedly apolitical, functions as a refusal to engage in the liberal commentary that defines his competitors. Whether this represents genuine resistance to the politicization of entertainment or simply a different market positioning remains an open question.
