The Upright Citizens Brigade is less significant as a comedy troupe than as a labour institution — and it is as a labour institution that Marxist analysis finds its most revealing object. Founded in 1990, UCB built a business model in which aspiring comedians paid for improv classes and then performed for free or minimal compensation at UCB theatres, generating revenue for the organisation through ticket sales and bar receipts while performers received 'exposure' and 'stage time.'

This is a textbook case of exploitation concealed by ideology. The promise of future success — the mythology that UCB is a 'pipeline' to SNL, sitcoms, and film — functions precisely like the ideology of meritocracy in broader capitalism: it justifies present exploitation through the prospect of future reward that statistically almost never materialises. UCB's closure during COVID and subsequent bankruptcy revealed the fragility of a business model dependent on free labour. The comedy industry's broader reliance on unpaid open mics, underpaid club dates, and the self-financing of careers mirrors the gig economy's extraction of value from workers who bear all the risk.
