John Mulaney is the most accomplished bourgeois comedian of his generation, and this is meant analytically rather than dismissively. His comedy expresses bourgeois class consciousness with exceptional precision: the meticulously crafted joke, the appeal to social propriety, the nostalgia for an ordered world where things made sense. His material is about the anxieties of the professional-managerial class -- social embarrassment, institutional navigation, the performance of respectability -- elevated to high art through formal mastery.

The nostalgia that pervades Mulaney's work is an ideological operation. His evocations of childhood, old-fashioned social norms, and bygone cultural forms construct a mythical past of coherence and stability against the perceived chaos of the present. This is a fundamentally conservative gesture, regardless of Mulaney's personal politics: it locates the good in what has been lost rather than in what might be built.

His post-rehab material introduced genuine contradiction into this polished framework, as addiction disrupted the performance of bourgeois respectability that was both his subject and his method. Yet even this disruption was rapidly metabolized into content -- the comeback special, the arena tour -- demonstrating capital's capacity to convert crisis into commodity.
