SCTV (1976-1984), operating from the Canadian periphery of the North American entertainment industry, produced a form of media critique more structurally incisive than its American counterparts. By framing the show as the programming output of a fictional low-budget TV station in Melonville, SCTV made the institutional apparatus of television production visible as an object of satire rather than simply parodying individual programmes.

This meta-televisual framework constituted a critique of the culture industry's commodity form: viewers watched television characters producing television, exposing the medium's manufactured reality. The Canadian perspective — observing American cultural imperialism from a semi-peripheral position — enabled a critical distance unavailable to American shows embedded within the system they ostensibly satirised. Characters like Johnny LaRue (SCTV's perpetually hustling entertainer) embodied the desperation of cultural workers commodifying themselves within an entertainment industry governed by the profit motive. The show's relative obscurity compared to SNL reflects the market's preference for comedy that flatters its audience rather than interrogating the medium itself.
