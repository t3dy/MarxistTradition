Key and Peele's sketch comedy systematically anatomized the performance of race under American capitalism. Their code-switching material -- the foundational conceit of the show -- exposed racial identity not as a natural category but as a set of performed social relations, constantly adjusted according to context, audience, and power dynamics. This is a fundamentally materialist insight: identity is not what you are but what the social structure requires you to perform.

Their Obama-era timing was significant. As biracial men producing comedy during the presidency of a biracial man, Key and Peele occupied a position from which the contradictions of post-racial ideology were uniquely visible. Their sketches repeatedly demonstrated that racial categories persist not because of individual prejudice but because they are structurally embedded in American capitalism -- in police encounters, job interviews, social interactions, and cultural consumption.

The sketch format itself served as an ideological laboratory, isolating specific mechanisms of racial production in controlled scenarios. Each sketch was an experiment: what happens when a Black man code-switches in this context? The results consistently revealed the gap between post-racial ideology and material racial reality, making their comedy a form of empirical ideology critique.
