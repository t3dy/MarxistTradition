Stewart Lee is the most formally sophisticated comedian working today, and his practice constitutes something approaching a dialectical method applied to stand-up. His comedy is relentlessly self-reflexive, examining its own construction, its relationship to the audience, and the market conditions of its production. The result is work that refuses the commodity form of comedy — the efficient delivery of laughs as product — in favour of prolonged, uncomfortable explorations of how meaning and humour are produced.

Lee's deliberate rejection of mainstream success — declining panel shows, refusing to expand beyond venues where his work can function — represents a practical refusal of commodification that echoes Adorno's analysis of art's compromised autonomy under capitalism. His class analysis of British comedy — how middle-class comedians perform working-class identity, how 'alternative comedy' became the mainstream it opposed — demonstrates materialist cultural criticism in practice. The audience's discomfort is the point: Lee forces viewers to confront their own expectations about what comedy should deliver and what they are willing to pay for.
