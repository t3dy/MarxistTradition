David Letterman's significance lies in his deconstruction of the talk show as ideological form. By deploying irony, anti-humor, and visible contempt for the conventions he was performing, Letterman exposed the talk show's rituals -- the monologue, the desk, the celebrity interview -- as constructed artifice rather than natural entertainment. This was a genuinely defamiliarizing gesture in the Brechtian sense, breaking the fourth wall of televisual ideology.

Yet irony proved to be a perfectly compatible posture for late capitalism. Letterman's knowing distance from the commodity form did not prevent him from being one of the most profitable commodities in television history. As Slavoj Zizek has argued, cynical distance is the dominant ideology of late capitalism: we know the system is absurd, and this knowledge allows us to continue participating in it.

His late-career turn to sincerity on the Netflix interview show represents the exhaustion of this ironic mode. When irony becomes the dominant register, sincerity becomes the new transgression -- though one equally available for commodification under platform capitalism's demand for authentic content.
