The Whitest Kids U' Know (2007-2011) produced sketch comedy rooted in a distinctly working-class sensibility — crude, violent, absurd, and deliberately transgressive of the taste boundaries that define 'good' comedy for the educated middle class. Their work illuminates how aesthetic taste functions as a class marker: what counts as funny, what is 'too far,' and what constitutes legitimate comedy are all determinations made by the cultural gatekeepers of the professional-managerial class.

WKUK's refusal to respect these boundaries — sketches involving extreme violence, bodily functions, and nihilistic absurdity — was not mere shock value but an implicit rejection of the class system embedded in cultural production. Bourdieu's analysis of taste as class distinction applies directly: the boundaries of 'good taste' in comedy exclude working-class sensibilities while naturalising middle-class preferences as universal aesthetic standards. The troupe's relative marginality (IFC rather than network television) and the tragic early death of founding member Trevor Moore in 2021 reflect the precarity that characterises cultural work outside the entertainment industry's established class hierarchies.
