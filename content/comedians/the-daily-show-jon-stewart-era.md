*The Daily Show* under Jon Stewart (1999-2015) became the most politically significant comedy programme in American history, functioning as a primary news source for a generation of liberal viewers. Stewart's genius was identifying the gap between media representations and reality — exposing hypocrisy, contradiction, and manufactured outrage through juxtaposed clips and deadpan commentary.

From a Marxist perspective, Stewart's contribution was deeply contradictory. He exposed the spectacle of political media with genuine skill, but his critique never transcended the liberal horizon. The analytical framework was always: the system is good but the people running it are bad, dishonest, or incompetent. Class analysis was absent; structural critique was replaced by appeals to reasonableness and centrism. The Rally to Restore Sanity (2010) made the political limitations explicit: in the face of systemic crisis, Stewart counselled moderation. The show trained a generation to feel politically engaged through consumption of comedic critique — what might be called irony as ideology, a stance that mistakes awareness of contradictions for their resolution.
