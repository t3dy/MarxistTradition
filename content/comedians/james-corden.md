James Corden's Late Late Show represents the final subsumption of late night television under the logic of platform capitalism. The show was designed not as a nightly broadcast but as a factory for producing discrete viral content units -- Carpool Karaoke, Crosswalk Musical, celebrity segments engineered for social media circulation. The audience in the studio became secondary to the audience on YouTube, and the comedy itself became secondary to shareability.

Carpool Karaoke is the paradigmatic commodity form of this approach. It offers the simulation of intimacy with celebrity -- the star as ordinary person, singing in a car -- while actually reinforcing the aura of stardom through proximity. The format naturalizes the celebrity relation by appearing to dissolve it, performing what Debord would recognize as the spectacle's absorption of all lived experience into its own representation.

Corden himself functions less as a comedian than as a content host, a genial facilitator of commodity production. His departure from the show changed nothing structurally; the format was always independent of any individual performer, which is precisely what makes it a pure expression of capital's indifference to the specific labor that produces its commodities.
