Jimmy Kimmel's career arc -- from The Man Show's fratboy misogyny to tearful monologues about healthcare -- is a case study in the ideological flexibility of capital. The same performer can serve diametrically opposed cultural functions as market conditions change, because capital is indifferent to content; it cares only about the extraction of value. Kimmel did not undergo a moral transformation; he adapted to a shifting ideological landscape in which liberal sentiment became more profitable than bro humor.

His emotional monologues, particularly on healthcare, illustrate how liberal ideology channels genuine political affect into sentimental individualism. The tears are real, the suffering they reference is real, but the implied solution is always personal empathy and incremental reform rather than the socialized medicine that would require confronting capital directly. Feeling replaces analysis; sincerity substitutes for politics.

This is not to deny the material reality of healthcare crises but to note how Kimmel's treatment of them functions ideologically. By personalizing systemic issues through individual stories, the structural causes -- the commodification of health, the insurance industry's extraction of surplus -- disappear behind a veil of emotional identification. The audience is moved but not mobilized.
