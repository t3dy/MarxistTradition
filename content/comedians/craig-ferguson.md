<!-- Note: Refusing the late night commodity format; immigrant perspective on American spectacle; the monologue as conversation -->

Craig Ferguson's Late Late Show was a minor experiment in resisting the commodity standardization of late night television. By abandoning prepared monologues, scripted segments, and conventional interview techniques, Ferguson disrupted the industrial rationalization that reduces the talk show to a predictable content-delivery mechanism. His use of a robot sidekick and improvisational interviews introduced elements of genuine contingency into a format designed to eliminate it.

Ferguson's immigrant perspective -- Scottish working-class origins transplanted to American media -- gave him a structural outsider's view of the spectacle. His comedy frequently registered the absurdity of American cultural production from a position of genuine bewilderment rather than performative irony. This distance allowed occasional moments of authentic de-naturalization.

Yet the rebellion was always contained. CBS tolerated Ferguson's formal experimentation precisely because the late-late slot generates minimal revenue and his approach attracted a niche demographic. Capital permits aesthetic rebellion in the margins where it does not threaten the core operations of accumulation.
