<!-- Note: Patriot Act as liberal critique within capitalist media; the fabrication scandal and authenticity as commodity -->

Hasan Minhaj's *Patriot Act* represented the liberal wing of political comedy in its purest form: sharp, well-researched critiques of specific corporate and governmental abuses that never questioned the capitalist system producing them. Episodes on pharmaceutical pricing or Saudi Arabia exposed individual excesses while implicitly affirming that the system could be reformed through awareness and policy. This is ideology functioning at its most effective -- presenting systemic critique as unnecessary by demonstrating that the problems are always specific and fixable.

The fabrication scandal, in which Minhaj was revealed to have invented or embellished personal anecdotes, exposed a deeper structural truth. In the political economy of contemporary comedy, personal narrative is a commodity form. Authenticity is not a genuine quality but a market requirement, and the pressure to produce ever more compelling personal content generates its own logic of inflation. Minhaj did not simply lie; he responded rationally to a market that demands increasingly dramatic authentic experiences.

This dynamic illuminates how identity functions under late capitalism -- not as lived experience but as raw material for content production, subject to the same pressures of competition and differentiation that govern any commodity market.
