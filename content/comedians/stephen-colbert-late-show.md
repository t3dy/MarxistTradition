Stephen Colbert's career trajectory is a textbook case of ideological recuperation. *The Colbert Report* (2005-2014) was genuinely critical comedy: the fictional right-wing persona exposed the structure of conservative ideology — its relationship to truthiness, its performative patriotism, its corporate servility — through sustained character work that made ideology's mechanisms visible. By inhabiting the ideological position, Colbert revealed it as a construction.

*The Late Show* abandoned this structural critique for personality-driven liberal entertainment. Colbert as himself is indistinguishable from the Democratic Party establishment: he hosts politicians as friends, treats systemic crises as partisan conflicts, and channels audience anxiety into nightly catharsis that resolves in commercial breaks. The transformation was dictated by the institutional logic of network television: CBS required a host who would attract advertisers, not a satirist who might alienate them. The comedian who once exposed ideology now reproduces it — functioning as what Gramsci would recognise as an organic intellectual of the liberal bourgeoisie.
