George Carlin's later career represents the most fully developed class-conscious comedy in the American tradition. His material explicitly identified a ruling class that owns the political system, controls the media, and deploys ideology to maintain domination. His analysis of the American Dream as a deliberate mystification -- you have to be asleep to believe it -- is a precise articulation of false consciousness in comedic form.

Carlin's linguistic analysis is equally significant from a Marxist perspective. His extended routines on euphemism, jargon, and official language demonstrated how the ruling class uses linguistic manipulation to obscure material reality. Shell shock becomes PTSD; the poor become economically disadvantaged. This attention to language as a mechanism of ideological control parallels Gramsci's analysis of how hegemony operates through the colonization of common sense.

His trajectory from countercultural hipster to class-war comedian traces a genuine political radicalization. Unlike comedians who mellow with age and wealth, Carlin grew sharper, his targets more structural. He remains the benchmark against which the political claims of American comedy must be measured -- and by which most of it is found wanting.
