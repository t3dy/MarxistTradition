Trevor Noah's tenure as *Daily Show* host (2015-2022) represents a missed opportunity whose failure was structurally determined. A mixed-race South African comedian — shaped by apartheid, colonial history, and the Global South's experience of capitalism — possessed the perspective to offer American audiences a genuinely internationalist critique of power. Instead, the institutional logic of the show constrained his analysis to standard American liberal commentary.

The *Daily Show* functions as a brand with specific ideological coordinates: centrist-liberal, Democrat-aligned, oriented toward educated urban professionals. Noah was hired to maintain this brand, not to transform it. His occasional references to South African or global perspectives were absorbed into the show's existing framework rather than challenging it. The result demonstrates how the culture industry manages ideological diversity: international perspectives are welcomed as flavour — an accent, an anecdote about growing up under apartheid — while the structural analysis they might enable is systematically excluded by the format, the advertisers, and the audience's expectations.
