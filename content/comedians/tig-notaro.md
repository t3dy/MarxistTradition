Tig Notaro's legendary 2012 Largo set — performing stand-up days after a cancer diagnosis — became a landmark in the commodification of authenticity. The set was immediately hailed as brave, raw, and real, and was quickly released as a best-selling album. The rapidity with which genuine human vulnerability was transformed into a product for consumption illustrates capitalism's capacity to commodify everything, including mortality itself.

This is not a criticism of Notaro but of the system in which she operates. The comedy industry's appetite for authenticity reveals a contradiction: in a culture of manufactured entertainment, 'realness' becomes the most valuable commodity precisely because it appears to transcend commodification. Notaro's subsequent career — Netflix specials, acting roles, podcast — demonstrates the economics of confessional performance: trauma generates cultural capital that is converted into economic capital through the entertainment industry's distribution channels. The audience's emotional response — feeling connected to genuine human experience — is itself a commodity exchanged in the attention economy.
