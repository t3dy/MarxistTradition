Monty Python's comedy is saturated with class consciousness in a specifically British register. From the Upper Class Twit of the Year to the anarcho-syndicalist peasants in *Holy Grail*, the troupe consistently made class structure visible as absurd, arbitrary, and maintained through ideological force rather than natural right. Their Oxbridge education gave them insider knowledge of ruling-class culture, which they deployed as a weapon against it -- though this same education marks the limits of their critique.

*Life of Brian* achieves a double critique that remains remarkable. It attacks religious ideology as a mechanism of social control while simultaneously satirizing revolutionary organizations whose sectarian fragmentation prevents effective opposition. The People's Front of Judea is a devastating portrait of the left's tendency to substitute internal conflict for confrontation with actual power -- a critique Marx himself would have recognized from his polemics against utopian socialists.

Their formal innovations -- breaking the fourth wall, refusing narrative closure, interrupting sketches before their punchlines -- constituted a genuinely Brechtian intervention into television comedy. By making the apparatus of production visible, they disrupted the passive consumption that conventional comedy demands, forcing the audience into an active, critical relationship with the spectacle.
