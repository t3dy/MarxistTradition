The *Philosophical Notebooks* comprise Lenin's reading notes on Hegel's *Science of Logic*, *Lectures on the History of Philosophy*, and other philosophical works, written primarily during 1914-15 while Lenin was in exile in Bern. Never intended for publication and first printed in 1929-30, the notebooks reveal a Lenin grappling seriously with Hegelian dialectics at a level of sophistication absent from his earlier philosophical writing, *Materialism and Empirio-Criticism* (1908).

The notebooks contain Lenin's celebrated aphorism that it is impossible to fully understand Marx's *Capital* without having studied the whole of Hegel's *Logic*, and his recognition that the intelligent idealism of Hegel is closer to intelligent materialism than the crude, mechanical materialism that had dominated Second International Marxism. Lenin's marginal comments on the categories of the *Logic* — being, essence, concept, contradiction, the unity of opposites — show him rethinking the philosophical foundations of dialectical materialism in response to the catastrophic failure of European social democracy to resist World War I.

The significance of the *Notebooks* for Marxist philosophy has been debated along predictable lines: some readers see them as evidence of Lenin's return to Hegel and a rupture with the crude reflection theory of *Materialism and Empirio-Criticism*; others argue for continuity in Lenin's philosophical development. The notebooks have been particularly influential in the Italian Marxist tradition and among those who, following Lukacs, insist on the centrality of dialectical method to revolutionary theory.
