*Dialectic of Enlightenment*, written in exile in Los Angeles during World War II, argues that the Enlightenment project of liberating humanity through reason has dialectically reversed into new forms of domination. Horkheimer and Adorno contend that instrumental rationality — the drive to master nature through classificatory thought and technical control — contains within itself the seeds of barbarism, as the same logic that disenchants nature ultimately reduces human beings to objects of administration and manipulation. The book opens with a philosophical excursus on Homer's Odyssey, reading Odysseus's cunning self-preservation as the prototype of bourgeois subjectivity formed through the repression of inner nature.

The famous chapter on the culture industry analyzes how mass-produced entertainment under monopoly capitalism enforces conformity and forecloses critical thought under the guise of consumer choice and individual freedom. Horkheimer and Adorno argue that cultural production has been industrialized in a way that preempts genuine aesthetic experience, substituting standardized pleasures that reconcile audiences to the existing order. This analysis profoundly influenced subsequent media theory, from Guy Debord's concept of the spectacle to contemporary critiques of algorithmic content delivery.

The book's uncompromising pessimism about the prospects for emancipation within existing social arrangements reshaped critical theory and remains a provocation: if reason itself is complicit in domination, what resources remain for critique? This question continues to animate debates in philosophy, social theory, and cultural criticism.
