*The Sane Society* (1955) extends Fromm's earlier analysis in *Escape from Freedom* to argue that modern capitalist society is itself pathological — not merely that individuals within it become mentally ill, but that the social system as a whole is structured in ways that prevent the realisation of fundamental human needs for relatedness, creativity, rootedness, identity, and a frame of orientation.

Fromm analyses alienation in contemporary capitalism through the concept of the 'marketing orientation' — a character type that treats the self as a commodity to be packaged and sold. He proposes 'humanistic communitarian socialism' as the alternative: decentralised, participatory, and oriented toward human development rather than profit. The work drew on Marx's early writings on alienation, particularly the 1844 Manuscripts, and contributed to the humanist Marxist revival of the 1950s-60s. Critics from both orthodox and structuralist Marxist positions have challenged Fromm's normative humanism and his reformist political conclusions.
