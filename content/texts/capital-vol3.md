*Capital, Volume III*, assembled by Engels from Marx's draft manuscripts and published nine years after his death, examines the capitalist process of production as a whole — that is, the unity of production and circulation. The volume's central problem is the transformation of surplus value into its concrete forms: industrial profit, commercial profit, interest, and ground rent. Marx shows how competition among capitals produces a general rate of profit through the equalization of profit rates, a process that systematically obscures the origin of profit in the exploitation of labor.

The most debated section develops the law of the tendential fall in the rate of profit, which argues that rising organic composition of capital — the increasing ratio of constant to variable capital driven by technological competition — creates a structural downward pressure on profitability. Marx identifies counteracting tendencies including the cheapening of constant capital, increasing exploitation, foreign trade, and the expansion of joint-stock companies, generating a dialectic of crisis and restructuring that has defined capitalist development.

The volume's later parts contain analyses of commercial and money-dealing capital, the credit system, and fictitious capital that have become central to contemporary Marxist political economy, particularly in discussions of financialization. Marx's theory of ground rent, distinguishing differential and absolute rent, provides tools for understanding real estate speculation and resource extraction. The transformation problem raised by Volume III — the relationship between values and prices of production — remains one of the most technically contested questions in Marxist economics.
