'The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction' (Das Kunstwerk im Zeitalter seiner technischen Reproduzierbarkeit, 1935) is Benjamin's most widely read essay and one of the foundational texts of media theory. Benjamin argues that mechanical reproduction — photography and film — destroys the 'aura' of the artwork: its unique existence in a particular time and place, its authority and authenticity rooted in ritual and tradition.

The loss of aura fundamentally transforms art's social function: freed from its ritual basis, art can serve political rather than cultic purposes. Benjamin identifies the revolutionary potential of film — its capacity to train collective perception and politicise aesthetics — against fascism's aestheticisation of politics. Adorno criticised the essay for underestimating the culture industry's capacity to neutralise this revolutionary potential. The essay's concepts of aura, mechanical reproduction, and the politicisation of aesthetics have become indispensable for analysing digital culture, social media, and the politics of visual representation.
