*Aesthetic Theory*, published posthumously and left unfinished, represents Adorno's comprehensive attempt to theorize art's relationship to society and truth. The central argument holds that authentic artworks embody a truth content that resists the commodity form and instrumental reason. Art achieves this not through explicit political messages but through its formal structure, which negates the false reconciliations offered by the culture industry and administered society.

Adorno develops a dialectical conception of aesthetic autonomy: art must be autonomous from society to criticize it, yet this very autonomy is socially produced and historically conditioned. The artwork's enigmatic character -- its refusal to be immediately consumed or understood -- preserves a space for non-instrumental experience in a world dominated by exchange value. Beauty becomes inseparable from the dissonance and suffering that marks modern experience.

The text's key contributions include the theory of art's double character as both autonomous and socially determined, the concept of truth content as distinct from ideological content, and the analysis of how modernist form registers historical antagonisms. It remains the most rigorous attempt to develop a materialist aesthetics that neither reduces art to ideology nor grants it false transcendence.
