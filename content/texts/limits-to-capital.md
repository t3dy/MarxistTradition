*The Limits to Capital* is Harvey's theoretical magnum opus, reconstructing Marx's critique of political economy with sustained attention to the roles of finance, credit, and geography in capitalist accumulation and crisis. Harvey works systematically through the three volumes of *Capital*, arguing that Marx's crisis theory must be understood as operating on three interconnected levels or "cuts": the crisis tendencies arising from the basic production process (overaccumulation and the falling rate of profit), the displacement of crisis through the credit system and fictitious capital, and the further displacement through geographical expansion and spatial restructuring.

The book's most original contribution is the concept of the spatial fix, which argues that capitalism resolves its internal contradictions of overaccumulation by investing surplus capital in the production of new spatial configurations — infrastructure, urbanization, and the opening of new territories for accumulation. This process, however, does not eliminate crisis but reproduces it at a larger geographical scale, creating patterns of uneven development, regional devaluation, and inter-territorial competition that define the landscape of global capitalism.

Harvey's integration of rent theory, credit analysis, and spatial dynamics within a unified Marxist framework has made the book foundational for economic geography, urban studies, and contemporary debates about financialization and real estate speculation. The updated 2006 edition includes a new introduction reflecting on how the intervening decades of neoliberal globalization confirmed and extended the book's analytical framework.
