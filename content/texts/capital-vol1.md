*Capital, Volume I* — the only volume Marx saw through to publication — presents his systematic critique of capitalist political economy, beginning with the analysis of the commodity form as the "cell form" of bourgeois society and developing through the theories of value, money, surplus-value, the working day, and the general law of capitalist accumulation. Marx demonstrates how capital as a social relation reproduces itself through the exploitation of labor-power: the worker sells the capacity to labor for a wage that covers the cost of reproduction, while the labor actually performed creates value exceeding that wage — surplus-value — which the capitalist appropriates.

The work is structured as a dialectical progression from the abstract to the concrete. The opening chapters on the commodity and money establish the categories of use-value, exchange-value, and abstract labor; the middle sections develop the theory of absolute and relative surplus-value and the struggle over the working day; and the final part traces the historical process of primitive accumulation — the violent separation of producers from the means of production — that created the conditions for capitalist production. Marx's account of the factory system, drawing on parliamentary reports and inspectors' testimony, provides one of the most vivid and empirically grounded analyses of industrial capitalism ever written.

First published in German in 1867 and translated into Russian (1872) and French (1875) during Marx's lifetime, *Capital, Volume I* remains the foundational reference point for all subsequent Marxist economic analysis, value theory, and critique of political economy. The recent Reitter translation (2025) has sparked renewed discussion of Marx's terminology and argumentative structure.
