*The Sublime Object of Ideology* is Zizek's first major work in English and the text that established his distinctive theoretical project: the synthesis of Lacanian psychoanalysis, Hegelian dialectics, and Marxist ideology critique. Published in the year of the Berlin Wall's fall, the book provides a theory of ideology suited to an era in which cynical distance — the sense that nobody really believes in the official values of the system — has become the dominant mode of ideological subjectivity.

Zizek's central argument is that ideology does not function primarily through false consciousness — the classical Marxist model in which people are deceived about the true nature of social relations — but through a structure of fetishistic disavowal. Drawing on Lacan's concept of *jouissance* (enjoyment) and Marx's analysis of commodity fetishism, Zizek argues that the formula of ideology is not "they do not know what they are doing" but rather "they know very well what they are doing, but they are doing it anyway." Ideology is located not in what people believe but in what they do: in the objective practices, rituals, and institutional structures that reproduce social relations regardless of the subjective beliefs of the individuals who participate in them.

The book also develops Zizek's theory of the "sublime object" — the impossible, traumatic kernel around which ideological fantasies are organized — and his reading of Hegel's dialectic as a process driven by the inherent incompleteness of every symbolic order. *The Sublime Object* has been widely influential in political theory, cultural studies, and film criticism, and remains the most accessible entry point into Zizek's expansive philosophical system.
