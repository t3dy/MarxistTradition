*The Theory of Communicative Action* (Theorie des kommunikativen Handelns, 1981) is Habermas's two-volume magnum opus, developing an alternative foundation for critical theory based on communicative rationality rather than the philosophy of consciousness or the critique of political economy. Habermas distinguishes communicative action — oriented toward mutual understanding — from strategic action — oriented toward success — and argues that modernity's pathologies stem from the colonisation of the communicative lifeworld by the systemic imperatives of money and administrative power.

Volume 1 reconstructs the concept of rationality through Weber, Lukács, Adorno, and Parsons. Volume 2 develops a two-level social theory distinguishing system (economy and state) from lifeworld (culture, social integration, socialisation). The work represents Habermas's decisive break with first-generation critical theory: where Adorno saw no escape from instrumental reason, Habermas locates emancipatory potential in the structures of everyday communication. The framework has been enormously influential but widely criticised by Marxists for abandoning the critique of capitalism in favour of procedural democratic theory.
