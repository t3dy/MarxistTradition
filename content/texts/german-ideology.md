*The German Ideology*, written in 1845-46 but unpublished in Marx and Engels's lifetimes, represents their first systematic elaboration of the materialist conception of history. Composed as an extended polemic against the Young Hegelians — Feuerbach, Bruno Bauer, and Max Stirner — the manuscript argues that consciousness is determined by material life rather than the reverse, and that the ruling ideas of any epoch are the ideas of the ruling class, expressing in idealized form the material relations of domination. Marx and Engels famously abandoned the manuscript to the gnawing criticism of the mice, and it was not published in full until 1932.

The text traces the development of the division of labor and forms of property — tribal, ancient, feudal, and bourgeois — as the material basis for understanding historical change, class formation, and the production of ideology. Marx and Engels argue that the state arises from the contradiction between particular and general interests within civil society, and that genuine human emancipation requires not merely a change in consciousness but the revolutionary transformation of material conditions. The section on Feuerbach contains their most concentrated statement of historical materialism as a method.

Though unfinished and repetitive in places, *The German Ideology* remains indispensable for understanding the intellectual break through which Marx moved from philosophical critique to the materialist analysis of social formations, laying the groundwork for the critique of political economy that would occupy the rest of his life.
