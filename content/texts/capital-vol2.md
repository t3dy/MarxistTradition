*Capital, Volume II*, edited by Engels from Marx's manuscripts and published posthumously, shifts the analysis from the production of surplus value to the circulation of capital as a whole. Marx traces the metamorphoses of capital through its three circuits — money capital, productive capital, and commodity capital — showing how each form presupposes and gives rise to the others in a continuous process of self-valorization. The analysis of turnover time reveals how the speed and continuity of capital's circuit directly affects the mass and rate of surplus value realized, introducing temporal considerations largely absent from Volume I.

The most theoretically consequential section develops the schemas of simple and expanded reproduction, which model the conditions under which the total social capital can reproduce itself by maintaining proportional exchange between Department I (means of production) and Department II (means of consumption). These schemas became the object of intense debate, most notably Luxemburg's argument that expanded reproduction is impossible within a closed capitalist system, and later Marxist discussions of crisis, disproportionality, and planning.

Though often regarded as the most difficult and least read of the three volumes, Volume II provides indispensable tools for understanding logistics, supply chains, inventory management, and the temporal dynamics of capitalist accumulation. Its analysis of the circulation process has gained renewed relevance in an era defined by just-in-time production, global supply networks, and the financialization of commodity flows.
