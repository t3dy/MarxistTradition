*Traditional and Critical Theory* is the programmatic essay that defined the Frankfurt School's intellectual project. Horkheimer draws a fundamental distinction between traditional theory, which models itself on natural science and treats social facts as given objects of contemplation, and critical theory, which understands itself as part of the social processes it analyzes and orients itself toward human emancipation. Traditional theory's claim to value-neutrality conceals its complicity with existing power relations.

Critical theory, by contrast, recognizes that the separation of fact and value, theory and practice, subject and object are themselves products of a specific social order. The critical theorist is not a detached observer but a participant in the struggle for a rational society. Horkheimer argues that theory must grasp the social totality and identify the possibilities for transformation immanent within existing conditions, refusing both positivist resignation and utopian abstraction.

The essay's key contributions include the systematic critique of positivism in social science, the articulation of theory's emancipatory vocation, and the insistence that knowledge is always shaped by social interests. It established the methodological foundations for the entire tradition of Frankfurt School critical theory.
