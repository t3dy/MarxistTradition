*The Accumulation of Capital* is Luxemburg's major theoretical intervention into Marxist political economy, written during her years teaching at the SPD party school in Berlin. The work argues that capitalist expanded reproduction is logically impossible within a closed system of purely capitalist social relations, because the surplus value produced cannot be fully realized without access to non-capitalist markets. Capital therefore necessarily drives outward, absorbing pre-capitalist economies through colonialism, militarism, and the forcible commodification of subsistence production. This process, Luxemburg contends, is not an aberration but the essential mechanism of capitalist accumulation on a world scale.

The book's central polemic targets Marx's reproduction schemas in Volume II of *Capital*, which Luxemburg regarded as incomplete and internally contradictory. Her critique generated one of the most sustained debates in Marxist economics, drawing responses from Bukharin, Henryk Grossman, and Paul Sweezy, among others. Critics argued she confused the conditions of realization with those of production, while sympathetic readers saw her as anticipating dependency theory and world-systems analysis.

Luxemburg's insistence that capitalism contains an inherent geographical and social limit to accumulation has gained renewed attention in an era of global ecological crisis and the ongoing enclosure of commons across the Global South. The work remains foundational for Marxist theories of imperialism, uneven development, and the relationship between capitalist and non-capitalist modes of production.
