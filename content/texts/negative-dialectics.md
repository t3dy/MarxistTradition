*Negative Dialectics* is Adorno's most ambitious philosophical work, mounting a sustained critique of identity thinking -- the philosophical tendency to subsume particular objects under universal concepts. Against the Hegelian tradition that resolves contradictions in higher syntheses, Adorno argues that dialectics must remain negative, attending to what escapes conceptual identification. The primacy of the object means that thought must acknowledge what is non-identical, what resists being captured by the concept.

The work represents a fundamental rethinking of the relationship between philosophy and materialism. Adorno develops the idea that conceptual thought necessarily does violence to its objects by making them equivalent and exchangeable, a process he links to the commodity form's real abstraction. Philosophy after Auschwitz must confront its own complicity with domination, recognizing that the drive toward systematic totality mirrors the administered world it claims to critique.

Central contributions include the metacritique of epistemology, the model analyses of freedom, history, and metaphysics, and the argument that philosophy can only remain true to its object by pursuing contradiction rather than resolution. The text remains foundational for any critical theory that seeks to resist the totalizing impulse of instrumental reason.
