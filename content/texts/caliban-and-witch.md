*Caliban and the Witch* reinterprets Marx's account of primitive accumulation by centering the systematic expropriation and disciplining of women's bodies and reproductive labor as foundational to the emergence of capitalism. Federici argues that the European witch hunts of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries were not medieval remnants or expressions of superstition but a coordinated campaign by emerging state and capitalist forces to destroy women's autonomy, communal practices of social reproduction, and popular resistance to enclosure. The persecution of witches, she contends, was functionally analogous to the colonial enslavement of African and Indigenous peoples — both were processes of primitive accumulation directed at creating a docile, dispossessed labor force.

Drawing on decades of research into the transition from feudalism to capitalism, Federici demonstrates how the enclosure of the commons, the rise of the wage relation, the criminalization of reproductive knowledge, and the devaluation of women's domestic labor were interconnected moments in the constitution of the capitalist mode of production. She draws a parallel between the figure of the witch in early modern Europe and Caliban in Shakespeare's *The Tempest*, both representing forms of resistance to capitalist discipline that had to be violently subdued.

The book has been enormously influential in feminist Marxism and social reproduction theory, providing historical depth to contemporary debates about care work, bodily autonomy, and the ongoing enclosure of commons across the Global South.
