*Empire* argues that the age of imperialism organized around competing nation-states has given way to a new, decentered and deterritorialized form of global sovereignty that Hardt and Negri call Empire. Drawing on Deleuze and Guattari's theory of smooth and striated space, Foucault's concept of biopolitics, and the Italian autonomist tradition's analysis of the social factory, the authors contend that power now operates through flexible networks — supranational institutions, NGOs, transnational corporations, and media systems — rather than through the territorial logic of classical imperialism. Empire has no outside; it is a total environment of control that encompasses all social life.

Against this new form of sovereignty, Hardt and Negri identify the multitude — a heterogeneous, networked subject of labor whose cooperative productive capacities in the era of immaterial and affective work exceed and potentially undermine imperial command. They argue that the shift from industrial to cognitive and communicative labor creates forms of social cooperation that are increasingly autonomous from capital's direct supervision, generating possibilities for self-organization and democratic constitution-making from below.

The book provoked enormous debate upon its publication, with critics on the left arguing that it underestimated the persistence of nation-state imperialism, military power, and the structural divisions within the global working class. Nonetheless, *Empire* remains a touchstone for discussions of globalization, post-Fordist capitalism, and the political composition of contemporary resistance movements.
