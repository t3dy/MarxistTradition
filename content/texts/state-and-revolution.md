*The State and Revolution*, written in hiding in Finland during the summer of 1917 between the February and October revolutions, recovers Marx and Engels's theory of the state as an instrument of class domination, systematically polemicizing against the social-democratic orthodoxy that sought to use the existing state apparatus for socialist purposes. Lenin argues that the bourgeois state — its army, police, bureaucracy, and judiciary — cannot simply be taken over by the working class but must be smashed and replaced by a new form of political power: the dictatorship of the proletariat, modeled on the Paris Commune of 1871.

Drawing extensively on Marx's *The Civil War in France* and Engels's *Anti-Duhring* and *The Origin of the Family*, Lenin develops a vision of the workers' state as a transitional political form characterized by the direct armed power of the organized working class, the election and recall of all officials, and the gradual elimination of the standing army and professional bureaucracy. This state, unlike all previous states, would begin to wither away as class distinctions dissolve and the administration of things replaces the government of persons.

The text was left unfinished — Lenin was interrupted by the October Revolution itself — and its relationship to the actual practice of Bolshevik state-building has been debated ever since. Critics from both the left and the right have argued that the vanguard party form Lenin advocated elsewhere contradicts the radically democratic vision outlined in this text. Nevertheless, *The State and Revolution* remains a central reference point for debates over revolutionary strategy, state power, workers' democracy, and the transition to communism.
