*Legitimation Crisis* (1973) analyses how late capitalist societies, in which the state actively intervenes in the economy, generate new forms of crisis beyond traditional economic crises. Habermas identifies four crisis tendencies: economic crisis (market failures), rationality crisis (the state's inability to manage the economy effectively), legitimation crisis (the erosion of mass loyalty to the system), and motivation crisis (the decline of cultural traditions that sustain work discipline and civic participation).

Habermas argues that as the state assumes responsibility for economic management, economic crises are transformed into political crises of legitimation. The rationality of administrative planning cannot replace the market's coordination function, while the expansion of state intervention erodes the privatism and traditionalism that previously secured consent. The work bridges Marxist crisis theory and Weber's rationalisation thesis, anticipating later analyses of neoliberalism as a response to the legitimation problems of welfare-state capitalism.
