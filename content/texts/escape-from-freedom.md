*Escape from Freedom* (1941, published in the UK as *The Fear of Freedom*) analyses why modern individuals, having achieved unprecedented political and economic freedom, frequently seek refuge in authoritarian movements and ideologies. Fromm argues that the dissolution of medieval bonds of community and security through capitalism created a 'freedom from' that was not accompanied by a positive 'freedom to' — the capacity for creative self-realisation.

Fromm identifies three mechanisms of escape: authoritarianism (submission to or domination of others), destructiveness, and automaton conformity. His analysis of the German lower middle class's attraction to Nazism examines how economic insecurity, social isolation, and resentment produced a character structure susceptible to fascist appeals. The concept of 'social character' — the personality structure shared by members of a social group as a result of common life experiences — bridges Marxist class analysis and Freudian psychology. The work remains relevant to contemporary analyses of right-wing populism and authoritarian movements.
