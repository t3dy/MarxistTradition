*What Is To Be Done?* is Lenin's foundational text on revolutionary party organization, written during the debates that would split Russian social democracy into Bolshevik and Menshevik factions. Lenin argues that the working class, left to its own spontaneous development, can achieve only trade union consciousness — an awareness of the need to struggle against individual employers for better wages and conditions — but not socialist political consciousness, which must be brought to the working class from outside, by a party of professional revolutionaries trained in Marxist theory and organized for disciplined, clandestine political action.

The text polemicizes against "economism" — the tendency within Russian social democracy to restrict political activity to supporting workers' immediate economic demands — and against all forms of organizational amateurism, tailism, and spontaneism. Lenin argues that without a centralized, disciplined party of the vanguard, the scattered struggles of the working class cannot be unified into a revolutionary political movement capable of overthrowing the autocracy and establishing proletarian power.

*What Is To Be Done?* has been one of the most consequential and contested texts in the history of socialism. Defenders regard it as the indispensable theoretical foundation for revolutionary organization; critics, from Luxemburg's *Organizational Questions of Russian Social Democracy* (1904) to the council communists and libertarian socialists, have argued that Lenin's model substitutes the party for the class and produces bureaucratic authoritarianism rather than working-class self-emancipation. The debate it inaugurated about the relationship between spontaneity and organization, class and party, consciousness and structure, remains unresolved.
