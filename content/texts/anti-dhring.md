*Anti-Dühring* (1878) is Engels's comprehensive polemic against Eugen Dühring, a Berlin professor whose eclectic system of philosophy, political economy, and socialism threatened to win adherents within German Social Democracy. The work became the most widely read systematic exposition of Marxist theory, covering philosophy (dialectical materialism), political economy (the labour theory of value and exploitation), and scientific socialism.

Engels defended dialectics against Dühring's metaphysical method, argued for the materialist conception of history, and outlined the Marxist theory of the state and its eventual withering away. Three chapters were published separately as *Socialism: Utopian and Scientific*, which became Marxism's most popular pamphlet. The text established many formulations that became canonical in the Marxist tradition, though critics — from Lukács to value-form theorists — have questioned whether Engels's systematisation of Marx's thought introduced positivist and determinist elements foreign to Marx's own critical method.
