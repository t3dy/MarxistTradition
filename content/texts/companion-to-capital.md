*A Companion to Marx's Capital* provides a chapter-by-chapter guide to Volume I, drawing on Harvey's decades of teaching *Capital* in university seminars and free public lectures — a course he began at Johns Hopkins in the 1970s and later made available online, where it has been viewed by millions. Harvey reads Marx closely while drawing persistent connections to contemporary phenomena: urbanization, financialization, environmental degradation, and the lived experience of work under neoliberalism.

Harvey's reading emphasizes the internal logic and sequential architecture of Marx's argument, showing how the analysis moves from the abstract categories of commodity and value through money, surplus value, and the working day to the concrete dynamics of accumulation and crisis. He is particularly attentive to Marx's use of metaphor, irony, and literary allusion, making the case that *Capital* is not merely a work of economic theory but a deeply humanistic critique of a social system that reduces human life to an appendage of the valorization process.

The *Companion* has become one of the most widely used introductory guides to Marx's critique of political economy, valued for its accessibility without sacrificing theoretical seriousness. Harvey followed it with a companion to Volume II in 2013, extending his pedagogical project across Marx's analysis of circulation and reproduction. Together they represent the most sustained effort to make Marx's economic writings available to a broad public in the twenty-first century.
