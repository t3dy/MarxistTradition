*Socialism: Utopian and Scientific* (1880) is a pamphlet extracted from three chapters of Engels's *Anti-Dühring*, published separately at Marx's suggestion as an accessible introduction to scientific socialism. It became the most widely read work of Marxist theory in the nineteenth century, translated into dozens of languages and distributed in millions of copies.

The pamphlet distinguishes utopian socialism — represented by Saint-Simon, Fourier, and Owen, who designed ideal societies without understanding the material conditions for their realisation — from scientific socialism, which grounds the socialist project in the analysis of capitalism's actual contradictions. Engels traces the development of materialism and dialectics, outlines the materialist conception of history, and argues that capitalism's socialisation of production creates the objective conditions for socialist transformation. Critics have noted that Engels's positivist framing of Marxism as a 'science' analogous to natural science differs from Marx's more nuanced understanding of critique, but the pamphlet remains historically significant as the gateway text through which millions encountered Marxist ideas.
