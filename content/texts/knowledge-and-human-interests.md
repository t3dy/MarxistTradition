*Knowledge and Human Interests* (1968) is Habermas's ambitious reconstruction of the relationship between epistemology and social theory. He argues that all knowledge is guided by underlying 'knowledge-constitutive interests': the technical interest in prediction and control (natural sciences), the practical interest in mutual understanding (hermeneutic sciences), and the emancipatory interest in liberation from domination (critical social science).

Habermas traces how positivism severed the connection between knowledge and human interests, reducing all knowledge to the technical model and thereby eliminating the possibility of a genuinely critical science. He reconstructs the emancipatory tradition through Kant, Fichte, Hegel, Marx, Freud, and Peirce. The work marked a turning point in critical theory, moving away from the first-generation Frankfurt School's critique of instrumental reason toward a communicative theory of rationality that would culminate in *The Theory of Communicative Action*.
