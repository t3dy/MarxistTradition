*Late Capitalism*, Mandel's major theoretical work, analyzes the post-1945 capitalist boom and its emerging contradictions through the lens of long waves of capitalist development — a framework drawn from Kondratiev but given a distinctively Marxist theoretical grounding. Mandel argues that the postwar expansion represented a third major phase of capitalist development, following competitive capitalism and classical imperialism, characterized by the dominance of multinational corporations, accelerating technological innovation, the growing economic role of the state, and the increasing commodification of all spheres of social life.

Mandel contends that this "late capitalist" phase does not overcome the fundamental contradictions of the system but displaces and intensifies them, setting the stage for renewed crisis. He rejects both the social-democratic thesis of managed capitalism that has transcended cyclical crisis and the orthodox Marxist expectation of imminent collapse, instead theorizing a contradictory dynamic in which each expansionary wave creates the conditions for the next downturn through the overaccumulation of capital and the tendential decline in profitability.

The book gave wide currency to the term "late capitalism," which Fredric Jameson later adopted to describe the cultural logic of postmodernity. Mandel's analysis of the arms economy, the internationalization of capital, and the relationship between technological revolution and profit rate recovery remains an important reference point for periodizing capitalist development. Written with a combination of theoretical ambition and empirical detail rare in Marxist economics, *Late Capitalism* stands as one of the most comprehensive attempts to update Marx's categories for the analysis of postwar capitalism.
