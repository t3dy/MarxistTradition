The *Critique of Hegel's Philosophy of Right* (1843) is Marx's first systematic engagement with Hegel's political philosophy, written during his transition from radical liberalism to communism. The manuscript critiques Hegel's theory of the state as the rational reconciliation of civil society's contradictions, arguing instead that the state is an expression of those contradictions.

Marx's famous Introduction (published 1844) contains the declaration that religion is the 'opium of the people' and identifies the proletariat as the universal class whose emancipation requires the abolition of all classes. The critique establishes key themes of Marx's mature work: the inversion of Hegel's idealism (the state does not determine civil society but civil society determines the state), the critique of bureaucracy, and the identification of philosophy's realisation with proletarian revolution. The text marks the decisive turn from philosophical criticism to the critique of political economy that would culminate in *Capital*.
