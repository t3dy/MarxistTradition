*Eclipse of Reason* traces the historical process by which reason has been reduced from a substantive faculty capable of determining ends and values to a merely instrumental capacity for calculating efficient means. Horkheimer distinguishes between objective reason, which once aspired to comprehend the rational structure of reality and guide human action toward the good, and subjective reason, which concerns itself solely with the adequacy of means to pre-given ends without questioning those ends themselves.

The work argues that this reduction is not accidental but structurally linked to the development of capitalism, which requires a form of rationality oriented exclusively toward domination of nature and efficient production. As reason loses its capacity to critically evaluate social arrangements, it becomes complicit with the very irrationality it was meant to overcome. The triumph of instrumental reason produces a world that is technically proficient yet ethically blind.

Horkheimer's central contributions include the typology of subjective and objective reason, the analysis of how pragmatism and positivism philosophically ratify this reduction, and the argument that the revolt against reason in fascism and irrationalism is itself a product of reason's self-liquidation. The text remains a foundational critique of technocratic modernity.
