*The Revolution Betrayed* is Trotsky's most systematic analysis of the Soviet Union under Stalin, written in Norwegian exile and published in 1936 on the eve of the Great Purges. Trotsky argues that the Soviet Union represents neither socialism nor a restored capitalism but a contradictory transitional formation — a degenerated workers' state — in which the social foundations of the October Revolution (nationalized property, planned economy, monopoly of foreign trade) persist under the political domination of a parasitic bureaucratic caste that has usurped power from the working class.

Trotsky traces the material roots of bureaucratic degeneration to the isolation of the revolution in a backward country, the destruction of the most politically conscious workers in the civil war, and the exhaustion and demoralization of the Soviet proletariat. The bureaucracy, he argues, arose not from some inherent flaw in Bolshevism but from the objective conditions of scarcity, which required a social layer to police the distribution of limited resources. However, this caste has no independent historical mission and must ultimately either be overthrown by a political revolution that restores workers' democracy while preserving nationalized property, or preside over capitalist restoration.

The book's analysis of Soviet economic planning, its comparison of Soviet and capitalist growth rates, and its examination of the bureaucracy's privileges, cultural conservatism, and foreign policy remain essential for understanding the trajectory of twentieth-century socialism. Trotsky's prediction that the bureaucracy would eventually move toward capitalist restoration has been widely cited since the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991.
