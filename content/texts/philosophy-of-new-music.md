*Philosophy of New Music* (1949) presents Adorno's dialectical analysis of modern music through two contrasting studies: Schoenberg and Stravinsky. Schoenberg's atonal and twelve-tone music represents for Adorno the authentic artistic response to modernity — its dissonance and formal radicalism express the truth of a damaged social totality by refusing the false harmony of tonal convention.

Stravinsky's neoclassicism, by contrast, represents regression: his rhythmic primitivism and ironic use of past styles amount to a pseudo-archaic restoration that accommodates the listener to the administered world rather than challenging it. Adorno argues that musical material has its own historical logic — composers do not freely choose their techniques but must respond to the objective state of the material as shaped by the social totality. The work exemplifies Adorno's broader aesthetic theory: that authentic art preserves the possibility of a different world precisely by refusing to represent it positively.
