*Eros and Civilization* (1955) synthesises Marx and Freud to argue that advanced industrial civilisation has created the material conditions to overcome what Marcuse terms 'surplus repression' — repression imposed not by the necessities of civilisation as such but by the specific requirements of class domination under the 'performance principle' (the reality principle in its capitalist form).

Drawing on Freud's late metapsychology, Marcuse argues that the liberation of Eros from surplus repression would transform work into play and abolish the antagonism between pleasure and reality. He develops the figures of Orpheus and Narcissus as images of a non-repressive civilisation. The work profoundly influenced the New Left and the 1960s counterculture, providing theoretical grounding for the idea that sexual and personal liberation were inseparable from political revolution. Critics have questioned whether Marcuse's optimism about instinctual liberation underestimates the depth of capitalism's colonisation of desire.
