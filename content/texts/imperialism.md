*Imperialism, the Highest Stage of Capitalism*, written in Zurich during the spring of 1916 and published the following year, argues that imperialism is not a policy choice of particular governments but a necessary stage of capitalist development. Lenin identifies five defining features of this stage: the concentration of production into monopolies, the merger of bank and industrial capital into finance capital, the export of capital as distinct from the export of commodities, the formation of international monopolist combines that divide the world among themselves, and the territorial partition of the globe among the great powers.

Drawing extensively on Hobson's study of imperialism and Hilferding's analysis of finance capital, Lenin contends that the monopoly stage transforms free competition into its opposite while simultaneously intensifying inter-imperialist rivalry. He argues that the superprofits extracted from colonial exploitation enable the metropolitan bourgeoisie to bribe a thin layer of the working class — the labor aristocracy — thereby splitting the proletariat and undermining revolutionary consciousness in the advanced countries.

Written as a popular pamphlet under wartime censorship, the work's theoretical framework became foundational for twentieth-century anti-colonial movements, from the Communist International's thesis on the national and colonial questions to dependency theory and world-systems analysis. Its analysis of the relationship between monopoly, finance, and geopolitical conflict remains a reference point for contemporary debates about neocolonialism, unequal exchange, and the structural dynamics of the capitalist world system.
