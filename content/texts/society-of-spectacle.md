*The Society of the Spectacle*, composed of 221 numbered theses, argues that in advanced capitalism all of life presents itself as an immense accumulation of spectacles — everything that was directly lived has receded into representation. Debord's concept of the spectacle is not reducible to mass media or entertainment but describes a social relationship between people mediated by images: the commodity form at the stage of its total colonization of social life, where the image has become the final form of commodity reification.

Debord draws on Marx's analysis of commodity fetishism and Lukacs's theory of reification to argue that the spectacle represents the historical moment when the commodity has attained the total occupation of social life. The spectacle is capital accumulated to the point where it becomes image; it is the autonomous movement of the non-living, a permanent opium war waged to prevent people from recognizing the poverty of their actual existence. Debord distinguishes between the concentrated spectacle of totalitarian societies (a single image imposed by the state) and the diffuse spectacle of advanced capitalist democracies (the proliferation of consumer choice as a substitute for genuine freedom), later adding the concept of the integrated spectacle that combines features of both.

Written on the eve of May 1968, the book's influence on the uprising — and the subsequent recuperation of Situationist ideas by the advertising industry and counterculture — demonstrated both the power of Debord's analysis and the difficulty of escaping the spectacle's capacity to absorb its own critique. The work has gained renewed relevance in the era of social media, where the production and consumption of images has become the dominant form of social participation.
