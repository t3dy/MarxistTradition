*Black Skin, White Masks*, Fanon's first book, written while he was a psychiatric resident in Lyon, develops a phenomenological and psychoanalytic account of the lived experience of racial alienation under colonialism. Drawing on Hegel's dialectic of recognition, Sartre's existentialism, Lacan's mirror stage, and his own clinical practice, Fanon analyzes how colonial racism structures the psyche of the Black subject, producing a form of alienation in which the colonized person is compelled to adopt the cultural values, language, and aspirations of the white colonizer while being systematically denied recognition as a full human being.

Fanon demonstrates how the "fact of blackness" — the experience of being reduced to one's skin color by the white gaze — constitutes a unique form of objectification that cannot be fully captured by the Marxist category of class exploitation or the existentialist category of bad faith. The colonized subject is caught in a double bind: to succeed in the colonial world requires assimilation to white cultural norms (wearing "white masks"), but this assimilation can never be complete because racial difference is inscribed on the body itself. Fanon's analysis of the psychopathology of colonialism — the neuroses, inferiority complexes, and self-hatred produced by systematic racial domination — broke new ground in connecting political domination to subjective experience.

The book has been foundational for postcolonial theory, critical race studies, and the intersection of psychoanalysis and political thought, though some readers note a tension between its existential-humanist framework and the more structuralist, revolutionary politics of Fanon's later *The Wretched of the Earth*.
