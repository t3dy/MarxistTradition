The *Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844* (also known as the Paris Manuscripts) are Marx's early notebooks, unpublished in his lifetime and first appearing in 1932. They contain Marx's first sustained engagement with political economy and his most extensive treatment of alienation, analysing four dimensions: alienation from the product of labour, from the labour process, from species-being, and from other human beings.

The manuscripts develop a philosophical anthropology grounding communism in the full development of human capacities and the overcoming of alienated labour. Their publication transformed Marx scholarship, inspiring humanist interpretations (Fromm, Mészáros) that emphasised continuity between the young and mature Marx. Althusser contested this reading, positing an 'epistemological break' separating the humanist early writings from the scientific critique of political economy in *Capital*. The manuscripts remain central to debates about Marx's philosophical foundations, the concept of alienation, and whether Marxism is fundamentally a humanism or a science of social forms.
