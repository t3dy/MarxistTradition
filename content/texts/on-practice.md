*On Practice*, written alongside *On Contradiction* at Yan'an in 1937, develops a Marxist theory of knowledge that insists on the primacy of practice — productive labor, class struggle, and scientific experiment — as the foundation, criterion, and purpose of all genuine cognition. Mao argues against both rationalist dogmatism, which derives knowledge from abstract principles without testing them in practice, and empiricist tailism, which remains at the level of perceptual experience without rising to rational, theoretical understanding.

Mao outlines a dialectical epistemology in which knowledge begins with perceptual experience of the objective world, rises through conceptual thought to rational knowledge (theory), and must then return to practice to be verified and refined. The movement from practice to theory and back to practice constitutes a spiral of deepening understanding, in which each cycle of practice-theory-practice yields more adequate comprehension of objective reality. Mao explicitly polemicizes against the tendency within the Chinese Communist Party to treat Marxist texts as dogma to be memorized rather than as guides to action to be tested and developed through revolutionary practice.

The text's significance lies less in its philosophical originality — its debt to Lenin's *Materialism and Empirio-Criticism* and to dialectical materialism as a whole is clear — than in its political function: providing a theoretical justification for adapting Marxist strategy to Chinese conditions and for the "mass line" method of leadership, in which the party systematizes the scattered ideas of the masses and returns them as policy to be tested in practice.
