*The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte* is Marx's analysis of Louis-Napoleon Bonaparte's coup d'etat of December 2, 1851, written in the immediate aftermath of events for Joseph Weydemeyer's German-language journal in New York. The text is Marx's most sustained exercise in concrete political analysis, demonstrating how class struggles produce political outcomes that no single class directly intended. Marx traces the trajectory of the French revolution of 1848 through its successive phases — the overthrow of the July Monarchy, the June Days insurrection, the conservative republic, and the final Bonapartist resolution — showing how each class fraction advanced and retreated according to its material interests and organizational capacity.

The text introduces the concept of Bonapartism: a political form in which the executive state power appears to rise above contending classes and achieve autonomy, while in practice serving the long-term interests of capital accumulation by guaranteeing bourgeois property relations. Marx's analysis of the French peasantry as a class that cannot represent itself politically, and of the lumpenproletariat as the social base of Bonapartist adventurism, remain foundational for political sociology.

The *Eighteenth Brumaire* opens with one of Marx's most quoted formulations — that historical events occur twice, first as tragedy, then as farce — and contains his sharpest reflections on how the weight of tradition constrains revolutionary action. The work is essential reading for understanding the relative autonomy of the political, the mechanics of counter-revolution, and Marxist method applied to conjunctural analysis.
