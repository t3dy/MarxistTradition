*Finance Capital* is Hilferding's analysis of the transformation of competitive capitalism into monopoly capitalism through the increasing dominance of banking over industry. Hilferding argues that the concentration of production into cartels and trusts, combined with the centralization of the banking system, produces a new form of capital — finance capital — in which the bank assumes a commanding role in directing industrial investment, organizing monopolistic combinations, and driving the territorial expansion of capital into colonial markets and spheres of influence.

The book provides a systematic Marxist analysis of the stock exchange, the joint-stock company, and the credit system as mechanisms through which finance capital coordinates economic activity on a scale that transcends the capacity of individual industrial capitalists. Hilferding shows how the export of capital, rather than the export of commodities, becomes the driving force of the world economy under conditions of monopoly, generating inter-imperialist rivalry over colonial territories and markets. He also develops the concept of the "general cartel" — the theoretical limit of monopolization in which a single trust controls all production — and argues that this tendency toward organized capitalism creates the objective preconditions for socialist planning.

Lenin drew extensively on *Finance Capital* for his own *Imperialism* (1917), adopting Hilferding's analysis of monopoly and finance capital while criticizing his reformist political conclusions. The work remains foundational for Marxist analyses of financialization, corporate concentration, and the relationship between banking, industrial capital, and state power.
