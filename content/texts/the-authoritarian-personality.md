*The Authoritarian Personality* is a landmark interdisciplinary study combining psychoanalytic theory with empirical social research to investigate the psychological foundations of fascist susceptibility. Adorno and his collaborators developed the F-scale (fascism scale) to measure authoritarian tendencies, identifying a character structure marked by rigid conventionalism, authoritarian submission and aggression, anti-intraception, superstition, preoccupation with power, destructiveness, projectivity, and exaggerated concern with sexuality.

The study's theoretical significance lies in its synthesis of Marxian social analysis with Freudian psychology. Rather than treating fascism as mere political ideology, it demonstrates how capitalist social relations produce specific psychic formations that predispose individuals toward authoritarian movements. The authoritarian character emerges from the patriarchal family's internalization of social domination, creating subjects who displace economic anxieties onto racialized and marginalized others.

Key contributions include the empirical methodology for studying ideology's psychological dimensions, the concept of the authoritarian character type, and the demonstration that prejudice functions as a coherent syndrome rather than a collection of isolated attitudes. The work pioneered the empirical study of ideology and remains influential in political psychology.
