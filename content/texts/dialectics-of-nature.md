*Dialectics of Nature* is an unfinished collection of notes and fragments by Engels, written between 1873 and 1886 and first published in the Soviet Union in 1925. The work attempts to demonstrate that dialectical laws operate in nature as well as in human history, applying dialectical categories to developments in physics, chemistry, and biology.

Engels formulated three 'laws of dialectics' — the transformation of quantity into quality, the interpenetration of opposites, and the negation of the negation — arguing that these operate as objective laws of nature independent of human consciousness. The work has been controversial: supporters see it as extending materialism into natural science, while critics from Lukács onward have argued that Engels's dialectics of nature represents a positivist distortion of Marx's critical method, which applied dialectics to historically specific social forms rather than to nature itself. The debate over Engels's philosophical legacy continues to divide the Marxist tradition.
