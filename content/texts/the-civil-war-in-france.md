*The Civil War in France* (1871) is Marx's address to the General Council of the International Working Men's Association, written during and immediately after the Paris Commune. It constitutes Marx's most detailed analysis of revolutionary politics in practice and his most important theoretical contribution to the Marxist theory of the state.

Marx analysed the Commune as the political form finally discovered under which the economic emancipation of labour could be accomplished. He identified its key features: the replacement of the standing army by the armed people, elected and recallable officials paid workers' wages, the fusion of executive and legislative functions, and the destruction of the centralised bureaucratic state machinery. The text established the principle — later developed by Lenin in *The State and Revolution* — that the working class cannot simply take over the existing state but must smash it and replace it with new organs of proletarian power. Marx's account of the Commune's suppression during the Semaine Sanglante remains one of the most powerful denunciations of bourgeois class violence in the Marxist canon.
