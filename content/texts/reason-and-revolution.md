*Reason and Revolution: Hegel and the Rise of Social Theory* (1941) is Marcuse's interpretation of Hegel as a fundamentally critical social theorist whose philosophy contains the intellectual foundations for Marxism. Written partly to counter the appropriation of Hegel by fascist and authoritarian thinkers, Marcuse argued that Hegel's dialectic is inherently revolutionary: its central principle of negativity — that every existing state of affairs contains the seeds of its own transcendence — makes it incompatible with any philosophy that accepts the given as final.

Marcuse traces the development from Hegel's philosophy through Marx's critique of political economy, showing how Marx realised Hegel's dialectic in material social analysis. The second part examines the rise of positivist sociology (Comte, Weber) as the abandonment of dialectical reason in favour of a 'positive' social science that accepts existing reality as its framework. The work established the Hegelian-Marxist tradition for an English-speaking audience and remains a foundational text of Western Marxism.
