Pashukanis's *General Theory of Law and Marxism* is the most original Marxist contribution to legal theory, arguing that the juridical form — the legal subject endowed with rights and obligations — is not a neutral instrument of social regulation but a historically specific social form that corresponds to the commodity form in the same way that the political state corresponds to the capitalist mode of production. Just as commodities confront each other as equivalents in the market, legal subjects confront each other as formally equal bearers of rights in the juridical sphere: the legal relation is the complement and mirror image of the commodity relation.

Pashukanis argues that bourgeois law cannot be understood by examining its content (the particular rules it enforces) but only through analysis of its form — the very category of the legal subject, with its abstractions of will, personality, and contract, is the juridical expression of the abstractions of commodity exchange. This "commodity-form theory of law" implies that law as such is a bourgeois category that will wither away under communism, along with the state, private property, and commodity production. Pashukanis explicitly rejected the attempt to construct a "proletarian law," arguing that this was as incoherent as the idea of a "proletarian political economy."

Written during the relatively open theoretical debates of the early Soviet period, the work was officially condemned in the 1930s, and Pashukanis was arrested and executed during the Great Purges in 1937. His legal theory has been revived since the 1970s and continues to influence critical legal studies and Marxist state theory.
