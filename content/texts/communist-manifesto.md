*The Communist Manifesto*, commissioned by the Communist League and published on the eve of the 1848 revolutions, outlines the materialist conception of history as a succession of class struggles and analyzes the historically revolutionary role of the bourgeoisie in dissolving feudal social relations while producing its own gravedigger in the modern proletariat. In prose of extraordinary rhetorical power, Marx and Engels argue that capitalism has created a world market, revolutionized production, and subordinated all social relations to the cash nexus, but in doing so has concentrated and organized a class whose exploitation compels it toward collective resistance.

The text combines historical analysis with a programmatic call for the abolition of bourgeois private property — not personal possessions but the ownership of the means of production that enables the appropriation of others' labor. Marx and Engels distinguish their position from utopian socialism by insisting that communism is not an ideal to be implemented but the real movement arising from existing class contradictions. The Manifesto's ten-point program, including progressive taxation, centralization of credit, and free public education, was intended as transitional demands for the immediate aftermath of revolution.

Written in six weeks by Marx at the age of twenty-nine, the *Manifesto* is the most widely translated and circulated text in the Marxist tradition. Its analysis of globalization, the revolutionary dynamism of capital, and the political centrality of class struggle has proved remarkably durable across nearly two centuries of capitalist development.
