*The Jargon of Authenticity* (Jargon der Eigentlichkeit, 1964) is Adorno's sustained critique of German existentialism, particularly Heidegger's philosophy and its broader cultural influence. Adorno argues that existentialism's vocabulary of authenticity, Being, encounter, and decision functions as an ideology that lends spurious depth and weight to everyday bourgeois consciousness.

The 'jargon' operates by treating historically specific social relations as ontological structures, transforming alienation from a critique of capitalist society into an inescapable feature of human existence. Adorno contends that Heidegger's philosophy of authenticity is itself inauthentic: by abstracting from the social conditions that produce alienation, it offers false consolation and mystifies domination. The work extends the critique of Heidegger that Adorno had pursued since the 1930s and complements his broader argument in *Negative Dialectics* against identity thinking and premature philosophical reconciliation.
