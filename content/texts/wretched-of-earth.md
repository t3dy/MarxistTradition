*The Wretched of the Earth*, written in the final year of Fanon's life as he was dying of leukemia and dictated in part from his hospital bed, is one of the most influential theoretical works of the twentieth century. Published in 1961 with a preface by Jean-Paul Sartre, the book provides a systematic analysis of colonialism and decolonization that combines Marxist political economy, existential phenomenology, and clinical psychiatry.

Fanon argues that colonial rule is sustained by comprehensive violence — economic, political, psychological, and spatial — and that it can only be overthrown through the organized counter-violence of the colonized, a process that is simultaneously political liberation and psychological regeneration. The book challenges orthodox Marxist categories by centering the peasantry and lumpenproletariat rather than the industrial working class as the revolutionary agents in the colonial context, arguing that the colonized proletariat and national bourgeoisie are both compromised by their structural positions within the colonial economy.

The chapters on national consciousness and the pitfalls of the national bourgeoisie provide a prescient analysis of how independence movements can be captured by a parasitic comprador class that reproduces colonial economic relations under a new flag. The final section, drawing on Fanon's psychiatric practice in Algeria during the war of independence, documents the psychopathology of colonial domination — the mental disorders produced in both colonizer and colonized by the systematic dehumanization of colonial society. *The Wretched of the Earth* remains essential reading for anti-colonial thought, revolutionary theory, and the analysis of racial capitalism.
