Trotsky's *History of the Russian Revolution*, written in exile on Prinkipo island between 1930 and 1932, is at once a detailed historical narrative of the February and October revolutions of 1917 and a sustained theoretical argument about the dynamics of revolutionary transformation. The work opens with an exposition of the law of combined and uneven development as the key to understanding Russian history: the peculiar amalgam of modern industry, semi-feudal agriculture, autocratic state forms, and a small but highly concentrated proletariat that made Russia the weakest link in the imperialist chain.

Trotsky traces the development of the revolution through the spontaneous uprising of February, the period of dual power between the Provisional Government and the soviets, the progressive radicalization of the masses, and the October insurrection, analyzing at each stage the relationship between the objective situation and the subjective factor — the consciousness, organization, and leadership of the revolutionary forces. His account of the July Days, the Kornilov affair, and the debates within the Bolshevik Party on the eve of October provides an unmatched insider's perspective on the mechanics of revolutionary decision-making.

The *History* is also a work of remarkable literary power, combining panoramic social analysis with vivid characterization of individual actors — from the vacillating Kerensky to the methodical Lenin. Trotsky's insistence on the indispensable role of revolutionary leadership within an objectively revolutionary situation remains one of the most debated propositions in Marxist political theory. The work is widely regarded as one of the great works of twentieth-century historical writing.
