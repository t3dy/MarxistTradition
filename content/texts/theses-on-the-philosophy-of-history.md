The 'Theses on the Philosophy of History' (Über den Begriff der Geschichte, 1940) is Benjamin's final work, written shortly before his death while fleeing the Nazis. In eighteen brief theses, Benjamin develops a revolutionary conception of history against both historicism (which empathises with the victors) and the social-democratic faith in automatic progress.

The famous image of the angel of history, blown backward into the future by the storm of progress while catastrophe piles wreckage at his feet, crystallises Benjamin's critique of progressive historiography. Against 'homogeneous, empty time,' Benjamin proposes Jetztzeit — the 'now-time' of revolutionary action that blasts open the continuum of history. The theses combine historical materialism with Jewish messianism in ways that have generated intense interpretive debate. They remain among the most influential and contested texts in critical theory, challenging teleological conceptions of history and insisting that revolutionary redemption requires remembering the defeated and oppressed of the past.
