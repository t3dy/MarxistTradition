*On Contradiction*, written at Yan'an during the anti-Japanese war, is Mao's most important philosophical text and his most sustained engagement with dialectical materialism. Mao develops a theory of contradiction that distinguishes between the universality and particularity of contradiction, principal and secondary contradictions, and the principal and secondary aspects of a contradiction, arguing that the correct identification of the principal contradiction at each stage of revolutionary development is the fundamental task of Marxist analysis and political strategy.

Mao's central innovation is the concept of the principal contradiction — the dominant contradiction whose resolution determines the character of a given historical period — which shifts as conditions change. During the anti-Japanese war, the principal contradiction was between the Chinese nation and Japanese imperialism, requiring a united front that temporarily subordinated class struggle; after liberation, the principal contradiction would shift to that between the proletariat and the bourgeoisie. This framework provided a theoretical basis for the flexible alliance politics that characterized the Chinese revolution and distinguished Mao's strategy from both the Comintern's mechanical class analysis and Trotsky's theory of permanent revolution.

The text also develops the thesis that contradiction is universal and absolute — present in all processes at all times — while the particular forms it takes are relative and historically specific. Mao's dialectics, with their emphasis on the transformation of opposites, the interpenetration of universality and particularity, and the role of practice in resolving theoretical questions, have influenced revolutionary movements across the Third World and continue to be debated within Marxist philosophy.
