*Mute Compulsion* develops a systematic theory of the specifically economic power of capital — the impersonal, structural form of domination that operates not through direct violence or ideological persuasion but through the silent compulsion of market dependence. Mau argues that most critical theory has focused on either the ideological or repressive dimensions of capitalist power while neglecting the distinctively economic mechanisms through which capital subordinates human life: the separation of producers from the means of production, the commodification of labor-power, and the disciplinary force of competition.

Drawing on Marx's mature critique of political economy, particularly the analysis of real subsumption and the concept of capital as an automatic subject, Mau traces how capital reshapes the material conditions of social reproduction — transforming bodies, desires, temporalities, and the relationship between humans and nature — in ways that produce compliance without requiring either consent or coercion in the traditional sense. The book distinguishes this economic power from sovereignty, disciplinary power, and biopower, arguing that it constitutes a form of domination specific to capitalism and irreducible to other modalities.

Published to wide acclaim and rapidly translated into multiple languages, *Mute Compulsion* has been recognized as one of the most significant contributions to Marxist social theory in recent years. Its integration of value-form analysis, social reproduction theory, and the critique of political economy provides a framework for understanding why capitalist domination persists even in the absence of overt repression or effective ideology.
