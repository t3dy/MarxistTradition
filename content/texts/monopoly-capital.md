*Monopoly Capital: An Essay on the American Economic and Social Order* (1966) by Paul Baran and Paul Sweezy argues that monopoly capitalism has fundamentally altered the laws of motion Marx identified for competitive capitalism. Under monopoly conditions, the central tendency is not the falling rate of profit but the rising surplus — monopoly firms generate more surplus than can be profitably reinvested.

The authors analyse how this surplus is absorbed through civilian and military government spending, the sales effort (advertising and marketing), and imperial expansion. Without these absorption mechanisms, monopoly capitalism tends toward stagnation rather than crisis. The work was enormously influential, shaping the Monthly Review school's analysis of US capitalism and Third World underdevelopment. Critics have challenged the abandonment of the labour theory of value, the concept of surplus as a replacement for surplus value, and the stagnation thesis itself, which seemed contradicted by the post-war boom's continuation until the 1970s.
