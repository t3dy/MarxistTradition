*Seven Interpretive Essays on Peruvian Reality* is Mariategui's masterwork, applying historical materialism to the analysis of Peruvian economy, politics, education, religion, literature, and the agrarian question with an originality that has made it the founding text of Latin American Marxism. The essays argue that the fundamental problem of Peru is the land question and the exploitation of the indigenous majority, rooted in a semi-feudal system of latifundia that survived independence from Spain and was reinforced rather than dissolved by the country's dependent integration into the world market.

Mariategui rejects the orthodox Marxist assumption that Peru must pass through a stage of bourgeois-democratic development before socialist revolution becomes possible, arguing that the Peruvian bourgeoisie is too dependent on imperialism and too intertwined with the landed oligarchy to carry through its own historical program. Instead, he proposes a creative synthesis of Marxism and indigenism, arguing that the communal traditions of the Andean *ayllu* — the indigenous system of collective land tenure and reciprocal labor — represent a living foundation for socialist construction that need not recapitulate the trajectory of European capitalist development.

The essays on education and literature demonstrate Mariategui's commitment to cultural analysis as an integral dimension of social transformation, anticipating Gramsci's analysis of hegemony and the role of intellectuals. Written in a lucid, combative prose that combines theoretical rigor with journalistic accessibility, the *Seven Essays* remain essential for understanding the relationship between Marxism, indigenous movements, and anti-imperialism in Latin America.
