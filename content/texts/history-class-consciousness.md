*History and Class Consciousness*, a collection of essays written between 1919 and 1922, is widely regarded as a founding text of Western Marxism. Lukacs reintroduced Hegelian dialectics into Marxist theory at a moment when the dominant orthodoxy of the Second International had reduced Marx's method to a positivist science of economic laws. The book's central essay, "Reification and the Consciousness of the Proletariat," extends Marx's analysis of commodity fetishism to show how the commodity form penetrates all aspects of social life under capitalism, fragmenting consciousness, rationalizing administration, and rendering social relations opaque to those who inhabit them.

Lukacs draws on Weber's theory of rationalization and Simmel's philosophy of culture to argue that bourgeois thought is structurally incapable of grasping society as a totality because it is itself a product of reified social relations. Only the proletariat, as both subject and object of the historical process, possesses the potential standpoint from which the social totality can be comprehended and transformed. This argument yokes together epistemology and revolutionary politics in a way that profoundly influenced the Frankfurt School, Sartre, and the New Left.

Lukacs later repudiated the book under political pressure, criticizing its idealist tendencies and its failure to ground dialectics in the labor process. Nevertheless, *History and Class Consciousness* remains a pivotal text for understanding the philosophical ambitions of Marxist theory and the relationship between reification, ideology, and the possibility of revolutionary consciousness.
