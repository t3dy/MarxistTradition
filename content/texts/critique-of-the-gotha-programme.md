The *Critique of the Gotha Programme* (1875) is Marx's critical commentary on the draft programme for the unification of German Social Democracy, written as marginal notes circulated among party leaders but not published until 1891. It contains Marx's most explicit statements on the nature of communist society and the transition from capitalism.

Marx distinguished between a lower phase of communism, in which distribution is governed by the principle 'to each according to their contribution,' and a higher phase based on 'from each according to their ability, to each according to their needs.' He criticised the programme's Lassallean formulations on the 'iron law of wages,' the 'undiminished proceeds of labour,' and the 'free state,' insisting that the state in the transition period must be the revolutionary dictatorship of the proletariat. The text remains foundational for debates about socialist distribution, the role of the state, and the distinction between socialism and communism.
