*The Making of the English Working Class* is Thompson's monumental history of the formation of working-class consciousness and organization in England between the 1790s and the 1830s, from the impact of the French Revolution through the Luddite risings, the radical reform movement, and the emergence of Chartism. The book's central argument is that the working class was not simply produced as an automatic effect of industrialization but actively made itself through the process of political struggle, cultural creation, and the shared experience of exploitation.

Thompson insists that class is a historical relationship, not a structural category: it happens when people, as a result of common experiences inherited and shared, articulate the identity of their interests as between themselves and against other groups whose interests are different from and usually opposed to theirs. This formulation places human agency, cultural tradition, and lived experience at the center of class analysis, challenging both the economic determinism of orthodox Marxism and the static, ahistorical treatment of class in conventional sociology.

The book recovers the histories of artisans, handloom weavers, radical Dissenters, Jacobin societies, and early trade unionists — people whom, in Thompson's famous phrase, had been "condescended to by posterity." His method of social history from below, combining economic analysis with the study of popular culture, religious dissent, political rhetoric, and everyday life, transformed the discipline of social history worldwide. The *Making* remains one of the most widely read and cited works of Marxist historiography, and its insistence on the irreducibility of class experience to economic structure continues to provoke debate.
