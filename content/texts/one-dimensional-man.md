*One-Dimensional Man* argues that advanced industrial society has developed mechanisms of social control so effective that they absorb and neutralize all opposition, producing a universe of one-dimensional thought and behavior from which critical transcendence has been virtually eliminated. Marcuse contends that technological rationality and consumer abundance generate false needs — needs that bind individuals to the existing system by providing satisfactions that preempt the desire for fundamental change. The result is a society of "comfortable unfreedom" in which the working class, integrated into the consumer economy, has lost its revolutionary potential.

The book extends the Frankfurt School's critique of instrumental reason into an analysis of how advanced capitalism achieves domination not through deprivation but through affluence, not through censorship but through the administered satisfaction of desire. Marcuse argues that the categories of classical Marxism — the contradiction between productive forces and relations of production, the immiseration of the proletariat — have been partially superseded by a system capable of containing its own contradictions through technological management and ideological absorption.

Published at the height of postwar prosperity, *One-Dimensional Man* became an unexpected bestseller and a theoretical reference point for the New Left, the student movements of 1968, and the counterculture. Its analysis of how consumer capitalism neutralizes dissent — turning rebellion itself into a marketable commodity — anticipates contemporary debates about corporate co-optation, performative resistance, and the capacity of capitalism to absorb and profit from its own critique.
