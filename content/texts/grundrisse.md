The *Grundrisse* — Marx's preparatory notebooks for *Capital*, written in a burst of intensive work during the winter of 1857-58 and first published in Moscow in 1939 — contains his most extensive reflections on dialectical method, the architecture of his critique of political economy, and speculative passages on automation and post-capitalist society that have no parallel in the published volumes. The famous "Fragment on Machines" envisions a stage of production in which scientific knowledge rather than direct labor becomes the main productive force, a passage that has been central to debates about cognitive capitalism, immaterial labor, and the contemporary relevance of the labor theory of value.

The notebooks range across the theory of money, the general concept of capital, pre-capitalist economic formations, and the relationship between production, distribution, exchange, and consumption. The "Introduction" of 1857, often read as a stand-alone methodological text, develops Marx's account of the movement from abstract to concrete as the scientifically correct method of political economy, distinguishing his approach from both empiricism and speculative philosophy. Marx argues that categories like labor, value, and money are not transhistorical abstractions but determinate forms of social relations specific to capitalism.

The *Grundrisse*'s rediscovery reshaped debates about Marx's intellectual development, the relationship between Hegelian categories and economic analysis, and the scope of the unfinished project of critique. It revealed a Marx more philosophically ambitious and methodologically self-conscious than the author of *Capital* alone would suggest, and provided ammunition for both humanist and structuralist readings of his work.
