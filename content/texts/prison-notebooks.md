The *Prison Notebooks* comprise thirty-three notebooks of fragmentary reflections on politics, history, philosophy, and culture, written by Gramsci during his imprisonment under Italian fascism from 1929 until his health collapsed in 1935. Published posthumously in Italy between 1948 and 1951, and in English from the 1970s onward, they constitute one of the most original and influential bodies of Marxist thought produced in the twentieth century.

Gramsci's central theoretical contribution is the concept of hegemony, which analyzes how ruling classes maintain power not solely through state coercion but through the organization of consent within civil society — the network of institutions including schools, churches, media, and cultural organizations through which particular class interests are universalized and naturalized as common sense. This analysis led Gramsci to distinguish between the war of maneuver (direct revolutionary assault on the state) and the war of position (the prolonged struggle for cultural and intellectual leadership within civil society), arguing that in advanced capitalist societies with developed civil societies, the latter strategy was essential.

The *Notebooks* also develop influential concepts including the organic intellectual (intellectuals organically connected to a rising class who articulate its worldview), the historical bloc (the alliance of social forces united by a shared hegemonic project), and the analysis of Fordism as a mode of social regulation extending from the factory into everyday life. Gramsci's work has exerted enormous influence across political theory, cultural studies, postcolonial thought, and education, making him one of the most widely read Marxist thinkers outside of Marx himself.
