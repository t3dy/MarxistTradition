*Reform or Revolution* is Luxemburg's polemic against Eduard Bernstein's revisionism, written in 1899-1900 as a defense of revolutionary Marxism against the attempt to transform social democracy into a purely reformist movement. Bernstein had argued that Marx's predictions of capitalist crisis and proletarian revolution had been falsified by capitalism's demonstrated capacity for self-correction through credit, cartels, and state intervention, and that socialism could be achieved gradually through parliamentary reform and trade union activity. Luxemburg's response constitutes one of the sharpest theoretical engagements in the history of the socialist movement.

Luxemburg argues that Bernstein confuses the forms through which capitalism temporarily displaces its contradictions — credit, monopoly, state regulation — with their resolution. Credit does not eliminate crisis but amplifies it; cartels do not overcome anarchy of production but intensify it at a higher level; the state does not stand above class interests but serves them. More fundamentally, she argues that reform and revolution are not alternative paths to the same goal but qualitatively different political logics: reforms that do not challenge the foundations of capitalist property relations are perpetually reversible, while revolution constitutes a rupture with the existing order that alone can establish new social foundations.

The text's insistence that the question is not reform *or* revolution but reform *and* revolution — that revolutionaries fight for every immediate improvement while refusing to accept the capitalist framework as permanent — continues to define the strategic horizon of revolutionary socialism against both reformist gradualism and ultraleft abstentionism.
