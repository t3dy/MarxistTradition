*The Black Jacobins* is C.L.R. James's history of the Haitian Revolution of 1791-1804, the only successful slave revolution in modern history. Written in London and Paris during the 1930s as anti-colonial movements gathered force across Africa and the Caribbean, the book places the uprising led by Toussaint L'Ouverture at the center of the Age of Revolution, arguing that the enslaved population of Saint-Domingue was not a passive mass but a conscious revolutionary class that drew on and radicalized the universalist ideals of the French Revolution.

James employs a Marxist framework while insisting on the autonomous agency of Black revolutionary subjects, demonstrating how the plantation system created the conditions for its own overthrow by concentrating a disciplined, exploited labor force with nothing to lose. His narrative traces the interplay between events in the Caribbean and metropolitan politics in Paris, showing how colonial revolution and European revolution were dialectically intertwined. The book also provides a devastating portrait of how revolutionary leaders can be consumed by the very structures they seek to transform, as Toussaint's diplomatic maneuvering increasingly estranged him from the masses whose struggle he led.

Revised in 1963 with a new appendix reflecting on African independence movements, *The Black Jacobins* remains essential reading for understanding the entanglement of race, class, and colonialism in the making of the modern world, and for any serious consideration of revolutionary self-emancipation.
