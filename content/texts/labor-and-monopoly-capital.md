*Labor and Monopoly Capital: The Degradation of Work in the Twentieth Century* (1974) revived Marxist analysis of the labour process, a field largely neglected since Marx's own treatment in *Capital*. Braverman argued that the capitalist drive to control the labour process leads systematically to the deskilling of workers through Taylorist scientific management, which separates conception from execution and transfers knowledge from workers to management.

Braverman traced deskilling across manufacturing, clerical work, and the service sector, demonstrating that monopoly capitalism's rationalisation of production progressively degrades the content of work. The book launched the 'labour process debate' of the 1970s-80s, with critics (Burawoy, Edwards, Friedman) arguing that Braverman neglected worker resistance, the role of consent in production, and alternative management strategies beyond direct control. Despite these criticisms, the work remains foundational for understanding how capital transforms work, and its analysis anticipates contemporary concerns about algorithmic management, surveillance, and automation.
