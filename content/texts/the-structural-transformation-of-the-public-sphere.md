*The Structural Transformation of the Public Sphere* (Strukturwandel der Öffentlichkeit, 1962) is Habermas's Habilitation thesis and one of the most influential works of twentieth-century social theory. It traces the historical emergence of the bourgeois public sphere in eighteenth-century Europe — a realm of private citizens assembling to engage in rational-critical debate on matters of public concern, mediated through coffeehouses, salons, and the periodical press.

Habermas analyses the subsequent 'refeudalization' of the public sphere under late capitalism, as mass media, public relations, and consumer culture transform citizens from critical participants into passive consumers of managed opinions. The work's normative core — the idea that democracy requires institutional spaces for undistorted communication — grounds Habermas's later communicative theory. Critics have challenged the idealisation of the bourgeois public sphere, noting its systematic exclusion of women, workers, and colonised peoples, while acknowledging the concept's continuing value for analysing democratic participation and media politics.
