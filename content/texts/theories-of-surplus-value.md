*Theories of Surplus Value* (Theorien über den Mehrwert, written 1862-63, published posthumously 1905-10) is Marx's critical history of political economy, examining the development of the concept of surplus value from the Physiocrats through Adam Smith, Ricardo, and their successors. Often referred to as the 'fourth volume' of *Capital*, the manuscript was part of Marx's plan for a comprehensive critique of political economy.

Marx traces how classical economists approached the problem of surplus without fully comprehending it: the Physiocrats located surplus in agriculture; Smith oscillated between labour-embodied and labour-commanded theories of value; Ricardo clarified the relationship between wages and profits but could not explain the origin of surplus value. Marx also critiques vulgar economists (Say, Bastiat, Senior) who abandoned scientific analysis for apologetics. The work is essential for understanding Marx's method of immanent critique — he develops his own categories through critical engagement with his predecessors, demonstrating how their insights and errors alike were conditioned by the class standpoint of the bourgeoisie.
