Rubin's *Essays on Marx's Theory of Value*, written in the Soviet Union during the relatively open theoretical debates of the 1920s, interprets Marx's value theory as fundamentally a theory of social relations between people mediated by things, foregrounding the qualitative analysis of the value-form over the quantitative determination of labor magnitudes. Rubin insists that abstract labor is not a physiological expenditure of human energy but a historically specific social category that exists only in a society where private labors are socially validated through market exchange. Value, on this reading, is not an intrinsic property of commodities but a social form assumed by labor under determinate historical conditions.

The book systematically distinguishes Marx's approach from both classical political economy, which naturalized economic categories, and the marginal utility school, which abandoned the labor theory of value altogether. Rubin argues that commodity fetishism is not a psychological illusion but an objective feature of a mode of production in which the social character of labor can appear only in the form of a property of things.

Rubin was arrested in 1930 and died in a Soviet labor camp, and his work was suppressed for decades. Its rediscovery in the 1970s profoundly influenced the value-form tradition, the *neue Marx-Lekture* in Germany, and the broader revival of interest in Marx's critique of political economy as a social theory rather than a positive economics.
